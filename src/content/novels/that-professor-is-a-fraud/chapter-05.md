The morning of his first lecture, Lucian sat on the edge of his bed and did something reckless.

He pushed [Parallel Thoughts] as far as it would go.

The original spell allowed a single secondary thread — one partition of the soul operating alongside the main consciousness. He'd been running it that way for days now, and the benefits were already extraordinary. But the spell's theoretical framework, as described in the ancient text, didn't limit it to one.

The limitation was supposed to be soul integrity. Each additional thread placed more strain on the soul's structure, requiring greater density and finer control. A Sixth Circle mage could manage two threads. A Seventh Circle might sustain three. Attempting more than your soul could handle would cause fragmentation — uncontrolled splitting that could permanently damage cognition.

But Lucian's soul was already fragmented. Deliberately, precisely, across tens of thousands of atom-bound particles, each one maintaining coherent resonance with the whole.

I'm not dividing a processor into threads. I'm networking multiple processors together. The architecture is completely different.

He closed his eyes and started adding threads. One by one.

Two. Three. Five. Eight. Twelve.

Twenty.

There was no strain on the soul. None. Each thread simply recruited a cluster of atom-bound fragments as its processing substrate, leaving the main soul untouched. It was like adding servers to a cloud network — the central node didn't even notice.

The bottleneck was his brain.

At twenty simultaneous threads, his biological mind — the wet, organic, very-much-human hardware that sat between his soul and the outside world — began to struggle. Not with the soul-level processing, but with interpreting the results. Twenty streams of analysis, twenty independent conclusions, all arriving simultaneously and demanding conscious attention. It was like trying to listen to twenty conversations at once. His soul could handle it. His brain couldn't keep up.

He scaled back to twelve. Then ten. Then found the sweet spot.

Eight. Eight permanent threads, comfortably sustainable. I can push to twelve or fifteen for short bursts — maybe thirty seconds — when I need the extra processing power. Beyond that, the brain becomes the limiting factor.

Eight simultaneous streams of consciousness. Each one capable of independent analysis. Each one sharing a unified memory space with the others.

To put that in perspective: a Seventh Circle mage — the kind of being that empires were built around — could manage three threads with intense concentration.

Lucian was running eight while sitting on his bed in his pyjamas.

It's not even the soul that limits me. It's the fact that I'm still thinking through a human brain. If I ever found a way to bypass that...

He filed the thought away. Dangerous territory. Interesting, but dangerous.

The practical implications were immediate. With eight threads, he could: teach a lecture, monitor six students' comprehension through micro-expressions and mana fluctuations, cross-reference three textbooks in memory, formulate his next two sentences, and still have a thread left over for background analysis — all simultaneously. Not sequentially. Not switching between tasks. All at once.

I'm not a professor with a secret. I'm a supercomputer wearing glasses and a faculty coat.

He put on said glasses, straightened said coat, and left his quarters.

Lecture Hall 7 was in the basement.

Of course it was.

It was the smallest teaching space in the academy — a cramped, windowless room with stone walls, a modest lectern, and wooden benches arranged in three short rows. The mana-crystal lamps on the walls gave off a tepid, yellowish light that made everything look vaguely institutional. The kind of room where ambition went to quietly suffocate.

They really rolled out the red carpet for me.

Lucian arrived fifteen minutes early, set his notes on the lectern — a prop, mostly, since he'd memorised everything — and waited.

The first student arrived ten minutes before the hour.

She walked in with the precise, controlled movements of someone who had been raised to never waste a gesture. Dark hair cut to the shoulder, posture immaculate, features that suggested old money and older expectations. Her mana signature was polished — First Circle, but the most refined First Circle Lucian had encountered. The product of expensive private tutoring long before she'd set foot in an academy. She scanned the room, registered its inadequacy, registered Lucian's inadequacy, and sat in the second row without a word.

Noble. Good training. Doesn't want to be here but isn't going to let that show. Interesting.

The second student arrived three minutes later — a thin young man with ink-stained fingers and a satchel so overstuffed it looked structurally unsound. He moved like someone who was permanently apologising for taking up space. His mana signature was faint and slightly irregular — First Circle, barely. He sat in the front row, pulled out a notebook, and began writing before anything had happened.

Bookish. Keen. Probably been looking forward to this course specifically. The kind of student who reads ahead and worries he hasn't read ahead enough.

Two more arrived together — a boy and a girl who were clearly friends, chatting in low voices. Both First Circle with decent cultivation — the product of competent, if unremarkable, training. They took seats in the back row with the relaxed posture of people who'd decided this class was a free period.

Filling a timetable slot. Not here to learn. Here because everything else was full.

The fifth student arrived two minutes before the hour. He slouched in like he was doing the room a favour by showing up. Sandy hair, a jaw that led the rest of his face, and a mana signature that was deliberately showy — First Circle, but he was projecting it like a peacock spreading its feathers. The kind of noble-born student who'd had private cultivation tutors since childhood and wanted everyone to know it.

He sat in the back row, leaned his chair onto two legs, and looked at Lucian with an expression of polite contempt.

Ah. There's always one.

The sixth student arrived last.

He came through the door like he wasn't entirely sure it was the right one — a broad-shouldered young man with sun-darkened skin, rough hands, and a battered rucksack that he carried like a shield. Lucian recognised him immediately. The commoner from the courtyard. The one with the boulder-sized soul.

Up close, the mana signature was even more striking. First Circle, technically — he'd only recently awakened — but the raw volume behind it was staggering. It was like standing next to a dam and hearing the water behind it. All that potential, completely unrefined, with no cultivation technique or fragment access to shape it.

He looked around the room, found the last empty seat in the front row next to the bookish student, and sat down with the careful movements of someone trying very hard not to break anything.

There you are.

Six students. In a room built for thirty.

Lucian looked at them. They looked back — some with mild interest, some with boredom, one with open disdain.

The leftovers. The students nobody else wanted, taught by the professor nobody else wanted. We're a matched set.

He let four threads handle observation — reading micro-expressions, mana fluctuations, posture shifts, breathing patterns. All six students analysed simultaneously in real-time. Four more threads ran background processes: lesson planning, vocabulary adjustment based on apparent comprehension levels, risk assessment of every word before he spoke it.

To the students, he appeared to do nothing more than stand behind a lectern and look at them with mild, bespectacled calm.

"Good morning," he said. "I am Professor Caelmont. This is Fundamentals of Soul Theory. And I suspect most of you would rather be somewhere else."

A beat of silence. The sandy-haired noble in the back snorted.

"Let me save us all some time," Lucian continued, his tone conversational. Unhurried. Not trying to impress. "The textbook for this course is Maren's 'Principles of the Soul,' fourth edition. It is comprehensive, well-organised, and almost entirely adequate."

He paused.

"Almost."

The bookish student in the front row looked up from his notebook.

"I could spend the semester walking you through that textbook chapter by chapter. You would pass the examination. You would learn the accepted fundamentals. And you would leave this course believing you understood how the soul works." He adjusted his glasses. "That would be a disservice to all of us."

The noble girl in the second row tilted her head. A fraction of a degree. Barely perceptible.

She's listening.

"So let me begin with a story." Lucian stepped out from behind the lectern and leaned against the edge of the desk. Casual. Unthreatening. "Two hundred years ago, a Third Circle mage named Aldren Fosse was working as a spell-etcher for the imperial post office. Boring job. He spent his days etching the same communication spell into relay crystals, over and over. Thousands of them. Year after year."

He let that image settle.

"One day, Fosse noticed something odd. A crystal he'd etched on a Tuesday worked slightly differently from one he'd etched on a Thursday. Not in what the spell did — but in how much mana it consumed. The Tuesday crystal used about three per cent less mana for the same result."

Emeric Hayle's pen stopped moving.

"Fosse, being a diligent and somewhat obsessive man, investigated. He tested hundreds of crystals. And he found that the variation was consistent — crystals etched in the morning consumed less mana than those etched in the afternoon. Not because the crystals were different. Because he was different. His soul was slightly more rested in the morning, which meant the etching pattern was fractionally cleaner."

Lucian picked up the textbook and held it up.

"This story is in Maren. Chapter four, page sixty-one. Maren uses it to illustrate the importance of soul condition during spell etching. Perfectly reasonable conclusion." He set the book down. "But Maren misses the far more interesting question. Does anyone see it?"

Silence. The two friends in the back exchanged a glance. Doyle Merce, the proud noble kid, was still leaning his chair back, but his eyes had sharpened.

"The question," Lucian said, "is this: if a three per cent variation in the etcher's soul condition produces a measurable difference in spell efficiency — what else about the etcher's soul might affect the outcome? We teach spell etching as a standardised process. Same pattern, same result. But Fosse's data proves that it isn't. The soul isn't a passive medium. It's an active participant. And if that's true..."

He looked at them.

"Then everything we think we know about how spells interact with the soul might be missing half the picture."

Ren Valkis, the commoner kid, was staring at him with brows drawn together — not following the technical details, but sensing that something was being cracked open. The noble girl had leaned forward a fraction of an inch.

Doyle Mercer's chair was back on all four legs.

"I am not going to give you the answer," Lucian said. "Because I don't have it. What I have is the question — and a semester to teach you how to ask better ones." He adjusted his glasses. "That is what this course is going to be. Not a recitation of accepted wisdom. An investigation. We're going to take the fundamentals apart, piece by piece, and find out which ones actually hold up."

He returned to the lectern and opened Maren's textbook to chapter one.

"Now. Let us start with what they got right. Open your copies to page twelve. The soul as a mana vessel — a useful model, if incomplete."

He began teaching. Properly teaching — weaving the textbook material into a narrative, connecting dry theory to vivid examples, asking questions that led students to conclusions rather than handing them answers. Two threads handled content delivery and real-time adjustment. Two more tracked comprehension across all six faces — noticing when Ren's expression glazed (too abstract, switch to a physical analogy) and when Emeric's pen accelerated (he's getting it, push further). The remaining threads monitored his own word choice, ensuring nothing he said drifted toward the territories he needed to protect.

Eight threads. Four to teach. Four to make sure I don't accidentally revolutionise magical theory in a first-year lecture.

The cocky noble — Doyle Mercer — had started the lecture with his arms crossed. Twenty minutes in, his arms were uncrossed. Thirty minutes in, he was leaning forward. Forty minutes in, he asked a question.

"If the textbook model is incomplete, why hasn't anyone corrected it?"

It was asked with challenge in the voice, but it was a real question. A good one.

"Because the incomplete model works well enough," Lucian said. "It produces mages who can cast spells, pass examinations, and function professionally. The gaps in understanding don't become apparent until you push the boundaries — and most people never push the boundaries. They learn the accepted wisdom, build their careers on it, and pass it on to the next generation unchanged."

He paused, letting that land.

"The history of knowledge, in any field, is the history of people being content with 'good enough.' Progress comes from the ones who aren't."

Doyle Mercer didn't respond. But he didn't lean back, either.

The hour ended. Lucian closed the textbook.

"For next session, I'd like you to read chapters one through three of Maren. But as you read, I want you to keep a list — every claim Maren makes that he doesn't prove. Every assertion he presents without evidence. Bring the list. We'll discuss them."

The students gathered their things. The two friends in the back were whispering to each other with an energy they hadn't had walking in. The bookish student — Emeric Hayle, according to the roll — lingered, looking like he wanted to ask something but couldn't quite summon the nerve.

Ren Valkis was the last to stand. He shouldered his rucksack and paused at the door, glancing back at Lucian.

Their eyes met for a moment.

Lucian gave him a small nod. Nothing more. Nothing that would mean anything to anyone watching.

But what he was thinking, behind eight threads of carefully controlled cognition, was:

That soul of yours is extraordinary. You have no idea what you're carrying. But you will.

Ren left.

Lucian stood alone in the basement lecture hall, surrounded by empty benches and the ghost of chalk dust, and allowed himself a moment of genuine satisfaction.

Six students. The leftovers of the academy's intake, dumped into the course nobody wanted, taught by the professor nobody respected.

That's fine. I didn't come here to be respected.

I came here to teach and learn.

He collected his notes, adjusted his glasses, and walked out.

The corridor was quiet. His footsteps echoed on the stone.

Somewhere above him, in the grand lecture halls with the tall windows and the prestigious faculty, a hundred students were learning the accepted wisdom from professors who had never questioned it.

Down here, in a basement room with six chairs and bad lighting, something else had just begun.
