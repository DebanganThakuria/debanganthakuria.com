Every soul is a vessel of two virtues: capacity, which we call density, and clarity, which we call quality. Cultivation deepens the vessel. Etching polishes it. The nine Circles are nothing but agreed milestones along that road — and the road is longer than any man's life.

— Whitsun's Primer of Soul Cultivation, twelfth edition

---

Lucian woke at six, in the dark, in a cold room, and lay still for one minute while his mind reported to him.

This was the routine. Eight threads ran in his head at all times — eight separate lines of thought, each working on its own task, none needing the others. It was an old spell called [Parallel Thoughts], recovered from texts nobody else had bothered to decode, and it was the first of his secrets. Most mages thought one thought at a time. He was a committee.

The night report was short. Thread five had kept watch on the cloud and had nothing to report except that the scholar overhead had worked until three and the east range's pipes had knocked from four. Threads one through four had spent the night on the fold-lattice from the evidence cellar, and had progress: the lattice used nine families of spell-forms, and six of the nine were now sorted and understood. Two weeks more, perhaps, at this pace. Thread eight had rested. Thread eight was always the one he rested, because thread eight was the one that listened for trouble, and a tired sentry was no sentry.

He got up, lit the grate, put the kettle on its hook, and washed in cold water while the room decided whether to warm.

While he shaved, he ran the morning drill. Cultivation was breathing for the soul: draw ambient mana in, press it dense, hold it, release. Density was fuel — it set how much a soul could hold and spend in a fight. But density alone had never advanced anyone a single Circle. Advancement was a different ledger, and his copy of it was the strangest in the kingdom.

And here was the second secret, the deep one, the one that made all the others possible.

Eight months ago, in this room, Lucian had taken his own soul apart.

Every text agreed on how mana regeneration worked: soul density set how much you could hold, soul quality set how cleanly it flowed back. Every text was measuring the vessel. Nobody had thought to measure its surface — and surface was the true law. Mana returned through the soul's outer face. Double the surface, double the regeneration. Nobody had noticed because nobody could change a soul's surface.

He could. He had fragmented his soul into countless particles, each too small to see, and bound each particle to hydrogen drawn out of ordinary water by a small spell of his own design. The result surrounded his body day and night in an invisible cloud a few yards across. That cloud was not a spell he cast. It was him — his own soul, spread out into the air, with more surface than a thousand ordinary souls together.

The consequences ran his whole life. Mana regenerated through a soul's surface — and his soul's surface, spread across billions of particles, exceeded an ordinary mage's by a factor he had calculated once, laughed at quietly, and stopped bothering to update. He regenerated more mana in a second than most master mages could hold at once. For practical purposes, he never ran dry.

Which answered the question any scholar would have asked first, if any scholar had been allowed to ask. [Parallel Thoughts] and [Atomic Soul] both ran day and night, and always-on master spells had upkeep — the threads alone would drain a Circle 6 dry in hours, and maintaining the cloud cost more still. No mage in the kingdom could pay for either. He paid for both without noticing, because the cloud's own regeneration covered its own upkeep first and left a surplus that covered everything else: the threads, every casting, every counter, forever. The engine fueled itself, then fueled the rest of him. It was also why the etching order had been forced — [Parallel Thoughts] was unaffordable until [Atomic Soul] existed to pay for it — and why the technique could never be copied even if someone stole every note he had. On an ordinary soul, the cloud's first hour of upkeep was bankruptcy. The spell only worked for someone already running the spell.

The cloud was also a limb: within a foot of his skin he could do fine work with it — pick a lock, hold a pen, unpick a spell mid-cast. Out to arm's reach he could shove, catch, and bind. Out to ten yards he could feel everything — every soul, every enchantment, every banknote in every pocket. Past thirty yards it thinned to a whisper. And no one could detect it. Soul-sense read souls as single bodies of light; his was a fog, and every mage in the kingdom had been trained since childhood to see lamps.

He called the technique [Atomic Soul]. It existed in no book, no lecture, and no other living person.

The kettle boiled. He made tea and counted the rest of the inventory while it steeped, because Tuesdays deserved an orderly start.

The law of advancement was simple enough that schoolchildren chanted it. A mage rose by etching — carving a fully mastered spell permanently into the soul, each etching improving the soul by one stage, ten stages to a Circle. Ten apprentice-grade spells carried you up each of the first three Circles: the apprentice tier, thirty etchings in all. Then came the Wall. To enter Circle 4 you had to etch your first master-grade spell — layered work, an order of magnitude deeper, never taught in any lecture hall, only owned, inherited, or bought. The master tier ran the same way, Circles 4 through 6, ten master etchings each. Circle 7 was called Grand Master, and there were a handful alive. Circle 8 was called Archmage, and there might be one. Circle 9 was called Saint, and there was no one.

Lucian's soul carried thirty-two etchings.

Thirty apprentice-grade: the full ladder of the first three Circles, completed years ago in the slow, expensive way everyone completed it, including the five he actually fought with — [Force Bolt] for stopping power, [Barrier Shell] for cover, [Mana Pulse] for close quarters, [Wind Step] for speed, [Binding Thread] for ending fights politely — and twenty-five more of the standard curriculum. Then three years stood stalled at the Wall: thirty etchings done, no master-grade spell obtainable, and a fading house with no library to lend him one. That much of the public story was simply true.

And then two master-grade, both his own. [Atomic Soul] had been the first — the etching that broke him through the Wall into Circle 4, eight months ago, in this room. [Parallel Thoughts] had been the second. Circle 4, second stage.

Eight more master etchings would make Circle 5, and under the law there was no ceremony about it: the tenth etching was the breakthrough. The public arithmetic said a man in his position might scrape together eight master-grade spells in fifteen years, if patrons smiled. His private arithmetic — with eight threads mastering spells in parallel, and a space-magic lattice already half-decoded in his head — said rather less. When the day came, he would choose it the way he chose his coats: deliberately, and to fit the occasion.

There was, of course, a question the entire College had been not-asking for eight months. Every mage above Circle 4 could see he had crossed the Wall, and crossing the Wall meant a master-grade spell, and everyone knew House Caelmont had no library left and Lucian Caelmont had no money. So where had it come from? Etiquette forbade the question — one did not ask a mage the provenance of his spells, any more than one asked a gentleman his income — which meant it could never be settled, only whispered. The whispers had settled into camps. A hidden family heirloom. A ruinous black-market copy. A secret patron. The cleverer whisperers, who knew his archive habits, said he had dug something lost out of the old texts, and they were nearly right about the second spell and perfectly wrong about the first.

His prepared answer, on the rare occasions a boor asked outright, was six words: "I'd rather not discuss provenance." Under the etiquette, this was not evasion. It was manners.

Then the craft he had never shown anyone at full strength: anti-spelling. Every spell was a pattern of flowing mana. Build the opposite pattern and press it on — the spell died. The theory was two centuries old and considered unusable, because doing it live required reading an incoming spell, computing its inverse, and casting the counter, all inside a second, all while someone was trying to kill you. No single mind could. Eight minds could. To everyone he had ever used it on, their own magic had simply failed.

And now, newest on the shelf: the fold. Space magic, read out of a bomb's casing and carried home in his head. A few weeks of nights and he would fold his first pocket of space — a hidden place inside his own coat that no search in the kingdom could find. After that, greater things.

That was the true ledger of Lucian Caelmont. The public ledger read differently: a stalled researcher, lately and unremarkably arrived at Circle 4, stripped of his sponsor in front of four hundred people, kept on at the College by a headmistress's charity, fit for tutoring difficult children and consulting on corpses.

The masquerade had four rules, and he kept them the way other men kept commandments. Never cast without the wand where anyone could see. Never know anything an instrument had not visibly told him. Never win in a manner that could not be called luck. And never, under any pressure, show a fifth spell, a ninth thread, or the cloud.

The College believed he was a failure pretending to be a researcher. The truth ran the other way: he was a power pretending to be a failure. Either direction, the word the students whispered fit him perfectly.

Fraud.

One thing in the room was neither secret nor performance. Under everything — under the threads, the cloud, the etched spells — his soul carried a wound, old and structural, torn through him when he was seven years old. The family records called it an accident and said nothing else. The wound did not hurt. It filtered. Joy, grief, fear — everything reached him at half its proper volume, the shape of each feeling arriving without its weight. Soul-lattice repair, the research the whole hall had laughed at, was not an academic interest. It was the only door out of the glass box he lived in.

He finished his tea, dressed — charcoal wool, watch chain, grey gloves, the walnut wand seated in its holster under the coat — adjusted his glasses, and went out into the city to spend money.

---

Tuesday's errand was tomorrow's tutoring. A first session needed a primer, chalks, and a practice crystal, and buying them meant a walk across half the Scholars' Quarter, which suited him. He thought well while walking, and threads one through four wanted the streets' spare rhythm for their work on the fold.

The fog had lifted to roof height and hung there, making a low grey ceiling over the Quarter. Coffee-house windows stood fogged from the inside; a newsboy on the corner was crying a tariff vote in the Commons; two constables came down the opposite pavement in step, wands holstered, capes pearled with damp. On the corner of Theobald Lane an iron wheel the size of a dinner plate was mounted on a housefront shrine, and an old woman touched its rim with two fingers as she passed, the standard courtesy to the Faith. The city, going about its business, in the amber of its lamps.

Penrith's bookshop smelled of dust and glue. Penrith himself was up his ladder, and sold Lucian a secondhand Whitsun's Primer, twelfth edition, for one mark fifty, without coming down.

"Teaching, Mr. Caelmont?"

"Attempting it."

"Whitsun never met a student in his life," Penrith said, making change from his waistcoat. "You can tell by the confidence. Oh — and the Row clocks, you'll want to know. The Crier had it this morning. A foundry regulator, streets away, throwing its cycle wide. All eleven clocks keeping the regulator's time instead of the city's."

"Disappointing."

"Deeply," said Penrith with satisfaction, and went back up the ladder.

The alchemy row began where the Scholars' Quarter shaded into the Mercantile District: six shopfronts of bottle-glass and brass, and behind each window, the mage economy laid out with price tickets.

Lucian stopped at Harrow & Sons and read the window the way the whole city read it.

A lesser density draught — one month's worth of faster cultivation — forty-five marks. A full course, the kind that moved a stalled student one step up within a Circle: three hundred. A common labourer earned four hundred marks in a year. On the shelf above, etching catalysts from one hundred and twenty marks, because botching an etching could cripple a soul, and insurance came dear. And along the back wall, the raw fuel of all of it: mana crystals. Rough furnace-grade by the pound. Clear cultivation-grade chips at five pence each, the little luxury a poor mage saved toward. And in a locked case at the counter, on velvet, three pale fingers of flawless deep-grade crystal, priced by the ounce at ninety marks, from which the true elixirs were refined.

Everything in the kingdom that made a mage stronger began as crystal in a case. Lucian bought a paper of practice chips, a box of casting chalks, and nothing else, and came out eleven marks lighter.

The family was sitting by the shop's downpipe, where the wall gave shelter from the drip.

A man in a stoker's canvas coat, ash worked so deep into the seams that no brush would ever raise it, holding a square of card lettered in careful capitals: SOUL-HURT CHILD. Beside him on a folded blanket sat a girl of perhaps eight, small for it, wrapped to the chin, watching the street with the stillness of children who have learned that energy is a cost.

Lucian's cloud read her before he had fully seen her.

The wound sat in the upper region of her soul's lattice — a tear the length of his thumb, edges frayed, and spreading. Slowly. A furnace-breach injury: raw mana had gone through her the way steam goes through a hand. Untreated, the fraying would creep for years, dimming first her senses, then her memory, then the girl herself. A stabilising draught would stop the spread — thirty-five marks, forty feet away, behind bottle-glass. Repair, the actual closing of the tear, was not for sale in that shop or any shop. The consensus of two centuries said repair was impossible.

He knew the consensus was in error. He had said so to four hundred people, and they had laughed, and none of them were sitting on this blanket.

"Sir," the stoker said. Not begging. Reporting. "The Ring took her, sir. Breach at Colton's, summer before last. Settlement was a week's wages." He said it the way men recite weather. "Draught's thirty-five marks. I get it some years."

Some years. A draught held the fraying for eight, ten months. The arithmetic of that sentence was the whole Manufacturing Ring in six words.

Lucian crouched to the child's level. "What's your name?"

"Ivy," the girl said.

"Ivy. May I?" He held out one gloved hand, the gesture of a scholar taking a pulse — theatre, entirely; the cloud needed no touch. She put her small hand on his glove, and for the eight seconds she held it there, invisibly, at a range where his control was absolute, Lucian worked.

He could not close the tear. Closing it needed the resonance-stitching his research had not finished — needed months and instruments and material he did not have. But the fraying edges could be settled, each loose thread of her lattice smoothed and sealed against its neighbour, the way one whips the end of a rope. It was exactly what the thirty-five-mark draught did, done cleaner, and it cost him eight seconds and a quantity of mana he would replace by the end of the street.

The spread stopped. The wound stayed — but it stopped.

"Her grip's strong," he said, releasing the hand. He stood, and gave the stoker two marks — a plausible coin, a scholar's coin, the kind that bought soup for a fortnight and raised no questions. "The Watch surgeon at the Embankment station sees children on Fridays," he added, which was true and nearly useless, because Watch surgeons could no more repair a soul than the Watch's tape measures could.

There was nowhere to send them. That was the plain fact of the kingdom, and he carried it away with him down the row: a city of two million, a Manufacturing Ring that broke souls at settled rates, six shining shops selling the cure for everything except what could not be cured — and nowhere, at any price, that a stoker could take a soul-hurt child and be told anything but sorry.

Someday, he thought — and it was a thought with a date attached, because his plans acquired dates or died — there would be a door in this city for the Ivys. A door with no price ticket in the window.

First, the research. First, the money. First, Tuesday.

Ivy would keep. He had made sure of it, eight seconds' worth, and she would never know.

---

He took the short way home, which was his second mistake of the errand, the first having been only two marks.

The short way cut behind the alchemy row through Tanner's Court — a slot of an alley, one lamp, standing fog — and the man was waiting a third of the way down it, because that was where the lamp's light ended.

"Purse," the man said. "And the parcel."

He was young, though it took looking to see it. Hollow-cheeked, wet-eyed, in a coat gone at both elbows, and his wand hand shook — a stolen wand, service pattern, with a crack glued along its length. Lucian's cloud read him in the first second, the whole sad file of him: soul newly awakened and never trained, Circle 1 in raw force, and fraying all over — not one clean wound but a general thinning, the signature of a mana-eater. Somewhere nearby, this man had found a corrupted pocket — a cellar, a culvert — where raw mana pooled, and he had been drinking from it for the warmth, and it was dissolving him at the edges the way lye dissolves cloth.

A self-awakened, then. One of the five in every hundred born with the capacity, ignited in some tenement with no one to teach him, now robbing scholars in an alley to eat. The alchemy row stood forty feet behind Lucian with three hundred marks of cultivation in its windows. The distance between this man and a College student was money. It had only ever been money.

"You are holding that wand backward for a bolt," Lucian said, "and the crack has severed the core in two places. If you cast through it, it will take your hand."

The man cast through it.

What happened next took four seconds, and Lucian conducted it at the pace of a man supervising a child's sums. The bolt died in the barrel — unpicked by the cloud before it formed, which incidentally saved the man's hand. The wand then left the shaking grip, floated sideways out of the lamp's light, and laid itself on a window ledge out of easy reach. And when the man lunged with his fists, because desperation always had a second act, a thread of force took his ankles, and he sat down on the wet cobbles, hard, and stayed there, held.

He began, hopelessly, to cry.

Lucian crouched — the second time that hour, the same movement, and the symmetry of it was not lost on the thread that watched him watch himself. Up the street, a child rotting from one wound. Down the alley, a man rotting from a hundred. The city manufactured both, at settled rates, and posted the cure in a window at prices set for other people.

"Listen to me," he said, and waited until the wet eyes managed it. "The pocket you have been drinking from. Culvert or cellar?"

Confusion, then fear. "Cellar. Off Dyer's Row. How—"

"Stop drinking from it. What you feel as warmth is the pocket eating the edges of your soul. Three more months of it and there will be no more warmth for you ever. Do you understand?"

"It's cold," the man said simply, and there was the whole argument of the poor against every good advice ever given.

"Yes." Lucian put coins on the cobbles between them — fifty pence, a hansom's fare, four days of dinners. "The parish kitchen behind the Wheel shrine on Theobald Lane serves at six. The Watch takes on lamp-trimmers at the winter hiring; the work is outdoors and the mana in the lamp oil will do your soul more good than the cellar is doing it harm. And leave the wand where it is. You cannot afford what it costs to use."

He released the binding-thread and walked on. Behind him he heard the man gather the coins, and then, after a long pause, footsteps going the other way — away from the window ledge, away from the wand. Sometimes the arithmetic came out that way. Not often. He noted the outcome without deciding what he felt about it, which saved time, since whatever he felt would arrive at half volume anyway and be filed with the rest.

The fold could hide anything from any search, once he built it. The cloud could stop any spell ever cast at him. Neither could do a single thing about a cold cellar off Dyer's Row.

He added it to the ledger — the true ledger, the one with dates.

---

By lamplight that evening, his study was ready for company.

The scarred oak desk had been cleared to bare wood. On it: the Whitsun's Primer, secondhand, its previous owner's pencil notes rubbed out margin by margin over an hour. The box of chalks. The paper of practice chips, each little crystal holding its five pennies' worth of clean mana. And the sheet with five questions on it, which was the only lesson plan Clara Vance's fifth tutor intended to bring.

He set the two unmatched chairs on opposite sides of the desk and looked at the arrangement for a moment. Since he had taken these rooms, no one had ever sat in the second chair.

Threads one through four went back to the fold. Thread five kept the building. Thread eight rested, listening. And somewhere in the remaining machinery of him, faint, at half its proper volume but present — unmistakably present — was something he took a moment to identify, because it visited so rarely.

He was looking forward to tomorrow.

He turned down the lamp.
