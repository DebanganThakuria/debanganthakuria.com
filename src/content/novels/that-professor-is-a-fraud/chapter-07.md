Lucian had developed a morning routine.

It was, he'd found, important to have one. Not for productivity — eight parallel threads made productivity a solved problem — but for appearances. A man with no routine attracted attention. A man who woke at the same time, walked the same route, ate at the same table, and arrived at the same library desk each morning was, by definition, unremarkable. And unremarkable was exactly what Lucian intended to be.

He rose at six. Washed. Dressed. Put on the glasses, which had already begun to feel more like a part of his face than an accessory. Then he left his quarters and walked.

The faculty residence hall was on the eastern edge of campus — a long, three-storey building of grey stone that had once been, according to the campus guide, a dormitory for visiting diplomats. It still had that air about it: serviceable, respectable, designed to accommodate without impressing. His room was on the second floor, wedged between a supply closet and the quarters of Professor Danthem, who taught Advanced Glyph Construction and communicated exclusively through disapproving silences.

They had not spoken. Lucian was content with this arrangement.

The corridor led to a staircase, which led to a courtyard, which led to the campus proper — and every morning, stepping through that archway felt like stepping onto a stage where he was simultaneously the least important actor and the only one who'd read the entire script.

---

Ashveil Academy in the early morning was, Lucian had to admit, beautiful.

The main courtyard was the hub from which everything branched. At its centre stood the fountain with the veild lady's figure, apparently the founder, its water catching the dawn light in colours that shifted from gold to pale violet as the sun climbed. Pathways radiated outward like spokes: north toward the lecture halls and administrative buildings, south toward the student dormitories, east toward the faculty residences and the library, and west toward the training grounds.

The buildings were a patchwork of centuries. The oldest — the library, the chancellor's Alabaster Hall, the original lecture wing — were built from pale stone so old it had developed a faint luminescence from centuries of mana exposure. Newer additions branched off in darker stone and sharper angles, functional rather than beautiful, built to accommodate an institution that had outgrown its founders' ambitions.

Between the buildings, gardens. Training plots where body cultivation exercises were performed each dawn, the grass worn thin in concentric circles. And in a secluded corner behind the northern wing were eery fog covered grounds, remnants of a minor March that had manifested on the grounds forty years ago and left traces of the Spirit World embedded in the soil.

Two threads catalogued the environment as he walked. The rest worked on problems that mattered.

---

The mission board was impossible to miss.

It dominated the wall outside the faculty wing — a massive slab of polished darkwood, six feet tall and ten feet wide, covered in pinned notices, sealed envelopes, and colour-coded assignment cards. Enchantment kept it organised: completed missions faded to grey, new postings glowed faintly at the edges, and urgent requests pulsed with a soft amber light.

Lucian stopped in front of it — not for the first time this week, but for the first time with serious intent.

He'd noticed it on his second day. Everyone had. The mission board was as central to campus life as the library or the training grounds. Students crowded around it between lectures, scanning for tasks that would earn them enough credits to stay enrolled another semester. Faculty checked it for consultation cases — external contracts that paid real coin.

The student side of the board was a study in inequality.

Tier 1 student tasks occupied the bottom third: herb gathering, message delivery, mana-beast observation, civilian construction assistance. Low danger, low pay. A cluster of first-years were studying these now — commoners, mostly, calculating which tasks would clear their semester quota fastest. One of them, Lucian noticed, was Ren Valkis. He stood slightly apart from the group, reading the postings with the focused intensity of someone for whom these weren't optional.

He needs about fifty credits this semester. The herb-gathering missions pay one to two each. That's about twenty-five missions on top of a full course load. Meanwhile, the Mercer boy probably had his quota supplemented by a family donation before the semester started.

The middle section was Tier 2 student tasks — escort duty, Sentinel calibration assistance, research support. Better pay, restricted to second-years and above. A few senior scholars were reviewing these with the practised eye of people who'd been working this board for years.

The top section was faculty consultations. External contracts posted by noble houses, merchant guilds, the Sentinels, and town authorities. Colour-coded by tier:

White cards — Tier 1. Pest control. Enchantment inspections. Property damage assessments. The work that nobody with a reputation would touch.

Blue cards — Tier 2. Missing person investigations. Criminal hunts. March residue analysis. Security audits. Fourth Circle minimum, typically.

Red cards — Tier 3. Sentinel contracts. Entity containment. Classified operations. Fifth Circle and above.

Lucian looked at the white cards.

Eight Sovereigns a month. That's my salary. The lowest faculty tier. A Tier 1 consultation pays one to three Sovereigns per case. If I take one a week, I nearly double my income.

More importantly — it gets me off campus. Into situations where I can test what I've built recently.

He reached up and pulled a white card from the board. The enchantment registered the removal, logging his name and the case details automatically.

Case #1147-T1: Farmer reports mana-beast activity near crop fields. Ashveil township outskirts. Contact: Garrett Thorne, Millhaven Farm. Fee: 2 Gold Sovereigns.

Mana-beasts. Probably corrupted foxes or feral mana-rats drawn to a ley-line leak. Basic pest control.

Perfect first outing.

He tucked the card into his coat and continued his walk. Behind him, a student glanced at the spot where the card had been, then at the bespectacled professor walking away, and looked faintly puzzled. Faculty didn't usually take Tier 1 cases. Certainly not professors who came from founding houses.

Let them wonder. A broke Caelmont taking pest control jobs. Entirely in character.

---

The training grounds occupied the entire western quarter of the campus — a sprawling complex of open-air arenas, enclosed sparring halls, obstacle courses, and what appeared to be spell power measuring devices. Even at dawn, they were alive with activity.

Students drilled in formation under the supervision of combat faculty. Mana flashed in controlled bursts — fire, force, barrier spells — accompanied by the sharp crack of spell-on-shield impacts and the occasional thud of someone hitting the ground. The physical intensity was striking. A young woman took a force-bolt to the chest, staggered, shook it off, and closed the distance to her sparring partner in two strides that left cracks in the hard-packed earth.

Body cultivation. She's investing heavily in the physical path. Smart — if your regen is average, make your body the weapon and use mana sparingly.

Three threads catalogued fighting styles, spell variants, and instructor techniques.

Near the edge of the training grounds, a different kind of preparation was underway. A group of second-year students in light armour were checking equipment beside a waiting carriage — supplies, waterskins, collection vials, a rolled map. A mission team, heading out for a Tier 2 task. Their faculty supervisor, a Fourth Circle combat instructor, was running through safety protocols with the bored efficiency of someone who'd done this a hundred times.

That could be me in a few weeks. Supervising student missions. Leading March expeditions. If I can build a track record with the Tier 1 cases first.

If anyone would trust a Third Circle professor to lead students into anything more dangerous than a library.

He turned east toward the faculty dining hall.

---

The faculty dining hall was not the student refectory.

The student refectory was a massive, noisy, stone-floored space where several hundred students ate at long communal tables and the air smelled permanently of porridge and adolescent ambition. Lucian had passed it but never entered. His salary covered faculty dining — one of the few perks that came with the lowest rung.

The faculty dining hall was smaller, quieter, and panelled in dark wood. Round tables seating four to six were arranged across the room, spaced for private conversation. The food was served from a sideboard — bread, cheese, cured meats, fruit, eggs, and a pot of something grain-based that Lucian had learned to accept as this world's approximation of porridge.

The mana in the room was dense. Not oppressive, but present — the combined ambient signatures of thirty-odd faculty members, ranging from Third Circle junior lecturers to the occasional Fifth or Sixth Circle department head. Walking in was like stepping into warm water.

Lucian took a plate, served himself eggs and bread, and sat at his usual table — a small one near the window, positioned where he could observe the room without appearing to.

The seating arrangement was as rigidly hierarchical as any noble court. Senior faculty held the central tables — Fourth and Fifth Circle, department heads, long-tenured professors with political weight. The table nearest the sideboard was the combat faculty's domain — louder, broader, radiating the particular confidence of people who could back up their opinions with force.

At the head of that table sat Professor Cassius Renweld.

Lucian had been studying him from a distance for several days now. Cassius was tall. Broad shoulders, dark hair kept military-short, a jaw that could have been used to break siege equipment. His mana signature was a constant, deliberate broadcast — Fifth Circle, dense and aggressive, the kind of presence that didn't just fill a room but claimed it.

He was, by all accounts, House Renweld's man at Ashveil. The family's largest patron, the family's professor, the family's eyes. His combat students consistently ranked among the academy's best. His private tuition fees, Lucian estimated, probably earned him more in fragments per month than Lucian's salary paid in Sovereigns per year.

The opposite of me in every measurable way. And if the consultation board gossip is accurate, he's been tipped to lead the advisory committee for the inter-academy tournament next year.

None of which concerns me in the slightest.

This was true. It was also, Lucian reflected, slightly concerning that it was true. The old him — either version — would have felt something about the disparity. Resentment, perhaps. Or competitive fire. Or at least the sting of being irrelevant.

Instead, two threads idly calculated the structural weaknesses in Cassius's mana barrier based on his signature output, and the remaining eight continued with their day.

At the opposite end of the room, alone at a table even more isolated than Lucian's, sat a man who was Cassius Renweld's precise opposite in every measurable way.

Professor Aldous Merrin was thin, stooped, and appeared to be engaged in a long-standing argument with his own porridge. His hair was white and escaped from his head at angles that suggested it had given up on discipline decades ago. His mana signature was Fourth Circle — respectable but not remarkable — and it had a quality Lucian could only describe as tired. Not weak. Tired. Like a fire that had been burning for a very long time on not quite enough fuel.

He was 130 years old. Twenty years to reach Seventh Circle, or join the Dimming, mages whose souls slowly erode after 150 if they haven't reached Seventh Circle.

A century of soul theory research. A lifetime spent one conceptual leap away from the answer. And nobody in this room — nobody in this world — has any idea how close he actually is. Except me.

We'll talk, Professor Merrin. Soon. But not yet.

Lucian returned his attention to his eggs and let the threads handle surveillance.

The conversation he'd been expecting arrived at approximately seven forty, from the central table, in a voice that carried like it had been trained to.

"—honestly, have you seen his class assignment? Fundamentals. In the basement. Six students." A Fourth Circle woman with a sharp face and sharper opinions. She said six students the way one might say six cases of plague.

"The Caelmont boy?" A male colleague. Mildly interested, the way one is mildly interested in a carriage accident. "I'd heard. Lysander's little charity project."

"Charity implies generosity. This is politics. Bertholme Caelmont called in a debt, and Lysander paid it in the cheapest currency available — a teaching slot nobody wanted."

"I'm told the boy is Third Circle. Barely." A third voice — dry, nasal, a collector of facts. "Peak Third, if we're being generous. His Veranthos transcript is in the registry. I looked. Thoroughly unremarkable."

"Unremarkable is generous. His soul quality signature is barely above the students he's teaching. And have you heard? He took a case from the consultation board this morning. A white card." She let the colour-coding do the work. "Pest control. A founding house professor doing pest control."

The laughter was polite, contained, and genuine. The kind that comes from shared certainty about the natural order of things.

"The real question is how long Lysander lets it continue. She's not sentimental. If he embarrasses the academy — and he will — she'll have him removed before the year is out. The Caelmont name doesn't carry the weight it used to."

"Barely carries the weight of the paper it's written on, these days."

More laughter.

Lucian ate a piece of bread.

Eight threads continued their various tasks — pattern analysis, ambient monitoring, lesson planning, cultivation modelling, Tier 1 case preparation, a rather interesting calculation about a new spell, and a preliminary design for a custom cultivation exercise that would hypothetically benefit someone with enormous soul volume and terrible quality.

Two threads noted the conversation, tagged the speakers for future reference, and filed them under a mental category that didn't have a name yet but would eventually be labelled something like: People who will be very confused in about six months.

He finished his breakfast, returned his plate to the sideboard, and left the dining hall.

Nobody noticed.

---

The walk back across campus was pleasant. Sun clearing the eastern towers. Courtyard filling with students heading to morning lectures. Lucian walked among them, unremarkable in his faculty coat and spectacles, invisible in the way that only the truly unimportant are invisible.

He passed the mission board again. Ren Valkis was still there — no, he was back again. The boy was studying the Tier 1 tasks with the focused arithmetic of someone who was counting every credit against his survival. A noble student brushed past him without acknowledgement, reaching up to pull a Tier 2 card with the casual ease of someone who didn't need the credits but wanted the experience line on their record.

Maybe I can change his future trajectory with the cultivation exercise.

He continued walking. Passed the notice board outside the main lecture wing and paused — not because anything on it concerned him, but because three threads had simultaneously flagged an anomaly in his peripheral awareness.

Cassius Renweld was crossing the courtyard, heading toward the training grounds. And as he passed, his gaze swept the crowd — a predator's reflex, scanning for anything worth his attention.

His eyes found Lucian. Passed over him. Moved on.

Not dismissal. Not hostility. Something worse.

Non-recognition.

He hadn't even registered Lucian as a person worth noting. A piece of furniture. A fixture of the scenery. The Caelmont professor whose existence was so thoroughly categorised as irrelevant that it didn't warrant the half-second of attention it took to form an opinion.

Lucian watched him go.

He felt nothing.

This, he was beginning to understand, was both his greatest advantage and the thing that should worry him most.

I should feel something about a man whose family has been dismantling mine for decades looking through me like I'm made of glass.

It worried him slightly.

He adjusted his glasses, felt the weight of the white card in his coat pocket, and turned toward the library.

He had work to do.
