From the Westmere Chronicle, 16th of October:

OUTRAGE AT THE GRAND MERIDIAN FOILED. — Acting upon information received, officers of the Metropolitan Watch early this morning discovered and made safe an infernal device in the cellars of the Grand Meridian hotel, where the Halverford Symposium had concluded hours earlier. Eight men were taken into custody without incident. Printed matter recovered at the scene indicates the outrage was conceived by the so-called Sons of Dunmoor, a provincial society hitherto unknown to the authorities. The Watch is praised for its vigilance. Professor Halverford, asked for comment, said only that he was very fond of the hotel.

---

Lucian read four newspapers over breakfast in his rooms — tea, toast going cold on the tray, the grate smoking over its first coal of the day — which was two papers more than usual, and he learned more from what was missing than from what was printed.

The Chronicle had the Watch vigilant and the provinces guilty. The Daily Crier had the same story with adjectives, a diagram of the coal store that was inaccurate in nine respects, and an editorial demanding to know how deep the provincial rot went. Nobody mentioned the Veldran delegation. Nobody mentioned anything taken, because nobody knew anything had been taken. The pamphlets had done their work: printed weeks ago in someone's quiet room, and already this morning they were the truth.

Everyone had been given a story good enough to stop them looking for a better one.

He noted, with something adjacent to professional respect, that this had been the plan all along, and that it had survived the plan's own failure. The fire had been cancelled, the crew was in cells — and the false trail marched on regardless, needing no further help from anyone.

The Crier's back page carried the Peculiar Intelligences column. The lead item concerned the clocks of Cooper's Row, all eleven of which had been running backward since Tuesday. No connection to anything. Probably. He finished his tea.

One thread kept returning, uninvited, to a man in soldier's boots walking out of a service entrance with something old and quiet under his coat. Officially, nothing had happened at the Grand Meridian except an outrage foiled. Officially, the man did not exist. Lucian set the thread down — marked, kept, to be returned to — and went to his appointment.

---

The Senior Common Room at eight o'clock smelled of coal smoke and older arguments. Panelled walls, portraits of dead masters, leather chairs polished by a century of sitting. The teapot was silver and the tea was strong. Lara Allard — plain dark wool with jet buttons, a small watch pinned at the breast — poured, and did not offer him sugar, having observed at some point that he took none. He did not know when she had observed it, which was itself informative.

"— and the fund will pay one hundred and fifty marks the month," she was saying, "which is more than Wren and less than you are worth, a ratio you will find is standard here. Facilities as discussed. Now, duties."

"I had assumed there would be duties."

"You assumed correctly. You will assist my lecture course this term — anti-spell theory, third-years, Tuesdays. You will invigilate at the examinations, because everyone must, and complaining about it is the faculty's oldest shared pleasure. You will report to me here, privately, each week." She turned a page in a slim ledger. "And the College receives, from time to time, petitions from the Metropolitan Watch for a consulting scholar. Corpses, cellars, unpleasant weather. Nobody senior will touch them."

"And I am not senior."

"You are not anything," said Lara Allard pleasantly. "That is the great advantage of you, Mr. Caelmont. You will handle the petitions."

"Of course, Headmistress."

He performed the small resigned pause of a man receiving one more humiliation duty, and she watched him perform it, and neither of them commented on the quality of the other's work. She had said nothing at all about last night — not the coal dust, not his hour's absence, not the morning's headlines. He noted the nothing. It was a very complete nothing, of the kind that took discipline to maintain.

"There is one further matter." She drew a folder from beneath the ledger and set it in front of him. It was thin. Folders that thin were usually somebody's whole life. "The College has been asked to provide a private tutor. A discreet one. I am assigning you."

The seal on the folder was a vane and quartered field: House Vance.

Lucian looked at it for a moment. "The house's timing is remarkable."

"The request predates the symposium by a fortnight," said Lara, "which spared them the embarrassment of withdrawing it, and me the tedium of letting them." The corner of her mouth did something brief that was not a smile. "They asked for discretion. They did not ask for anyone in particular. I find that combination stimulating."

He opened the folder.

Clara Vance. Eighteen. Circle 1, awakened at fifteen — late. Below, in a clerk's tidy hand, the record: three years of private tuition. Four tutors. He read the four names — two he knew by reputation, one by his fees — and the dates beside them, each engagement shorter than the one before. The last had lasted five weeks.

At the bottom of the page, in different ink and a different hand — someone senior, someone tired — three words: Final attempt authorised.

"The family's assessment?"

"Stunted," said Lara. "Their word. The girl is to be withdrawn from the College at the end of term if there is no improvement." She retrieved her teacup. "There is no improvement. There has been no improvement for three years, at considerable expense, under tutors with excellent letters. You have Tuesdays and Fridays, four o'clock. The fee goes to the fund; you get the experience."

"And if I improve her?"

"Then House Vance will owe its results to the man it dropped in front of four hundred people," said Lara Allard, "which is nothing to do with why I chose you, and you may draw your own conclusions about that." She set down the cup. "Eight o'clock next Thursday for your report. Good morning, Mr. Caelmont."

He was at the door when she added, without looking up:

"The girl has been told four times that she is a difficult case. Do not be the fifth."

---

The College between eight and nine in the morning was a city inside the city. Gowned figures crossed the quadrangle through the fog, each on a fixed schedule. Water ran off the east range's gargoyles and cracked on the flagstones below. On the cloister notice-boards, the term's calendar had gone up overnight — examinations in six weeks, and below that a printed slip announcing that the Founders' Trials would be held at the customary time, all Circles, entries through one's tutor. Somebody had already pinned a card offering coaching at ruinous rates.

None of it had anything to do with him, and all of it would continue whether he lived or died. He found the thought steadying. Some men had cathedrals; he had this.

Outside the library, two junior fellows were discussing the invitations to the Ashbourne–Marchwell engagement ball, which had gone out that morning; the taller one said the guest list ran to four hundred; and then they saw him, and the conversation became, with practiced smoothness, a conversation about the weather. He wished them a pleasant morning. They wished him one back with the particular kindness the College reserved for men it had already buried once.

The porter's lodge smelled of pipe tobacco and floor polish. Behind the counter a wall of pigeonholes rose to the ceiling, and the head porter — grey mutton-chop whiskers, sleeves gartered — sorted letters into them without reading the names, by memory. In Lucian's pigeonhole there was post. Three items. A printed circular from the Junior Fellows' Dining Society, which had misplaced his resignation twice now. A bill from his tailor, polite, patient, and correct. And an envelope with a northern postmark, addressed in a small careful hand he had known before he could read.

He paid the tailor's bill at the lodge counter, on the spot, in coin. The other two items he carried upstairs.

His rooms were up two flights in the east range — a set held at the College's pleasure, which was to say, at hers: a study with a scarred oak desk and two chairs that did not match, a bedroom with an iron bedstead, shelves he had filled in his first month, and a grate that drew badly. Cheaper than any lodging in the Quarter, and the price was not paid in marks. A man who lived, worked, and ate inside his patron's walls could be evicted from his entire life with one signature. He kept a figure in his head — the sum at which he could rent rooms in the city with a door the College did not own — and he knew precisely how far away it was.

Inside, he put his mother's letter in the left-hand drawer of the desk, where its predecessors lay in date order, unopened. He did not decide anything about it. He had not decided anything about it eleven times now.

On the study door, when he had come up, a note had been pinned at eye level. The hand was large, round, and generous with underlining.

PROFESSOR. I called at half past seven but you were OUT. I have spoken to my father and it is all ARRANGED. I shall come on Wednesdays. Please do not be alarmed, the fee is very fair, I negotiated it myself against my own father which Papa says is not how it is done. — P. HOLLOWAY

Below it, smaller:

P.S. — I also recommended you to Clara, who needs somebody proper. You will like her. She is much more sensible than I am, but nobody is perfect.

Lucian stood in the corridor and read the note twice.

One thread observed that he had not told Miss Holloway where his rooms were, nor which mornings he kept, nor that he tutored at all — he had not known it himself until twenty minutes ago. Another thread observed that the Vance request had gone to the College a fortnight past, and that Penelope Holloway had recommended him to Clara Vance before the symposium, before the sponsorship fell, before any of it — when recommending Lucian Caelmont was, by every measure the world kept, a joke.

He added a line to a ledger that was becoming a habit.

Then he unpinned the note, folded it into his coat, and went in to prepare for a difficult case who must not be told she was one.

Wednesday, apparently, was already settled.

---

He gave the evening to the file.

The lamps of the east range went out one stair at a time; the fog took the quadrangle at dusk and kept it. Lucian spread the contents of the thin folder across his desk and read the whole of Clara Vance's magical life in ninety minutes: three years, four tutors, and enough correspondence to show that the family had paid promptly and asked for results often.

The four tutors told him more about themselves than about the girl.

The first had lasted a year, and his reports were a calendar of standard drills — breathing cycles, mana circulation, the Whitsun beginner's sequence — each month recorded in the same optimistic clerk's prose, each month showing no progress at all. A patient man following the manual over a cliff.

The second had lasted eight months and blamed the student. His reports used the word *temperament* eleven times. Towards the end he had recommended a firmer hand, and, reading between two lines of it, had applied one. Clara's marks in general studies dropped in the same season. Nobody appeared to have connected the two.

The third had lasted seven months, and his final report ran nine pages, of which nine defended the third tutor. It cited his other successes by name. It suggested, in language dressed for dinner, that some souls were simply poor material. His fee had been the highest of the four.

The fourth had lasted five weeks. His entire file was one paragraph: engagement concluded by mutual consent, no assessment offered, fees returned in full.

Lucian read that paragraph three times.

Tutors did not return fees. Failed tutors especially did not return fees — a returned fee was an admission entered in one's own ledger, and these were men who wrote nine pages to avoid admissions of one line. A tutor returned his fee when he wanted the engagement not merely ended but erased. Five weeks was long enough to run the standard diagnostics twice.

Something in the standard diagnostics had made a professional hand its money back.

He sat with that for a while, in the circle of amber lamplight at the centre of the dark study, while the College went quiet around him — a floor of scholars overhead settling into the night's work, somebody's kettle, somebody's violin, two million people beyond the walls going about ten thousand pieces of business that had nothing to do with a stunted Circle 1 or the man assigned to her.

Every tutor had drilled the standard forms, and drilled them harder when they failed. Not one report, in three years of paper, recorded anyone asking whether the standard forms fit the soul they were being driven into.

He drew a clean sheet toward him and did not write a lesson plan. There was no point planning lessons for a structure nobody had mapped. He wrote four questions instead — the four nobody in the file had asked — and, after consideration, a fifth: *what did the fourth tutor see?*

Tuesday, four o'clock. He turned down the lamp.

Beyond the window the fog had the quadrangle to itself, and somewhere under it the city went on: the presses printing tomorrow's confident explanations, the Watch's cells holding eight men who would not talk, and, in a room he had not found yet, a man with soldier's boots keeping something old and quiet company through the night.

The thread stayed marked. He went to bed.
