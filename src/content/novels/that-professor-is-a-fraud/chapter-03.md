The interior of Alabaster Hall smelled of old paper, polished wood, and the faint ozone tang of concentrated mana. The entrance foyer was a cathedral of administrative authority — high ceilings, marble floors, and portraits of former chancellors lining the walls in chronological order, their painted eyes following visitors with the quiet judgement of people who had been important once and intended to remain so.

Every university has this hallway. Every single one. It's like they share a catalogue.

A clerk sat behind a reception desk near the far wall — a thin, precise-looking man in spectacles who radiated the energy of someone whose filing system was his religion.

"Pardon me," Lucian said. "I'm here to see the Chancellor. Lucian Caelmont."

The clerk looked up. His eyes performed the same rapid assessment Lucian was becoming accustomed to — clothes, face, mana signature — and arrived at the same conclusion everyone else had.

"Ah. Yes. You are expected." He said it the way one might say "the plumber is here." Not hostile. Just placing Lucian in the correct category. "The Chancellor's study is on the third floor. Up the central staircase, second door on the right. Do knock before entering."

"My thanks."

The staircase was wide enough for four people abreast and lined with a carpet so thick it swallowed footsteps. By the second floor, Lucian could feel a change in the ambient mana — denser, more structured, like the difference between still air and a steady breeze. This part of the building was saturated with the residual presence of powerful mages who had worked here for decades.

The higher you climb, the stronger the mana gets. Subtle. Very on-brand for an academy that's literally built on a hierarchy.

He reached the third floor, found the second door on the right — dark oak, brass handle, no nameplate because presumably everyone who mattered already knew whose door it was — and knocked.

"Enter."

The voice was measured, unhurried, and carried the particular clarity of someone who had never needed to raise it to be heard.

Lucian pushed the door open.

The Chancellor's study was not what he'd expected. He'd imagined something austere — stone walls, a severe desk, perhaps a skull or two for atmosphere. Instead, the room was warm. Bookshelves lined three walls, filled not with the imposing matched sets of academic vanity but with a chaotic mix of volumes in different sizes, colours, and states of wear. A large desk dominated the centre, buried under organised chaos — papers, crystal instruments, a half-eaten biscuit on a saucer. Afternoon light poured through a tall window behind the desk, gilding everything in amber.

And behind the desk sat Chancellor Vivienne Lysander.

She was younger than he'd expected — or rather, she looked younger. The borrowed memories placed her in her nineties, but Sixth Circle mages aged slowly, and she could have passed for a sharp-featured woman of fifty. Dark hair streaked with silver, pulled back in a style that was practical rather than decorative. Brown eyes that moved with the quick, assessing intelligence of someone who was accustomed to being the cleverest person in the room and had grown bored of it.

She was smiling.

That's not a good sign. In my experience, people in positions of authority only smile at you when they're about to make your life difficult and want you to know they're going to enjoy it.

"Professor Caelmont," she said, rising from her chair. "How good of you to arrive. Do sit down."

She gestured to a chair opposite the desk. It was comfortable — upholstered, well-cushioned, the sort of chair designed to make you feel at ease so you'd lower your guard.

Noted.

He sat.

"I trust your journey was pleasant?" She settled back into her own chair and folded her hands on the desk with the easy grace of a woman who had conducted ten thousand of these conversations.

"Quite pleasant, Chancellor. My thanks for the welcome."

"Oh, think nothing of it. We are always glad to receive new faculty. Ashveil has a long tradition of welcoming... unconventional appointments."

And there it is. First blood. 'Unconventional.' Beautifully done.

"I shall endeavour to honour that tradition," he said.

Lysander's smile widened by a fraction. She reached for a folder on her desk — thin, which probably wasn't a good sign — and opened it.

"Let me be forthright, Professor Caelmont. Your appointment was arranged through channels that are, shall we say, well-established between your family and this institution. House Caelmont helped found Ashveil Academy three centuries ago. We do not forget such things. Nor do we forget that certain... courtesies are owed." She turned a page. "Your academic record from the Academy of Veranthos is, I must say, remarkably consistent."

Consistently mediocre, she means.

"Third Circle, Peak. Graduated without distinction. No published research. No notable spell work. No commendations from faculty." She closed the folder with the delicate finality of someone closing a coffin. "You are, by every measurable standard, the least qualified professor we have appointed in the last forty years."

The silence that followed was, Lucian supposed, meant to be uncomfortable.

It was.

"I appreciate your candour, Chancellor."

"I rather thought you would. I find it saves time." She set the folder aside and leaned forward. "I shall be equally candid about your assignment. Ashveil's curriculum is broad — students study both combat and theory in their foundational years before declaring a specialisation. We refer to these as the Iron Path and the Silver Path. I assume you are familiar with the distinction?"

"Combat and research, respectively."

"Quite. You are in no condition to teach combat — even first-years would find the experience underwhelming — and the advanced theory courses require a minimum of Fifth Circle and a published body of work." She paused, letting the implications settle. "Which leaves us with somewhat limited options."

Here it comes.

"I am assigning you to teach Fundamentals of Soul Theory. First-year core curriculum — every student, regardless of where they intend to specialise. It is, as the name suggests, a foundational course. Basic principles of soul structure, mana flow, and elementary spell theory. The textbook is standardised. The curriculum has not been meaningfully updated in, oh, thirty years or so. Your predecessors in the role have generally treated it as a formality — a stepping stone to more prestigious assignments, or a place to park someone until a better use for them presents itself."

She delivered this with the serene pleasantness of a woman handing someone a shovel and informing them where to dig their own grave.

"I see," Lucian said. "And which category do I fall into?"

Lysander tilted her head. Something shifted behind her eyes — the briefest flicker of interest, quickly filed away.

"That, Professor Caelmont, is entirely up to you."

Okay. I'll give her this — she's good. That was almost a compliment wrapped inside an insult wrapped inside a dare.

"The semester commences in eleven days," she continued, her tone shifting to brisk administrative efficiency. "You will find the course materials and previous examination papers in the faculty library. Your office is in the faculty wing — Room 14, ground floor. Your quarters are in the faculty residence hall, east side of the campus. The keys and a campus guide will be provided by the clerk downstairs."

She rose, and Lucian understood that the audience was over.

He stood. "My thanks, Chancellor. I shall do my best not to—"

"Embarrass the family?" She smiled again — that same knife-in-silk smile. "Yes, I received your grandfather's letter as well. He used remarkably similar phrasing. One wonders if it's a family motto."

Bertholme, you old bastard. You wrote to her too.

"If there is nothing else, Professor?"

"Nothing at all, Chancellor. I appreciate your time."

"I rather think you'll need to appreciate your own time more, Professor Caelmont. You have eleven days and a great deal of reading to do." She held the door open for him. "Do try to stay ahead of the first-years. It would be terribly awkward otherwise."

The door closed behind him with a soft, final click.

Lucian stood in the corridor for a moment, staring at the wood grain.

She's going to be a problem. Not an enemy — not yet — but definitely a problem. She's too sharp. Too watchful. The kind of person who collects information the way other people collect stamps, and she's just started a new page with my name on it.

He exhaled slowly.

Fundamentals of Soul Theory. The most basic, boring, beneath-everyone course in the entire academy.

The corner of his mouth twitched.

She has absolutely no idea what she's just handed me.

The faculty quarters were modest. 

That was the charitable description. The less charitable one was that they were a bedroom, a washroom, and a small study crammed together with the architectural generosity of a prison cell that had been given a coat of paint and told to think positive.

But the bed was clean, the desk was solid, and there was a window that looked out over the eastern grounds — a stretch of manicured lawn sloping down toward what the campus guide identified as the lecture halls.

I've lived in worse. The postgrad housing at uni made this look like a suite at the Ritz.

Lucian set his satchel on the desk, hung his coat on the hook behind the door, and sat on the edge of the bed. The springs complained.

For the first time since waking up in the wagon, he was alone. Truly alone, with no driver to talk to, no town to navigate, no chancellor to verbally spar with. Just silence, a borrowed body, and the weight of everything that had happened settling onto his shoulders like a coat made of lead.

I died.

He let the thought sit there. He hadn't really processed it yet — there hadn't been time. But now, in the quiet, it pressed against him with a heaviness that had nothing to do with magic.

I died. I was twenty-four years old. I was six months from finishing my thesis. I had a half-eaten Tesco meal deal in the lab fridge and a library book that was three weeks overdue. And now I'm here. In someone else's body. In someone else's world.

And I can't go back.

He sat with that for a while. Not fighting it. Just... acknowledging it. The way you acknowledge a wall you've walked into. It's there. It hurts. You can't pretend it isn't.

Then he stood up, walked to the desk, and pulled the journal from his satchel.

Grieve later. Survive now.

It was something his thesis supervisor used to say, usually about deadlines rather than existential displacement, but the principle held.

He opened the journal to a blank page, picked up the pen he'd found in the desk drawer, and began to write.

Not the original Lucian's careful, defeated notes. His own.

But before his own thoughts, he flipped back through the original Lucian's entries. Not the depressing progress logs — the academic notes. Somewhere in here, buried among years of diligent, hopeless study, were the building blocks of a world he needed to understand.

He found what he was looking for near the middle of the journal. Notes from a second-year lecture on magical fauna — a table of data that the original Lucian had copied down dutifully without ever questioning it.

"Comparative Mana Regeneration Across Species (normalised per unit soul volume):"

Thornfield Rat — Soul Volume: 1 (baseline) — Regen per unit volume: 1.00
Grey Mana Fox — Soul Volume: 4 — Regen per unit volume: 0.63
Ironhide Boar — Soul Volume: 20 — Regen per unit volume: 0.34
Greater Stoneback Behemoth — Soul Volume: 200 — Regen per unit volume: 0.12

"Note: Professor Hargrieve attributes the declining regen efficiency in larger species to 'diminishing soul quality at scale.' Larger souls are inherently less efficient. No further explanation given."

Lucian read the table twice.

Then a third time.

Then he picked up the pen and started doing maths in the margin.

Hargrieve says larger souls are "less efficient." He sees the numbers dropping and calls it diminishing quality. But that's not an explanation — it's a description wearing a lab coat.

Let's actually look at this.

If regeneration scaled with volume, the regen per unit volume should be constant — 1.0 across all species. It isn't. It drops sharply as volume increases.

If regeneration scaled with soul quality, the decline should be irregular — quality is a biological variable, it would scatter messily. But this decline is clean. Too clean.

He wrote the volumes as rough radii, assuming spherical souls. Volume scales as r³. If soul volume increases by a factor of 4 (the fox), the radius increases by roughly 1.59x. Surface area scales as r², so surface area increases by roughly 2.52x. Divide by the volume increase of 4x and you get a regen-per-unit-volume of about 0.63.

That's exactly what the table says.

His hand was shaking slightly. He kept writing.

The boar. Volume 20x. Radius roughly 2.71x. Surface area roughly 7.37x. Regen per unit volume: 7.37 divided by 20 = 0.37.

The table says 0.34. Close. Not perfect — but biological systems are never perfect.

The behemoth. Volume 200x. Radius roughly 5.85x. Surface area roughly 34.2x. Regen per unit volume: 34.2 divided by 200 = 0.17.

Table says 0.12. Further off — but at this scale, the soul almost certainly isn't a smooth sphere. Larger creatures have more complex soul geometries. Folding, convolution — like how a brain has ridges to increase surface area within the skull. Some deviation is expected.

He put the pen down. His hands were suddenly very still.

It's not volume. It's not quality. It's surface area.

Every data point in this table fits a surface area curve. The smaller the creature, the higher its surface-area-to-volume ratio, the more efficiently it regenerates per unit of soul. The mages of this world looked at this data and said "bigger souls are less efficient" — and never once asked WHY.

Because the answer requires thinking about geometry. About the mathematical relationship between surface and volume. About scaling laws.

Things an engineer thinks about every single day.

His heart was pounding. Or rather, Lucian Caelmont's heart was pounding, and he was along for the ride.

Surface area. It's surface area. That's the hidden variable.

The engineering knowledge from Earth crashed into the observation like a key sliding into a lock. Heat dissipation in microprocessors — the entire reason chips ran hot was because surface area didn't scale with volume. The solution was always the same: increase effective surface area. Fins. Channels. Porous structures. Same mass, vastly more surface exposed to the environment.

And if I go the other direction — if instead of one large soul, I fragment mine into thousands, millions, billions of tiny pieces — each piece has an enormous surface-area-to-volume ratio. Like grinding a solid block into powder. Same total volume. Exponentially more surface.

Then I could increase regeneration exponentially.

And nobody in this world would see it coming, because nobody in this world has ever connected these dots. They looked at the data and said "diminishing quality." They never asked the obvious question: what if it's not quality? What if it's geometry?

He sat back, breathing hard.

Calm down. This is just a hypothesis. I don't even know if souls can be fragmented safely. I don't know if the physics translate. I don't know anything yet.

But the engineer in him was already sketching diagrams in his mind. Surface area to volume ratios. Fragmentation models. Binding mechanisms.

I need data. I need to test this. Tonight.

HYPOTHESIS: Mana regeneration is partially determined by the surface area of the soul exposed to the ambient magical field. Large magical beasts regenerate faster not because of superior soul quality, but because their souls have proportionally greater surface area due to natural convolution.

PROPOSED TEST: Use [Soul Binding] to detach a microscopic fragment of soul and bind it to a small object. Measure whether the act of separation — creating two surfaces where one existed — produces any change in ambient mana absorption rate.

He paused.

The problem is measurement. How do I measure my own mana regeneration rate with any precision? This world doesn't have oscilloscopes. Or any instruments I recognise, for that matter.

He tapped the pen against the page.

But I can feel mana. This body can feel it — that's what the First Circle awakening does, according to the borrowed memories. I can sense the ambient field. If I pay close attention, I should be able to sense whether my own absorption rate changes after fragmenting even a tiny piece.

It won't be precise. It'll be barely qualitative. But it's a start.

He set the pen down and closed his eyes.

Okay. Soul Binding. Let's see what we've got to work with.

The original Lucian had learned the basic theory of [Soul Binding] at the Academy of Veranthos. Every mage did — it was foundational, the way every physics student learns Newton's laws whether or not they ever use them. He'd never successfully cast it. His mana regeneration was too slow to practise it enough times to etch the pattern properly.

But the pattern was there. Faded, incomplete, a sketch rather than an etching — but there, buried in the soul like a watermark in old paper.

Lucian reached for it.

It was a strange sensation. Not physical, not quite mental — something in between, like flexing a muscle he'd never known he had. The pattern was simple in concept: isolate a fragment of soul, compress it, and press it into an object. The original Lucian had understood the theory perfectly. He'd simply never had the mana to practice.

Let's see if I can manage even a partial activation.

He picked up the pen from the desk. A simple object. Nothing precious.

Then he turned his attention inward, to the dim, flickering presence that was his soul.

It was... small. He could feel that immediately. Not just damaged — diminished. Like a campfire that had been burning too long without fuel, reduced to embers and ash. The borrowed memories had told him he was weak. Feeling it was something else entirely.

Right. Not a lot to work with. Let's be very, very careful.

He focused on the outermost edge of his soul — the thinnest, most peripheral layer — and tried to pinch off a fragment so small it was barely a sliver. Not a chunk. Not even a grain. A speck. A mote of soul-stuff that he could afford to lose.

The pain was immediate and sharp, like pressing a thumb into a bruise.

He pressed harder.

Something gave. A tiny piece of his soul separated from the whole — so small that the loss was negligible, like removing a single grain of sand from a beach. He could barely perceive it, a faint mote of warmth floating free in his consciousness.

Now bind it.

He pressed the fragment toward the pen in his hand. The soul-mote resisted at first, then settled, sinking into the wood and metal like water into dry earth.

The pen shivered. Not visibly — not to the eye — but to his soul-sense, it was suddenly there. Present. Connected. A tiny extension of himself, no larger than a whisper, but unmistakably real.

Holy—

He sat very still, holding the pen, and turned his attention to his mana regeneration.

It was subtle. So subtle he almost missed it. But there — at the very edge of perception, like hearing a single extra instrument join an orchestra mid-performance — his mana absorption had ticked up. Not dramatically. Not transformatively. By a margin so small that any other mage would have dismissed it as imagination.

But Lucian didn't dismiss it.

Because he knew exactly what it meant.

Two surfaces. Where there was one continuous soul, there are now two separate pieces — the main soul and the bound fragment. Two surfaces exposed to the ambient field. And the absorption increased.

It's real. The surface area hypothesis is real.

His hands were shaking. He set the pen down carefully, as though it were made of glass.

If a single speck produces a measurable increase... what happens with a thousand specks? A million? A billion?

What happens if I bind fragments to something so small they can't be destroyed? Something at the atomic level?

He stared at the pen on the desk. Such a small thing. Such an ordinary thing.

I need more data. I need to understand [Soul Binding] properly, not just the sketch in this body's memory. I need the theory, the history, the edge cases.

He looked out the window toward the lecture halls, dark and quiet in the evening gloom.

And in eleven days, I'll be teaching the foundational course that covers exactly that.

For the first time since waking up in a dead man's body, Lucian Caelmont smiled.
