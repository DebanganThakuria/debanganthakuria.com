The town of Ashveil looked different when you weren't arriving in a state of existential crisis.

Lucian walked through the main gate — the same gate he'd entered two weeks ago in a carriage, confused and half-panicking in a stolen body — and this time, actually looked. Not with the glazed survival-mode attention of his first visit. With ten threads. With an engineer's eyes.

The central avenue was wider than he'd first appreciated. Cobblestone, well-laid, with gutters cut into both sides that channelled rainwater and — he noticed — faint traces of mana runoff from the academy hilltop above. The buildings lining the avenue were a mix of old stone and newer timber-frame construction, three to four storeys, with shops at ground level and residences above. Signage was hand-painted, some illuminated by mana lamps — a luxury that signalled the shopkeeper could afford a mage's services, or was one themselves.

The market square was busier than on his arrival day. It was a proper market morning — stalls arranged in concentric rings around the central fountain, merchants calling prices, customers haggling with the practised intensity of people for whom every Iron Mark mattered.

Late medieval. That's the closest analogue. Maybe early Renaissance in the cities. They've got printing — I saw a broadsheet vendor near the academy gate. They've got glass windows in the wealthier buildings. They've got sophisticated metallurgy — the ironwork on that balcony is better than anything fifteenth-century Europe produced. But no steam power. No electricity. No internal combustion.

Because they have mana instead.

He paused near a shopfront that caught his attention. A workshop, not a retail store — the sign read Gorvain & Sons, with a smaller line underneath: Mana-crystal installation. Structural reinforcement. Experimental commissions welcome.

Through the open door, he could see a workbench covered in crystal arrays, wire frames, and what appeared to be a half-assembled mana lamp of unusual design. Two men — a grey-haired mage and a younger apprentice — were arguing over a schematic pinned to the wall.

Magic engineering. Out here in a provincial town, not just the capital. The tradesman's version — not theorists, but people who build things that work.

One thread filed the location away. He'd be back.

The town's infrastructure told a story if you knew how to read it. The main avenue was well-maintained — imperial funding, probably, channelled through the provincial authority. But the side streets degraded quickly. Cobblestones gave way to packed dirt within two blocks. The mana-crystal street lamps appeared on the central avenue and the market square; everywhere else, it was iron brackets for oil lanterns, many of them empty or broken.

Veranthos Province is declining. You can see it in the streets. The core looks fine — but the edges are fraying. Fewer Marches, less fragment income, less imperial investment, less maintenance. And the people who live on those side streets feel it every day.

He passed a canal that cut through the eastern quarter — a narrow waterway with stone banks and a series of locks that controlled the water level. Two of the locks had magical mechanisms — glowing crystals embedded in the ironwork that regulated water flow without manual intervention. A third lock was broken, its crystal dark, and a team of civilian workers was manually operating the gates with ropes and pulleys while a bored-looking Second Circle mage supervised.

Three locks. Two working on magic, one on muscle. That's the state of the empire in miniature.

Millhaven Farm was a forty-minute walk southeast of town, along a rutted cart track that wound through fields of something grain-like that Lucian's borrowed memories identified as "southern wheat" — a staple crop that grew well in mana-poor soil.

The farm itself was modest. A stone farmhouse, a wooden barn, a cluster of outbuildings, and fenced fields stretching toward a tree line that marked the edge of uncultivated woodland. The farmer — a stocky, weathered man named Garrett Thorne, no relation to Lucian's carriage driver — met him at the gate with the cautious hospitality of someone who'd paid good money for help and was reserving judgement on whether he'd get his money's worth.

"Professor, is it?" Thorne looked him up and down. The glasses. The thin frame. The faculty coat that was already collecting dust from the cart track. "From the academy?"

"Professor Caelmont. You reported mana-beast activity near your crops?"

"Aye. Three nights running now. Something's been at the wheat in the eastern field. Not eating it — spoiling it. The grain goes black where they've been. Touched by mana corruption, the wife says. She's got a sense for these things."

"May I see the affected area?"

Thorne led him along the fence line to the eastern field. The damage was obvious — a ragged patch of blackened crop, maybe ten metres across, where the wheat had withered and turned the colour of charcoal. The soil beneath was discoloured too, and when Lucian knelt and extended his mana-sense, he could feel it: a residue. Not natural mana — something fouled. Corrupted.

Mana-beast marking. They've been feeding on the ambient mana in the soil and leaving waste product behind. Like animals fouling a water source.

Four threads analysed the residue pattern while two more mapped the surrounding mana field for anomalies.

"How many creatures have you seen, farmer Garrett?"

"The wife saw two. Small things — fox-sized, she said. Moved fast. Dark fur, eyes that glowed. I didn't get a proper look myself. I'm no mage, Professor. I know when to stay indoors."

Corrupted mana-foxes. Two confirmed, possibly more. They're feeding on the ley-line seepage from the soil — this area probably sits on a minor mana vein that feeds the crops. The foxes are drawn to it, feeding, and their corrupted mana is poisoning the ground.

"The good news," Lucian said, straightening up, "is that this is a containment problem, not an infestation. They're feeding on a specific mana source in your soil. Remove the foxes, cleanse the residue, and the crop should recover within a few weeks."

Thorne's shoulders relaxed fractionally. "And the bad news?"

"They'll likely return tonight. Corrupted mana-beasts are creatures of habit. I'd like your permission to wait for them."

"You're going to — what, sit in my field all night?"

"Something like that."

Thorne looked at him — this thin, bespectacled young professor who'd walked forty minutes from town to look at dead wheat — and made the calculation that farmers had been making about hired help since the beginning of agriculture: he's here, he's paid for, might as well let him try.

"There's a spot by the eastern fence with good sight lines. I'll have the wife bring you a blanket."

"That won't be necessary. But I wouldn't turn down a cup of tea, if it's not too much trouble."

Thorne almost smiled. Almost.

Night on the farm was quiet in a way that campus never was.

No ambient hum of dozens of mage signatures. No faint vibration of centuries-old enchantments. Just wind through wheat, distant insects, and the enormous sky overhead — more stars than Lucian had ever seen, in this life or the last. The light pollution of cities and academies didn't reach out here. The Milky Way — or whatever this world called its galaxy — blazed across the darkness like a river of crushed silver.

I've never seen this sky. Not here, not on Earth. My old life was labs and libraries and the orange glow of streetlights. This is what the universe actually looks like when you turn the lights off.

I should feel awed. I think I'm supposed to feel awed.

I feel... observational.

He noted the emotional absence the way a doctor notes a symptom. Clinically. Without distress. Which was itself a symptom.

He sat cross-legged by the eastern fence, six threads running a passive sensor net across the surrounding mana field while the remaining four worked on unrelated problems. His spell library was ready: [Fireball], [Force Bolt], [Barrier Shell], [Mana Pulse], [Wind Step], and [Binding Thread]. Five combat spells, all etched to working competency over the past two weeks of nightly practice. Less than any Third Circle mage would have. The previous Lucian was remarkably mediocre. But the best part was what should have taken years had taken days for the new Lucian to learn.

My first real fight. Training exercises in my quarters don't count. This is the test — live targets, unknown variables, consequences if I fail.

I'm not nervous.

That should probably concern me more than it does.

At twenty past midnight, his sensor net flagged movement.

Three signatures, not two. Small, fast, corrupted — mana patterns that flickered and pulsed irregularly, like a heartbeat with arrhythmia. They were approaching from the tree line, moving low through the thornwheat with the practised stealth of predators who'd made this journey before.

Three. The wife only saw two. There's a third — smaller, possibly juvenile. Family unit.

He stood. No hurry. No tension. Just a man getting to his feet because there was work to do.

The first fox broke from the wheat line fifteen metres from his position. It was larger than he'd expected — not house-fox sized but more like a large dog, with matted dark fur that seemed to absorb light and eyes that glowed a sickly amber. Its mana signature was a tangle of corruption — natural beast-mana curdled by something, twisted into patterns that set Lucian's soul-sense on edge.

Corrupted, not possessed. They're still animals — just animals whose soul has gone wrong. Driven by instinct, not intelligence.

The second appeared ten metres to the left. The third — smaller, definitely juvenile — hung back at the tree line.

The first fox spotted him. Its amber eyes locked on. A growl — low, resonant, carrying a subsonic mana-vibration that was designed to trigger a prey response in anything with a soul.

Ah. Fear projection. A corrupted variant of natural beast intimidation. Interesting.

He felt the fear-pulse wash over him. Seven threads noted it, analysed its frequency, and filed it for later study. The eighth thread, the one running his emotional responses, registered it as... mildly interesting.

That's supposed to make me run. Instead, I want to take it apart and see how it works.

Definitely a soul-corruption symptom.

The fox charged.

Lucian activated [Wind Step] — a burst of wind below his feet movement that carried him three metres to the right in a fraction of a second. The fox sailed through the space he'd occupied and hit the fence post with a crack of splintering wood.

Fast. Stronger than expected. The corruption enhances their physical capabilities at the cost of stability.

He raised his right hand. Two threads managed targeting — one tracking the first fox as it recovered, one locking onto the second as it circled left.

[Force Bolt]. [Force Bolt]. [Force Bolt].

Three shots in rapid succession, each modified mid-flight by real-time thread analysis of the targets' movement patterns. The first bolt caught the lead fox in the shoulder as it turned, spinning it sideways. The second hit the flanking fox dead centre, lifting it off its feet and sending it tumbling through the wheat. The third — a lower-powered shot, deliberately restrained — struck the ground in front of the juvenile, sending up a spray of dirt and mana-sparks that sent it yelping back toward the tree line.

The juvenile isn't corrupted yet. Just scared. It'll flee if I give it the option.

The lead fox was getting up. Tougher than a single [Force Bolt] warranted. Lucian adjusted his approach — three threads now dedicated to combat analysis, reading the creature's movement patterns, mana flow, and structural weak points.

The corruption concentrates in the chest cavity, around what would be the beast's mana core. If I disrupt that—

[Mana Pulse]. Not the detection variant he'd been using passively all night. The offensive application — a targeted burst of destabilising mana aimed directly at the fox's corrupted core.

The fox staggered. Its amber eyes flickered. The corruption patterns in its signature spasmed, lost coherence for a moment, and the beast whimpered — a sound that was suddenly, painfully, just an animal in distress.

The corruption isn't inherent. It's an overlay. Something external corrupted their mana. If I strip the corruption, there might be a normal animal underneath.

That's... not what I expected.

But there wasn't time for that investigation tonight. The second fox was recovering, snarling, preparing to charge.

[Force Bolt]. [Force Bolt]. [Binding Thread].

Two bolts to stun. One thread of mana-hardened binding to wrap around the beast's legs and pin it to the ground. It thrashed, snapped, and gradually stilled as the binding held.

The lead fox, still destabilised from the [Mana Pulse], made a decision. It turned and ran — back toward the tree line, following the juvenile, disappearing into the darkness with a final amber flicker of corrupted eyes.

One fled with the juvenile. One captured. Not bad for a first outing.

Lucian stood in the ruined wheat field, breathing evenly. Not winded. Not shaking. His mana was already full — it had been full throughout the fight. The total expenditure of the engagement had been maybe fifteen seconds of moderate casting, and his regeneration had replaced every drop before the last bolt landed.

Infinite regen means I never run dry. In a sustained fight, I simply don't stop. The only limitation is my spell repertoire and my tactical creativity.

I need more spells. And I need harder opponents.

He looked down at the bound fox. It was still alive, still breathing, the corruption swirling in its mana like oil in water.

Something corrupted these animals. Not a natural process — the [Mana Pulse] disrupted it too easily. An overlay, not an integration. Someone — or something — did this to them.

That's not in the case description. The farmer thinks this is simple pest control. It might be. Or it might be the edge of something larger.

He knelt beside the bound fox and, very carefully, began to examine the corruption pattern with all ten threads focused.

First case. First questions. Let's see where this goes.

---

He returned to the farmhouse at dawn. The bound fox was sedated — a minor application of [Mana Pulse] tuned to slow its nervous system — and wrapped in a canvas tarp Thorne had provided.

"Three of them," Lucian reported. "One captured for study. One fled with a juvenile. Your eastern field should recover once the residue is cleansed — I've done what I can tonight, but you may want to hire an alchemist to finish the job. The soil contamination goes deeper than surface treatment can reach."

Thorne looked at the bundled fox, then at Lucian, then at the bundled fox again.

"You handled three by yourself. At night."

"It was within my capabilities."

For a Third Circle professor doing pest control on his evening off. Nothing unusual here.

Thorne reached into his coat and produced a small leather pouch. Two Gold Sovereigns, counted out and handed over with the gravity of a man parting with serious money.

"My thanks, Professor. If they come back—"

"They won't. But if anything else unusual occurs — anything at all — send word to the academy. Ask for me specifically."

He tucked the coins into his coat — next to his monthly salary of eight, this was a quarter of his income in a single night — and walked back toward town with a sedated mana-fox under one arm and new questions turning in his mind.
