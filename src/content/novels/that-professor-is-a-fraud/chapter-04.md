From the diary of Miss Penelope Holloway, 20th of October:

Today I visited a police station. Mother believes I was at choir. The constables work terribly hard and are brought nothing nice, ever, which I consider a scandal worse than most of the ones they investigate, so I have taken steps. Mottram's. The lemon ones. Sergeant Bell has four grandchildren and the youngest bites. The Professor was downstairs the whole time doing something important with a box.

---

The runner from the Watch arrived on Monday morning — a boy of twenty in a caped greatcoat a size too big, boots polished, whistle-chain bright — and with him, folded small, a note in the headmistress's hand.

The note said: Mr. Caelmont has time. — L.A.

That was the whole of it. Lucian read it twice, then went to fetch his coat. A consulting petition from the Metropolitan Watch, routed through the College, passed downward until it reached the one man who could not refuse it. He was going to spend his morning in a police station.

He was three steps down the stair when a voice above him said, "Where are you going?"

Penny Holloway stood at the landing rail in her College coat, books strapped, ribbons in order.

"The Embankment station house," he said — the truth, because he wanted to see what she would do with it.

She clapped once, handed her books to the porter for safekeeping, and fell in beside the Watch runner, asking him friendly questions about his work. At no point did she ask whether she could come. Lucian went back over the conversation afterward and could not find the moment where refusing her had been possible. She had never asked a question that allowed the answer no.

The runner, twenty years old and outmatched, kept glancing at Lucian for rescue. None came.

She made them stop at Mottram's on the corner of Theobald Lane. Warm air came out of the shop door; trays of lemon biscuits sat in the bow window, and the counter-girl weighed them out on a brass scale and tied the sack with string.

"Policemen are never brought anything nice," Penny explained, watching the scale. "My uncle was a magistrate. He said the whole system runs on tea and resentment, and half of that can be improved."

---

The Embankment station house wore the Watch's lantern over its door — copper-cased, amber glass, the crown-and-key stamped in the casing. Inside there was a counter worn pale at the elbows, gas brackets burning yellow along the walls, a coal stove with a kettle riding its edge, and a case board running the width of the back wall: two dozen entries in chalk. A burglary in Fenchurch Stair. A missing barge-hand. A pawnshop dispute involving a cursed clock. The eleven backward clocks of Cooper's Row, with a question mark after them.

Two dozen pieces of the city's business. Not one of them was his.

Penny looked the room over for four seconds, then walked toward the desk sergeant — a heavy man with a walrus moustache and spectacles on a chain — with the sack held out in front of her.

Inspector Hollis received Lucian in a back office with two chairs and no fire. He was a squarish man of about fifty, grey-eyed, in a coat that had seen thirty years of rain, and his boots had been resoled at least twice. A short briar pipe sat on the desk. Through the whole interview he filled it and tamped it and never once lit it. He looked at Lucian without much hope.

"Mr. Caelmont. The College says you're what they could spare."

"The College is being generous. I'm what they couldn't use."

Something moved at the corner of the inspector's mouth and was gone. He picked up a folder. "You'll know the Meridian business from the papers."

"I know what the papers printed."

Hollis's eyes came up, brief and level. "Mm," he said, and opened the folder. "Eight men in the cells downstairs. Not one of them has said a word in four days. No name, no lawyer, no complaint about the food. Men who don't complain about the food are being paid better than I am. We have their device in the evidence cellar, and my people can't read it. The men who could read it are at the College, and the College sent you." He stood. "I'm told you study souls."

"Damaged ones."

"Then you'll manage here," said Hollis, and took up the lamp.

---

The evidence cellar was brick, barrel-vaulted, whitewash flaking off the courses, cold enough to show breath. A single lamp hung from a hook by the door. The device sat on trestles in the middle of it, tagged and roped: the travelling-trunk, lid open, its dead lattice-work exposed. On the shelves around it, the rest of the haul — eight service wands, eight shoulder rigs, a brass coin in a jar, the satchel of pamphlets.

Lucian's problem was simple, and he had settled it on the walk over.

His cloud could read the device completely in about a minute. But a probationary Circle 4 researcher could not know things in a minute. So he asked for the station's instruments — resonance rods, a reader's lens, all Watch-issue and older than the runner — and did the examination twice at the same time: once with the cloud, invisibly, for himself, and once with the rods, slowly and aloud, for the notebook. Every finding he announced would have an instrument to explain it. That was the whole trick of being a consulting scholar in his position: never show a conclusion without showing the ladder that a normal man would have climbed to reach it.

The first hour produced three findings for the Watch.

Finding one: the bomb had an appointment. He held a rod against the dead timing-coil until it chimed out its stored count, and did the arithmetic aloud for Hollis. The charge had been set to fire at ten minutes past one in the morning. The symposium's closing toast had been called for one o'clock. At ten past, every guest would have been in the ballroom, and every servant would have come up from below to pour the wine.

"Every soul in the building in one room," Hollis said. "Three floors of stone between them and the street."

"Yes. This device was never a threat or a demonstration. It was built to kill everyone in the Grand Meridian and to be blamed on the pamphlets afterward. The pamphlets are printed on blast-rated satchel paper, Inspector. They were meant to be found in the ashes."

Hollis stared at the trunk, tamping the unlit pipe with his thumb, and worked it through. "The papers have it as an outrage foiled. A grievance with a fuse on it."

"The grievance is a costume. Look at the money instead. This spell-work is layered — a charge inside a resonance inside a timing-lattice. That is months of building by a trained maker, and trained makers charge accordingly. Provincial radicals do not have this kind of gold. Someone who does have it paid for a massacre and printed someone else's confession in advance."

"I've thought most of that for four days," Hollis said, "and kept it off the board upstairs, because my superiors like the provincial story. It closes." He wrote in a slow square hand. "Now the College agrees with me. That's either very good or very bad for my career."

Finding two: the prisoners were a unit. Lucian laid the eight wands and eight shoulder rigs side by side and showed him the plain fact: identical kits, and identical etchings — the same spells, etched in the same order, in the same style. "Etching style is handwriting, Inspector. Eight men with the same handwriting means one instructor, drilling them together for years. You are not holding eight angry provincials who found a cause. You are holding somebody's private soldiers. Find who has been paying and feeding a drilled company of ex-army men, and you find your employer."

Hollis wrote that down too. "And they don't talk because the employer pays their families."

"That would be my guess."

Finding three came at the end, and it was the strangest, and Lucian staged its discovery carefully — because he had missed it himself on the night, and had come here intending to fix that.

He began with the recovered blast-material: four large evidence crates of it, lined against the wall. Then he measured the trunk, outside and in, with the Watch's own tape, and made Hollis hold one end.

The arithmetic did not work. The four crates of material had all come out of the trunk. The trunk, measured honestly, could not have held two of them.

"Measure it yourself," Lucian said. "Outside: three feet by two by two. Now the inside." He ran the tape along the trunk's inner wall. "Three feet and eleven inches. The trunk is bigger inside than outside."

Hollis looked at the tape for a long moment. "That's not possible."

"It is not possible with any magic taught in this kingdom. But it is what the tape says, and it is how a trunk this size carried enough material to bring down the hotel." He pointed to the lining with a rod. "There is a dormant spell-lattice worked into the casing's inner face. I can detect that it exists — the rods show it. I cannot read what it does or how. Space-folding is a theoretical topic; there are perhaps three serious papers on it, and no living caster I know of. Somewhere, someone can actually do it — a specialist. The bomb-builder bought this trunk from that specialist. That is a second tradesman for your file, Inspector: identity unknown, craft supposedly impossible."

All of that was true, and all of it was arranged. What Lucian did not say was that his cloud had already read the dormant lattice from anchor to anchor — every line, every joint of the pattern that told space to be larger than its container — and that he had memorized the whole of it, twice over, on the two threads he reserved for things he could not afford to lose.

The Watch was leaving this cellar with a mystery. Lucian was leaving it with a blueprint.

"The College man says the box is bigger inside than outside," Hollis said, writing, "and would like that minuted." He finished the line. "Twenty-nine years I stayed off this station's cellar stairs, Mr. Caelmont. I knew there would be a reason."

---

The inspector walked him up through the public room. At the counter stood three constables, the desk sergeant, and a prisoner's escort, all within reach of an empty Mottram's sack, and Penny Holloway in the middle of a story, conducting it with a teaspoon.

Hollis considered the scene. "Yours?"

"Her own entirely."

At the door, the inspector took a paper in an evidence sleeve from his breast pocket. Lucian recognised his own block capitals through the glassine — THE COAL STORE OF THE GRAND MERIDIAN WILL REPAY INSPECTION BEFORE MORNING — and gave it the polite attention of a man seeing it for the first time.

"Came through the letter-grate downstairs, the night of the symposium," Hollis said. "Whoever wrote it knew the coal store repaid inspection before we did. Knew it early enough to say bring wagons." His grey eyes rested on Lucian a moment. "What sort of person knows a thing like that at four in the morning, and tells us sideways?"

"A cautious one, I should think."

"Mm," said Inspector Hollis. "Those are the ones I retire without meeting." He put the sleeve away. "The Watch thanks the College, Mr. Caelmont. I expect we'll petition again. The city's generous that way."

---

Fog came up from the river with the evening and hid the quadrangle.

Lucian sat at his desk with the lamp low and counted what the morning had actually paid him. The Watch's money order lay on the blotter — one guinea's worth of marks, his first consulting fee. That was the smallest item on the list.

One: confirmation. The Meridian attack was bought by someone wealthy, executed by a private company of trained men, and dressed as provincial terror. He had believed it since that night; now the evidence cellar agreed with him, and — more useful — so did Inspector Hollis.

Two: a lead he could not have found alone. The crew's identical etchings were traceable. Drilled companies left records — army rolls, pension payments, someone's ledger somewhere. The Watch would dig at that, because he had shown them where to dig.

Three: a second craftsman in the story. The bomb-builder had bought a spatial fold from a specialist whose craft was supposed not to exist. Somewhere in this city or beyond it worked a person who could fold space for money. Finding the specialist meant finding their client list. He intended, eventually, to find the specialist.

Four — and this one belonged to him alone: the lattice itself, complete, memorized. Space magic. A branch of magic with three theoretical papers and no known casters, and he was now holding a finished, working example of it in his head, the same as holding a solved equation and working backward to the method. It would take him a few weeks to decode — the lattice used forms he had never seen, and rushing strange spellwork was how researchers ended up as his case studies. But at the end of those weeks he would be able to build what no one else in the kingdom could build. A hidden fold to carry things past any search. In time, perhaps barriers made of folded space itself. An entire school of magic, his alone, bought for one guinea and a morning's acting.

He wrote nothing down. He gave the memorized lattice four threads and the rest of the night, and began.

And tomorrow at four o'clock: Clara Vance, whom four tutors had drilled and none had mapped, and five questions waiting on a clean sheet.

He turned down the lamp.
