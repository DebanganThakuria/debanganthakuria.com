Day 1.

The faculty library opened at dawn, and Lucian was waiting at the door.

The librarian — a wiry, humourless man named Craith who looked as though smiling might physically injure him — regarded him with the particular suspicion reserved for people who arrive before the doors open.

"Professor Caelmont. You are early."

"I have a great deal of reading to do."

Craith's expression suggested he doubted this very much but was professionally obligated not to say so. He unlocked the door and gestured Lucian through.

The faculty library was not what Lucian had expected. It was better. Much better.

Three floors of shelves arranged in concentric rings around a central atrium, the ceiling open to a crystalline skylight that bathed everything in soft, even light. The collection was enormous — thousands of volumes covering every branch of magical theory the empire had ever formalised. And unlike the public libraries he'd glimpsed in the original Lucian's memories, these shelves included restricted sections accessible only to faculty.

I'm faculty. That little folder of mediocrity Lysander waved at me came with one very significant perk: access.

He started with the fundamentals. Not the first-year textbook he'd been assigned to teach — he'd get to that — but the primary sources it was based on. The original treatises on soul structure. The foundational papers on mana flow theory. The first documented studies of Soul Binding.

By midday, he'd filled half a notebook with observations, questions, and cross-references.

By evening, he'd finished six volumes and started a seventh.

The soul theory in this world is remarkably sophisticated in some areas and shockingly blind in others. They've mapped soul density gradients to four decimal places. They've catalogued over three hundred distinct spell-etching patterns. They've got empirical data on mana regeneration rates across thousands of subjects.

But they've never once questioned why regeneration varies independently of the two factors they've identified. They see the variance in their own data and attribute it to "individual variation" or "measurement error."

It's right there. In their own numbers. And nobody's looked.

---

Day 3.

He found the book on [Parallel Thoughts].

It was in the restricted section — a slim, ancient volume bound in leather so old it had gone almost black. The spell had been recovered from a Spirit March nearly four centuries ago, and the text was a reconstruction from a fragmented Spell Fragment, annotated by generations of scholars.

[Parallel Thoughts] allowed a mage to split off a portion of their soul to operate as an independent thinking process while remaining coordinated with the main consciousness. It was classified as a Sixth Circle technique — not because it required enormous mana, but because the soul manipulation involved was considered too delicate for anyone below that level.

Too delicate for anyone below Sixth Circle. Right.

Lucian read the spell description three times.

The principle was straightforward, at least to someone who'd spent four years studying computer architecture. It was multithreading. That's all it was. The soul was the processor, and [Parallel Thoughts] spawned a new thread — an independent execution context that could process information in parallel with the main thread while sharing the same memory space.

The implementation was the hard part. You had to partition a section of your soul without severing it entirely — a controlled split, not a fragmentation. The split portion had to maintain coherence with the main soul while operating independently.

But I've already done something harder than this. I've severed a fragment entirely and bound it to an external object. Compared to that, maintaining a connected split should be—

He stopped himself.

Should be what? Easy? Don't get cocky. You've done one experiment with a pen. One.

But the engineer in him was already running the numbers.

His mana regeneration, even with just the single pen-bound fragment, had noticeably increased. Not dramatically — but enough that he could practise spells more frequently than the original Lucian ever could. And [Parallel Thoughts] didn't require massive mana to cast. It required precision and repeated practice.

Repeated practice was exactly what he now had the mana budget for.

He checked out the book and took it back to his quarters.

---

Day 4.

He failed to cast [Parallel Thoughts] forty-seven times.

Day 5.

Sixty-three failures. But on attempt sixty-four, something happened. A flicker. A momentary sensation of his awareness splitting — like briefly seeing double, but with thoughts instead of vision. It lasted less than a second before collapsing.

That's not failure. That's a proof of concept.

Day 6.

He held the split for three seconds. Then five. Then, just before midnight, eleven seconds of genuine parallel cognition — two independent thought processes running simultaneously, coordinated but distinct.

It was, without exaggeration, the strangest thing he had ever experienced. Including dying and waking up in another world.

It's like having two browser tabs open in your brain. Both loading, both active, but you can switch focus between them without losing the other.

He collapsed on the bed and laughed at the ceiling.

Two hundred attempts in three days. A Sixth Circle technique. If anyone saw my practice logs, they'd think I'd fabricated them.

But nobody's going to see them, are they?

---

Day 7.

With [Parallel Thoughts] stabilising, Lucian turned back to his real project.

He'd spent every available hour in the library between practice sessions, working through the complete academic literature on Soul Binding. He now understood the spell at a level that would have made his Veranthos professors weep — not because he was smarter than them, but because he could read faster, process information in parallel, and cross-reference across disciplines in ways that a single-threaded mind simply couldn't.

The picture was clear.

Soul Binding worked by compressing a fragment of soul and fusing it with the material structure of an object. The fragment maintained a quantum-like connection to the main soul — not through any physical medium, but through what the texts called "soul resonance." The bound fragment and the main soul vibrated at the same frequency, allowing communication and control across any distance.

Entanglement. They're describing quantum entanglement and they don't even know it.

The key insight was this: the size of the fragment didn't matter for the binding itself. The spell worked equally well with a large chunk or a microscopic speck. The texts noted this as a curiosity — why waste more soul than necessary? — but nobody had explored the implications of going in the opposite direction. Of going impossibly small.

Because nobody had a reason to. Why would you bind a fragment so small it's functionally useless for sensing or control?

Unless the binding itself — the act of creating new surface area — was the point.

He tested it that evening. Bound a second fragment to a pebble from the courtyard. Then a third to a splinter of wood. Then a fourth to a grain of sand he'd plucked from the path outside.

Each time, the same result. A tiny, measurable tick upward in mana absorption. Four fragments now, four additional surfaces, and the cumulative effect was unmistakable — his regeneration had roughly quadrupled from its original pitiful baseline.

It scales. Every fragment adds surface area, and every surface adds regeneration. The relationship is consistent.

But grains of sand can be destroyed. Pebbles can be crushed. Anything large enough to see is large enough to break — and when a bound object is destroyed, the soul fragment is lost with it.

I need something indestructible. Something so small that destroying it would require more energy than any mage could produce.

He needed atoms.

---

Day 8.

The problem was generating individual atoms to bind to.

In his old world, this was trivial. Electrolysis — pass current through water, split it into hydrogen and oxygen. Basic chemistry. But this world didn't have electrical circuits, batteries, or any concept of electric current.

It did, however, have mana.

Mana was energy. The texts were explicit about this — mana could be converted into heat, light, kinetic force, and various other forms through spell work. If he could design a spell that mimicked the effect of an electrical current — applying directed energy to break molecular bonds in water — he could produce hydrogen atoms.

I am going to build an electrolysis spell from first principles using magic.

My thesis supervisor would either be incredibly proud or have me sectioned.

It took him two days.

The spell itself was crude — a modification of a basic heating spell, restructured to deliver energy in a controlled, directional pulse rather than as diffuse heat. He practised on cups of water in his quarters, adjusting the mana flow pattern until he could feel the molecular bonds beginning to separate.

On the evening of Day 9, he held his hand over a cup of water and felt the faintest whisper of free hydrogen atoms rising from the surface.

Hello, little ones.

---

Day 9, late evening.

Lucian sat at his desk with his hand hovering over a cup of water, his soul-sense extended to its limits.

He could feel them — hydrogen atoms, impossibly small, freed from their molecular bonds by his makeshift electrolysis spell. They existed for fractions of a second before recombining or dispersing. He had a window of moments to work with.

This is either going to be the most important thing anyone in this world has ever done, or I'm about to shred what's left of my soul trying to bind fragments to things I can barely perceive.

Only one way to find out.

He activated [Soul Binding]. Not the tentative, fumbling version he'd used on the pen. He'd practised this spell hundreds of times over the past week, etching it deeper into his soul with every repetition. It was becoming instinctive — fast, precise, efficient.

He pinched off a fragment. Smaller than the pen-fragment. Smaller than anything the textbooks described as possible. A mote of soul so minuscule it was barely a concept, let alone a thing.

And he pressed it into a hydrogen atom.

The fragment vanished from his perception. Not gone — he could still feel it, distantly, through soul resonance — but so small it was like trying to hear a single voice in a stadium.

One down.

He did it again.

And again.

Each binding was faster than the last. The spell pattern was etched deep enough now that it fired almost on reflex — pinch, compress, bind. Pinch, compress, bind. A rhythm. A production line.

With [Parallel Thoughts] active, he ran two processes simultaneously: one managing the electrolysis spell, keeping the hydrogen flowing, and one handling the binding operations in rapid sequence.

After an hour, he'd bound somewhere between one and two thousand fragments.

After two hours, the number was in the tens of thousands.

He couldn't count them precisely. They were too small, too numerous, too far below the threshold of individual perception. But he could feel the collective effect — a cloud of soul-bound atoms hovering around him, invisible, imperceptible to anyone else, each one a microscopic surface exposed to the ambient mana field.

And the regeneration.

Oh.

It was like someone had opened a floodgate.

Mana poured into him — not in the thin, reluctant trickle he'd been living with, but in a steady, rushing current that filled his soul's capacity in seconds and kept pressing against the walls. He had to actively let the excess dissipate because his soul's density was too low to hold it all.

I'm full. My mana capacity is completely full. It took... seconds. It took actual seconds.

A Third Circle mage with the regeneration rate of a... what? Fifth Circle? Sixth? I don't even know where this falls on the scale. The scale might not go this high.

His hands were trembling. Not from exhaustion — from adrenaline. From the sheer, overwhelming reality of what he'd just done.

I've been in this world for nine days. And I've just broken it.

He sat back in his chair and stared at the ceiling.

The cloud of soul-bound hydrogen atoms hung around him like an invisible cloak. He couldn't see it, couldn't hear it, but he could feel it — a constant, humming presence, like standing next to a power line. Tens of thousands of microscopic soul fragments, each one drawing mana from the ambient field, each one a surface that shouldn't exist.

And this was just the beginning. He could go further. Bind more. Every session would add thousands more, and the regeneration would keep climbing.

Atomic Soul.

The name came to him unbidden, and it was right.

[Atomic Soul].

---

Day 10.

He barely slept. Three hours, maybe four — but it didn't matter. The adrenaline of what he'd achieved and the sheer efficiency of [Parallel Thoughts] meant his waking hours were worth triple what they'd been before. One thread processed information while the other managed his body's mundane needs. It wasn't sustainable long-term, but for one night, it was enough.

He spent the early hours binding more fragments. Tens of thousands more. The process was mechanical now — electrolysis, pinch, bind, repeat — and with each session the cloud thickened, the regeneration climbed, and his capacity filled faster and faster.

He could feel his soul density shifting. Not dramatically — not yet. The constant saturation of mana was doing what years of cultivation hadn't: slowly, steadily compressing his soul. Like water wearing away stone. He wasn't anywhere near Fourth Circle — that threshold required a qualitative transformation, not just incremental growth — but the direction was unmistakable. What should have taken years was happening in days.

At this rate... months, maybe. Not days. The density has to reach a critical point before the soul tempers, and there's no shortcut for that. But months instead of decades? I'll take it.

The real advantage wasn't the density gain. It was everything else.

He spent the rest of the morning in the library.

With [Parallel Thoughts] running, reading was no longer a sequential process. One thread read the page. The other analysed, cross-referenced, and filed the information. He could process a dense academic text in a quarter of the time it would take a normal reader, and retain it with near-perfect accuracy.

By dawn, he'd consumed the entire first-year curriculum for Fundamentals of Soul Theory.

By noon, he'd moved on to the second-year materials.

By evening, he was reading fourth-year texts and understanding them.

The past examination papers Lysander mentioned — I could sit the final exam for every course in this academy right now and pass. Not because I'm a genius. Because I have unlimited mana to practise with, parallel processing to study with, and an engineer's instinct for systems.

Lysander told me to stay ahead of the first-years. She has no idea.

Day 11.

The morning before the semester began.

Lucian stood at his window, watching the grounds come alive. New students — first-years, obvious from their wide eyes and uncertain movements — filtered through the main gate in a steady stream. Some arrived in fine carriages with family crests on the doors, attended by servants who carried their luggage. Others walked in on foot with everything they owned in a single bag.

The haves and the have-nots. Same as everywhere. Same as home.

He watched one arrival in particular — a broad-shouldered young man with sun-darkened skin and the build of someone who'd spent his life doing physical work. He carried a battered rucksack over one shoulder and walked through the gate with the careful, deliberate steps of a person who wasn't sure they belonged but was determined not to show it.

No carriage. No servants. No family crest.

But even from this distance, even through a window, Lucian could feel something. The young man's mana signature was... unusual. Not strong in the way that Fourth Circle students felt strong. It was raw. Enormous in volume but completely unrefined — like a boulder compared to the polished gems around him. A massive soul with no training, no cultivation, no shaping.

Interesting. Very interesting.

The young man paused in the courtyard, looking up at the academy towers with an expression that Lucian recognised. Not awe, exactly. More like the quiet, stubborn resolve of someone who had been told their entire life that they didn't belong and had decided to show up anyway.

I know that look, mate. I've been wearing it for eleven days.

He turned from the window and picked up his notes — a thick sheaf of papers covered in diagrams, equations, and spell modifications that would have given the entire faculty a collective aneurysm if they'd known what they were looking at.

He tucked them into a locked drawer, straightened his coat, and paused at the small mirror by the door.

It was the first time he'd really looked at himself. Properly looked, without the haze of panic or exhaustion or the urgent need to be somewhere else.

The face staring back was — well, it was handsome. Not in the broad, obvious way of the military officers he'd seen in the original Lucian's memories. It was sharper than that. High cheekbones, a narrow jaw, dark hair that fell across his forehead in a way that suggested it had never been introduced to a proper barber. His eyes were a deep grey-blue, pale enough to catch the light in a way that made him look like he was perpetually thinking about something more interesting than whatever was in front of him. The overall effect was scholarly — the sort of face that belonged in a library or a lecture hall. Well-fed, at least — whatever else the Caelmonts had failed to provide their disappointment of a son, food wasn't among the shortages.

Not bad, actually. Decent face to wear.

He reached for the desk and picked up his latest creation — a pair of spectacles he'd assembled over the past few days from materials scavenged around the academy. The lenses were ground from a chunk of spent Resonance Crystal he'd found in a waste bin near the faculty workshop — optically clear, perfectly flat, with no magnification whatsoever. The frames were thin wire, shaped and soldered using controlled mana-heat from his fingertips. Crude by the standards of his old world. A minor masterpiece by the standards of someone working without tools in a dormitory.

He didn't need them. His eyesight was fine.

But he'd worn glasses since he was fourteen on Earth. They were part of how he thought — a familiar weight on the bridge of his nose, a frame around the world that made it feel more manageable. Without them, he felt exposed. Unfinished. Like a sentence missing its full stop.

He slid them on and looked at his reflection again.

There you are.

The glasses completed something. The sharp features softened into something approachable. The intense grey-blue eyes became merely studious behind the lenses. He looked, for all the world, like exactly what he was supposed to be — a bookish, unremarkable, slightly eccentric young professor who posed no threat to anyone.

Perfect disguise. And I didn't even have to use magic for this one.

He was already looking slightly healthier than he had eleven days ago. The skin had more colour. The posture was straighter. Subtle changes — nothing anyone would remark on yet — but the beginnings of a body being quietly rebuilt from the inside by a torrent of mana it had never experienced before.

His mana signature still read as Third Circle Peak — genuine, not suppressed. His density was climbing fast, but the soul hadn't tempered yet. To anyone who checked, he was exactly what his file said: a barely-qualified Caelmont with nothing to offer.

The face of someone who belongs in the background. Perfect.

He opened the door and stepped into the corridor.

Tomorrow, he would stand in front of a room full of first-year students and teach them the fundamentals of soul theory — the same fundamentals that their textbooks got wrong, that their entire civilisation had built on an incomplete understanding, and that he had quietly, invisibly, utterly surpassed.
