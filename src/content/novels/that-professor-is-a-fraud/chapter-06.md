Three days after his first lecture, Lucian sat at his desk grading — or rather, pretending to grade — the preliminary soul-assessment forms the academy required of all incoming students.

He'd already memorised every form. Eight threads made administrative work trivially fast. The actual task had taken eleven minutes. He'd been sitting here for forty, letting one thread handle the performance of page-turning while the rest worked on problems that mattered.

Thread one: refining the electrolysis spell. Thread two: modelling the long-term effects of sustained mana saturation on soul density growth rates. Thread three: designing a cultivation exercise for someone with massive soul volume but terrible quality — a hypothetical exercise, aimed at no one in particular. Thread four: reviewing third-year spell theory for inconsistencies he could use in future lectures without approaching dangerous territory.

Threads five through seven: maintaining general awareness, running background pattern recognition on the ambient mana fluctuations across campus, and monitoring his own soul's condition.

Thread eight was doing nothing.

He'd noticed that this morning. One of his threads had simply... stopped contributing. Not crashed, not overloaded. Just gone quiet. As though there wasn't enough of him to fill eight lanes of cognition simultaneously, and the eighth had found itself with nothing to say.

He'd dismissed it as fatigue. Probably fatigue.

I've been pushing hard for two weeks. The brain needs rest even if the soul doesn't. That's all it is.

But for a moment — brief, barely there — he'd tried to remember the name of his thesis supervisor. The man he'd worked with for two years. Whose office he'd sat in every Tuesday afternoon for feedback sessions.

The name came. Eventually. After a delay that shouldn't have existed.

Dr. Harrison. David Harrison. See? Fine. It's fine.

It was fine.

He set the forms aside, adjusted his glasses, and checked the time. Twenty minutes until his second lecture.

Right. Let's see if they did the homework.

---

Ren Valkis had never been good with books.

It wasn't that he couldn't read — he could, thank you very much, the village schoolmaster had made sure of that — but books had always felt like they were written for someone else. Someone who'd grown up with a library in their house instead of a pickaxe in the shed. Someone who'd learned words like "paradigm" and "foundational" before they'd learned how to split kindling.

But he'd done the homework.

Three chapters of Maren's Principles of the Soul. He'd read them twice, slowly, sounding out the longer words in his head the way he used to sound out his letters as a boy. And he'd made his list.

It wasn't a long list. Four items. Written in blocky, unpractised handwriting on a piece of paper that had already been folded and unfolded so many times it was soft at the creases.

He sat in the front row of Lecture Hall 7 — his seat from the first class, because Ren Valkis was not the sort of person who changed his seat once he'd chosen it — and waited.

Professor Caelmont arrived precisely on time. Not early, not late. He walked in wearing the same faculty coat as before, the same wire-framed spectacles, carrying nothing but a single book and his own apparent indifference to the fact that his classroom contained six students in a space built for thirty.

Ren watched him. He'd been watching him since that first lecture, with the careful attention of someone who'd grown up learning to read people because getting it wrong meant getting hit.

Professor Caelmont didn't move like a Third Circle mage.

Ren couldn't have articulated what he meant by that. He didn't have the vocabulary. But he'd worked alongside mages at the mines — Fourth Circle overseers who managed the extraction crews. They moved like people who were constantly aware of their own limitations. Careful with their mana. Measured in their gestures. Slightly tense, the way a man with a small purse is tense in a crowded market.

Caelmont didn't have that tension. He moved like someone who had never once worried about running out.

Probably just confidence, Ren thought. Noble-born confidence. They're all like that, aren't they?

But it nagged at him.

"Good morning," Caelmont said, setting the book on the lectern. "I trust you've all read chapters one through three. And I trust you've brought your lists."

---

Emeric Hayle's list was four pages long.

He'd organised it by chapter, then by sub-section, with page numbers and direct quotations for each unproven claim he'd identified. He'd used two colours of ink — black for the claim, red for his annotation on why he believed it was unproven. He'd also added a supplementary page of cross-references to secondary texts he'd found in the student library that either supported or contradicted Maren's assertions.

He was, he was fairly certain, the only student who'd done this.

He was also fairly certain that Professor Caelmont was going to think he was ridiculous.

"Let's begin," Caelmont said. "Who'd like to share what they found?"

Silence. Emeric gripped his list under the desk. The noble girl — Isolde, her name was Isolde Varen, he'd overheard her introducing herself to the clerk — sat perfectly still with a single sheet of paper on her desk. Doyle Mercer had his arms crossed and appeared to have brought nothing written down at all. The two friends in the back — Lira and Tomas — had a shared list between them.

Ren, beside him, had a folded piece of paper that looked like it had survived a war.

Nobody spoke.

"I'll start, then," Emeric said, before his nerve could fail him. His voice came out thinner than he'd intended. "Chapter one, page eighteen. Maren states that all souls are fundamentally uniform in composition — that the difference between a strong soul and a weak one is purely a matter of density and quality. But he doesn't cite any experimental evidence. He references Godwin Hale's taxonomy from two hundred years ago, which was itself based on observational classification, not controlled testing."

He looked up from his notes.

Caelmont was watching him. Not with the glazed patience that professors usually wore when a student rambled, and not with the polite impatience that preceded being told to get to the point. He was watching with — attention. Genuine, focused, interested attention.

"Go on," Caelmont said.

"Well — the implication is that there might be structural differences between souls beyond density and quality. Variations in composition, perhaps, or in internal organisation. But Maren never explores this because the uniformity assumption simplifies his entire framework. If souls are uniform, you only need two variables to describe them. If they aren't..."

"Then the model breaks," Caelmont finished. Not impatiently. As though he were pleased. "And if the model breaks, you'd need to rebuild it from scratch. Which is exactly the kind of work that gets you tenure at this academy, if you do it carefully."

Was that — had he just complimented him? Emeric wasn't sure. Caelmont's tone made it hard to tell. Everything the man said sounded like it might be either praise or a setup for the next question.

"Who else? What did the rest of you find?"

Isolde Varen spoke for the first time. "Chapter two, page thirty-one. Maren claims that spell etching permanence is solely a function of repetition count — more casts, deeper etching. He provides no evidence for the linearity of this relationship and ignores Fosse's data from his own later chapter that suggests etching quality varies independently of repetition count."

She said it as though she were reading a weather report. Precise. Detached.

Emeric stared at her. That was a connection between chapters that he'd missed entirely.

Caelmont's expression didn't change, but something behind his eyes — a warmth, maybe, or a recognition — flickered briefly.

"Excellent. You've just identified a contradiction within Maren's own text. He presents data in chapter four that undermines his claim in chapter two, and never reconciles them. That's not a minor oversight. That's a structural flaw in the textbook."

He turned to the rest of the class. "Anyone else?"

Doyle Mercer hadn't done the homework.

Well — that wasn't entirely true. He'd read the chapters. He was a Mercer. Mercers didn't skip readings. But he hadn't written a list because he hadn't found the exercise worth his time. Maren was the accepted textbook. If there were errors, someone more qualified than a first-year student would have found them already.

That was what he'd believed walking in.

Forty minutes later, he wasn't sure what he believed.

The class had become something he'd never experienced in any of the private tutoring sessions his family had paid for. Caelmont didn't lecture. He dismantled. He'd taken the students' observations — even Ren's halting, poorly worded contribution about how Maren described mana storage differently in two separate chapters, "like he changed his mind and forgot to go back and fix it" — and woven them into a systematic demolition of the textbook's foundational assumptions.

Not all of them. Caelmont was careful about that. He acknowledged what Maren got right, defended the claims that held up, and praised the framework's practical utility. But the gaps — the places where assertion replaced evidence, where convenience replaced rigour — he laid open with the calm precision of a tailor.

And the unnerving thing was how easy he made it look.

Doyle had studied under private tutors who were Fourth and Fifth Circle. Serious, accomplished mages who commanded real respect. They taught with authority — the authority of people who were stronger and more experienced than their students.

Caelmont didn't teach with authority. He taught with understanding. And those were very different things.

A Fourth Circle tutor could demonstrate a spell. Caelmont could explain why it worked, why it was designed that way, what alternatives had been tried and discarded, and what assumptions the designer had made that might not hold in all cases. His knowledge wasn't just deep — it was architectural.

And he was Third Circle.

Third Circle.

"Professor," Doyle said, interrupting a discussion about whether Maren's mana-flow model accurately represented real-world soul behaviour. "Might I ask a question?"

Caelmont looked at him. There it was again — that mild, bespectacled calm that gave away absolutely nothing. "Of course."

"How do you know all this?"

The room went quiet. Emeric's pen stopped. Ren looked up. Even Isolde's perfect composure wavered by a fraction.

It wasn't a polite question. It was the question everyone had been thinking and nobody had asked.

A Third Circle professor, appointed through blatant nepotism, from a disgraced family — teaching like this?

Caelmont regarded him for a moment.

Then he adjusted his glasses and said, simply: "I read a great deal."

It was not a satisfying answer. Doyle knew it wasn't meant to be. But there was something in the way Caelmont said it — an absolute, settled certainty that this was all the answer he intended to give, and that pressing further would be met with the same five words — that made Doyle decide, against every instinct, to let it go.

For now.

"The hour is nearly up," Caelmont said, returning to the lectern. "For next session, I want you to read chapter four in full — Fosse's spell-etching studies. But I want you to read it alongside chapter two. Map the contradictions. Don't just find them — try to explain them. Come with theories, even bad ones. Especially bad ones. Bad theories are where good theories start."

He closed the textbook.

"Also." A pause. "I'm aware this class isn't what any of you expected when you enrolled. It was supposed to be a formality — an easy mark taught by an unremarkable professor. I'm afraid I can't do much about the unremarkable professor part." The ghost of a smile behind the glasses. "But I can promise you that if you do the work, this will not be easy. And it will not be a waste of your time."

The students filed out. Doyle lingered for a moment, watching Caelmont gather his things. The professor moved without hurry, without fuss, without the faintest sign that he'd just dismantled two hundred years of accepted textbook wisdom in front of six first-year students.

I read a great deal.

Doyle left the room with the distinct and uncomfortable feeling that he'd just been lied to by the most honest-sounding man he'd ever met.

---

The corridor was empty. The students were gone.

Lucian stood in the doorway of Lecture Hall 7, one hand resting on the frame, and let the threads settle.

It had gone well. Better than well. The Fosse contradiction was a gift — a genuine gap in the textbook that any sharp student could find, leading to real questions about spell-soul interaction without touching surface area, fragmentation, or anything that pointed toward his work. He could build an entire semester around it.

And the students were better than he'd expected. Isolde Varen had made a cross-chapter connection that most third-years would miss. Emeric Hayle had a researcher's instinct buried under all that anxiety. Even Ren — barely literate compared to the others — had noticed an inconsistency by pure intuition, a raw analytical instinct that just needed sharpening.

And Doyle Mercer had asked the one question that mattered: How do you know all this?

I read a great deal.

He'd felt nothing when he said it. No anxiety, no thrill of deception, no satisfaction at the misdirection.

That bothered him. Just slightly.

The old him — the grad student, the one who'd panicked in the wagon eleven days ago — would have felt something. A twinge of guilt at the lie, or at least the nervous comedy of pulling it off. Some flicker of "I can't believe they bought that."

Instead, there had been nothing.

It's fine. I'm just settling in. The emotional range will normalise once I'm less focused on survival.

He adjusted his glasses and walked toward the faculty library.

Behind him, eight threads hummed in quiet synchronisation, and the empty corridor felt, for just a moment, very large and very still.
