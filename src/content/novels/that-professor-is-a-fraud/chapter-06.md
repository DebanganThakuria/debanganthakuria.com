Every spellform in this Primer has been proved upon ten thousand souls. Where a form will not take, the fault will be found in preparation, in diligence, or in the student.

— Whitsun's Primer of Soul Cultivation, twelfth edition, preface

---

Clara Vance arrived at four o'clock exactly.

That told Lucian something before she said a word. She had come early and waited out of sight until the hour struck. Early was presumptuous; late was disrespectful. Somewhere along the way, this girl had stopped taking chances with either.

She was small, mousy-fair, eighteen and dressed to be twenty-five, in grey gloves that she kept still in her lap by visible effort. Her maid — a broad, calm woman who had clearly done this four times before — took the stool by the door without being asked and produced knitting. The family had requested discretion; discretion meant a private study instead of the public tutorial halls, and a maid by the door instead of the world's eyes.

Clara sat down in the second chair, folded her hands, and delivered a sentence she had prepared on the stairs.

"Thank you for taking me, Mr. Caelmont. I am told I am a difficult case, and I will understand if the arrangement does not answer."

Every word level, every word complete. A girl who had learned that if you said the terrible thing first, calmly, it hurt less than hearing it.

"Who told you that you were a difficult case?"

The question surprised her. "Everyone," she said, and then, honestly: "Four tutors. And Father says—" She stopped, and started the sentence again. The first version had been truer. "Father says you used to work for the house, and that I am not to waste your time, because your time was expensive once."

"Your father is correct on both points, and mistaken about which of us wasted it," Lucian said. "We'll come to that. First I am going to ask you some questions. They will sound strange. Nobody has asked them before, which is precisely why I am asking."

He had decided, over the past week, how to run this session. The girl had been handled, and handling had failed four times. So he would not handle her. He would tell her the truth and work the problem with her, side by side, the same as he would with any colleague.

But there was a history in the room, and it was his, so he paid it first.

"Since your father raised it: yes. House Vance sponsored my research for two years. The house wanted marketable soul-medicine. My research went instead toward repair, which the house considered unsellable and indecent, and three weeks ago the house ended the arrangement in front of four hundred people. You will hear versions of this at home. I would rather you heard mine once, so that whatever happens in this room isn't happening around a secret." He let that sit. "None of it is your fault, none of it is your debt, and none of it will walk through that door on Tuesdays or Fridays. Now. The questions."

Clara looked at him with an expression he recognised, because he had worn it himself: a person being spoken to as an adult, and looking for the trick in it.

"Yes," she said. "All right."

There was a part he did not say aloud, because it was his to carry. The sponsorship contract had included a clause: a master-grade spell from the Vance library, transferred to him upon delivery of results — with the results defined by the house, and redefined by the house, quarter after quarter. For a man stalled at the Wall, that clause was the door out of Circle 3, kept visible and kept shut. Houses did not fund researchers to advance them. They funded researchers to hold them. Then, eight months ago, he had crossed the Wall without their spell, their leverage died, and the house never forgave him for killing it. That was the real reason the sponsorship ended. The lecture was only the excuse.

None of which was the business of the girl in the second chair, who was the one person in House Vance he intended to leave better than he found her.

---

The questions took forty minutes, and the maid's knitting slowed and finally stopped, because nobody in that household had ever heard anyone ask Miss Clara anything at all.

"When an etching fails," Lucian asked, "what does it feel like? Not what the tutors said it should feel like. What it does."

Silence. Then: "Slipping," Clara said. "Everyone said it would feel hot, and to push through the heat. It never felt hot. It feels the way a boot feels on wet ice. The form goes in, and it — skids. And then there's a pain along the skid, for days." Her hands were very still. "Tutor Merrow said the pain was my temperament objecting to discipline."

"Tutor Merrow etched eleven spells in his life, all before he was thirty," Lucian said. "How many times has your family had you attempt an etching?"

"Fourteen. Three took." She said the numbers completely, without flinching, and they were terrible numbers. Eleven skids. Eleven weeks of pain. Three years of being told the ice was her fault. "I'm at the third stage of the first Circle, Mr. Caelmont. My cousins finished ten etchings by sixteen. I am to come home at the end of term if nothing changes."

"And your casting? Not etching — daily casting, the drilled forms."

"Adequate and slow," she recited. Four tutors' phrase, worn smooth.

"Show me. The [Candle], as you were taught it."

She drew her wand — good rosewood, given as encouragement once — and cast the first spell of the Whitsun sequence: the small steady flame every child in the kingdom learns first. It came late and thin, with a stammer in it, and the effort showed at her jaw.

To the room, that was all that happened. Behind his stillness, Lucian watched the true event through the cloud: the shape of her soul as the mana moved through it, the pattern of the casting laid against the pattern of her.

And there it was.

Every standard spellform in the kingdom wound rightward, spiralling in toward the anchor — the direction most souls naturally ran. Most souls. Not all. Clara Vance's soul ran leftward. Mirrored. Not damaged — he checked twice; damage was his profession — but complete, healthy, and built in reverse, so that every form she had ever been handed fit her backward. That was the skid. He could see its cost, too: faint slip-scars along her lattice, fourteen attempts' worth, where forced etchings had dragged. Minor. Settleable. Cruel.

And nobody could have seen it. That was the terrible part, the part that acquitted everyone and no one. Diagnostic instruments measured quantities — density, quality, flow, the soundness of the nodes. None of them measured direction; the old surveys that built every tool had simply assumed it. So her tests had always come back truthful and useless: healthy soul, adequate density, failing student. And when every instrument swears the soul is sound, the books offer exactly one explanation, printed in the Primer's own preface. The fault lies in the student. Her parents had bought the best tutors and the best instruments in Westmere, and every mark they spent had confirmed the lie. Even Lucian had needed the cloud, and a live casting dragging through her against the grain, to catch it.

Which left the fourth tutor, and now Lucian understood him too. The mirror was invisible to instruments — but the slip-scars were not. Damage was exactly what the diagnostics were built to find, and he had run them twice in five weeks: small accumulating injuries on a great house's daughter, laid down under professional hands, his own now among them. He had not understood the cause. He had understood the liability. So he had concluded the engagement without assessment and returned his fees in full, so that no record of his five weeks would remain to argue with him.

He had seen the harm, and saved himself.

Lucian noted, at half volume, something that would have been anger at full.

"Miss Vance," he said. "Put down the wand. I'm going to draw something, and then I am going to tell you something, and the thing I tell you is going to be unconventional, which will take some getting used to."

He took the chalk and drew the [Candle] form on the slate — the form from every primer, the form she had failed at fifteen and apologised for ever since.

Then, beside it, he drew it again, reversed. Every line mirrored, winding leftward to a leftward anchor.

"Your soul is mirrored," he said. "Built in reverse of the common run. Not damaged — I want that word out of the room — not stunted, not slow. Reversed, complete, and rather cleanly made. Every form you have ever been given was built for the other direction. You have spent three years being handed right-handed gloves, Miss Vance, and been told your hands were at fault." He set down the chalk. "And before you ask the question you are already forming: no one could have found this. No instrument in the kingdom measures a soul's direction. Every test you ever sat came back healthy, because you are healthy — so the books blamed the only thing left, which was you." He tapped the second diagram. "This is your [Candle]. Cast it."

Clara looked at the mirrored form for a long moment. Her face did not change; it was well trained. But her breathing did.

"The form is backward," she said.

"So are you. Cast."

She raised the rosewood wand, and cast the first spell of the Whitsun sequence as it had never been printed in any edition of Whitsun.

The flame came at once. Steady, upright, bright. An ordinary first-year child's flame — and that was the whole miracle. For the first time, it had come without the skid, without the stammer, without the cost. It sat above her wand and burned.

Clara Vance looked at it and did not say anything, because she could not, and the flame shook once and steadied, and by the door the maid had stopped pretending to knit.

"Adequate," said Lucian, in Tutor Merrow's voice, "and slow."

The sound the girl made was half a laugh, and the other half had years in it, and he let both halves have the room for as long as they needed. The flame burned on through all of it. It did not go out until she let it, and she did not let it for a long time.

---

He made tea. There was only the one kettle and the College's plain brown pot, and he served the maid as well, which appeared to unsettle her more than anything else that had happened in the room.

Clara talked.

Too fast at first, then steadier. She talked about the family dinners at the winter holidays, where each cousin demonstrated the term's new etching in the long drawing room, oldest to youngest, and how the order stopped before it reached her, every year, by silent agreement, as a kindness. She talked about the rosewood wand — thirteenth birthday, when there was still hope, her mother saying rosewood suits a healer's temperament — and how she had kept it wrapped in a drawer for a year after the eleventh failed etching, because looking at it was worse than failing. She talked with her hands, which had escaped her lap entirely.

"May I ask something impertinent?" she said, when the pot was half gone.

"Those are the only kind worth the tea."

"Penny says that too." Clara smiled without guarding it, the first unguarded expression of the afternoon. "You know she's my dearest friend. Penelope Holloway. She attached herself to me at the Michaelmas tea two years ago and refused all discouragement. Penny collects people who sit alone." She turned the teacup in its saucer. "She is the only person who never once asked what was wrong with me. She asked what I was good at. Nobody had ever—" she stopped the sentence, and finished the thought elsewhere. "She's why I'm here, you know."

"The tutoring request came through the College."

"The request did." Clara looked at him with sudden care, choosing to say it. "But last spring — before the term, before your lecture, before anyone was talking about you at all — Penny told me that if the family ever tried a fifth tutor, I should ask for the one they laugh at. I told her that made no sense. Nobody had even heard of you." The teacup went still. "I asked her why. She said: because he reads the backs of things. I didn't understand it then. I don't understand it now." She looked at the slate, where the mirrored form still stood in chalk. "But she wasn't guessing, Mr. Caelmont. Penny never guesses. She just knows things sideways."

"So I have observed," Lucian said, in a level voice, while a thread carried the sentence to the ledger and set it beside the others. Last spring. Before the term, before the lecture, before the Meridian, before Penelope Holloway had ever stood in front of him with a plate of lemon ices. He reads the backs of things.

The ledger was becoming a document he intended, someday, to read from beginning to end in one sitting.

"One more question," Clara said. "Then I'll do the mechanics, I promise. You said the books were built by people who never imagined me." Her hands had gone still again, but differently now: attention, not armor. "Are there others? Built backward, the way I am?"

"Statistically, there must be. In practice they are almost never found. Most wash out of magic before anyone thinks to look — called talentless, returned to ordinary life. The ones from careful families are sometimes hidden outright; there are old superstitions about souls that run against the Wheel, and families prefer 'slow' to 'strange.'" He watched her absorb it: an invisible population, scattered through the kingdom, each one alone in a room being told the fault was theirs. "You may be the first mirrored mage properly diagnosed in this kingdom in a generation."

Clara was quiet for a moment.

"Then somebody ought to be finding them," she said. Not a complaint. A conclusion, complete, in her usual way — and kept, he could see, somewhere permanent.

---

The last quarter hour was work. The mechanics had to be laid down while the proof was still fresh.

The plain truth, stated as to a colleague: the mirror changed nothing about the ladder and everything about the climb. Ten apprentice etchings per Circle, the same as anyone — but every form would have to be mirrored before she drilled it, and no printed primer would ever be directly usable by her. That was the cost. The gain: etchings laid along her true grain would take cleanly, the scarring could be settled in a session or two, and the word difficult could be retired.

"Homework. Drill the mirrored [Candle] until it bores you. Nothing else — do not touch the old forms; they were never yours. By Friday I will have the next three forms of the sequence mirrored and copied." He was already doing it, on two threads, while he spoke. "And Miss Vance. At the end of term, when your family asks what changed — tell them the truth. Tell them the curriculum was printed for other people."

Clara copied the mirrored form into her notebook in a small exact hand, closed it, held it on her lap with both hands, and stood to go. At the door she stopped.

"Fourteen times," she said, not turning around. "Somebody could have looked fourteen times."

"Yes."

"But looking wasn't the assignment." She said it evenly, her own conclusion, arrived at completely. "Improving me was the assignment. Nobody was ever paid to find out what I am."

She curtseyed — precise, complete — and went out. The maid gathered her knitting and paused in the doorway, choosing, once, to say a thing out of turn.

"Three years I've taken her to lessons, sir. That's the first one she's left smiling."

Then they were gone, and the study was quiet, and the second chair stood at its small new angle by the desk, used.

Lucian sat in the amber lamplight and let the threads sort the afternoon. The mirrored sequence for Friday, half-built already. The slip-scars, to be settled next session, with her consent and a full explanation first. A note for the research ledger, phrased carefully: a complete mirror-lattice, healthy and whole — the rarest structural variant he had ever read, sitting in his second chair twice a week, paid for by the very house that had no idea what it had.

And one more observation, underlined, because it belonged to the future. A mind that had spent years involuntarily flipping every spellform it saw, just to perceive them, had been training — unknowing, unwilling, through pain — in the single skill all counter-magic was built on. He would say nothing yet. But down that road there was work no other mage in the kingdom was shaped for. Then somebody ought to be finding them, she had said. He suspected she would want it.

One question he set aside, unanswered. House Vance was a soul-research house. Instruments could not find the mirror — but had no one there ever run a deeper assay on its own puzzling daughter? And if someone had, and had gotten back readings that fit no chart — what had they done with the file?

He knew houses. Houses shelved things.

And the eighth thread held up the day's last accounting, the one with no research value at all: House Vance had spent two years paying him to cure souls while forbidding him the road to the cure — and the first soul his forbidden road would save was theirs.

They would never forgive him for it.

He found, checking the feeling twice, at half volume, that he could live with that.
