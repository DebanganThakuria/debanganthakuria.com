Three hundred years ago, nine summoned heroes defeated the Demon King.

The songs praise eight.

The ninth — the cloning hero whose "copies" fought the war while the real man slipped away — was erased from the chronicles, and mostly by his own hand. What remains of him is superstition: the Kind Stranger, the traveler in crossroads tales who fixes things and is gone by morning. Grandmothers still set an extra plate.

Today he is a nameless man dozing on tumbling carriages. He is also a network of masks embedded quietly across the world — a thieves' guild right hand, a mages' guild advisor, a canonized saint — and, somewhere no map can find, a god asleep on the chaos he swallowed, whose sleep is the world's ward.

He has had a thousand eyes for three hundred years, and he chose not to look. It was comfortable.

Then a runaway princess with a three-hundred-year-old letter and a small, age-appropriate problem climbs into his carriage — and walks him straight into the wound between worlds.
