The rain had started before dawn and showed no sign of having anywhere better to be.

The mail carriage ran the north road twice a week, weather permitting. Nobody had asked the weather. It was an old vehicle with honest springs and a roof that held, mostly, and it smelled of wet wool and older journeys. The driver's name was Hann. He had said so at the toll bridge, to no one in particular, and had not spoken since.

There were two passengers.

One was a man in a much-repaired oilskin coat, asleep, or doing a fair imitation of it. He was tall and broad through the shoulders, and the coat did not quite hide it. He sat at ease. He had slept in worse places. His dark hair was tied at the back of his neck in a short knot that had last been neat some days ago. His boots were very good. People never noticed the boots first, which suited him.

The other passenger was a girl of perhaps thirteen, and she was pretending, with great discipline, that she had not spent the night in a hedge.

There were leaves in her hair. The hair itself was honey-colored and done up in a braided crown, and the braid had been redone that morning, tightly, with cold fingers — one could tell, because everything else about her had surrendered. Her dress was brown, the sensible brown of a merchant's daughter, but the seams were fine and even, and the hem had been let down once by a hand that knew what it was doing. She sat very straight, holding a leather satchel on her knees with both hands.

The carriage held nothing but rain-noise and one sleeping man.

At mid-morning she took half a bread roll from the satchel, considered it, divided it in her mind into two further halves, and ate one of them slowly, looking out the window with great interest at nothing.

The man opened one eye.

"There's cheese," he said, "in the basket under the bench. The driver sells it. He overcharges."

The girl's chin came up. "I have eaten, thank you."

"Mm," said the man, and went back to sleep.

Her stomach growled, at length, loudly. The man did not smile. It was a near thing.

"Perhaps," the girl said, to the window, "a small piece. As I'll be keeping the draft off you by sitting here, it seems a fair exchange."

"Very fair," the man agreed gravely, and knocked on the boards for the driver.

The cheese was overpriced and good. She made herself eat it slowly, and the man watched the road unroll behind them, and for a while the rain did all the talking.

At the seventh mile-mark the carriage passed a standing stone.

It was a squat grey thing, hip-high, carved with a spiral worn shallow by three hundred winters. Every child on the north road knew the stones. Knowing them, nobody looked. In the man's youth — in the stone's youth — a traveler could have warmed his hands at that spiral on a night like this. Now the rain sat on it, unbothered, and the carved channels ran dark.

The man looked at the stone until the rain took it.

The girl looked at the man. She had been watching him in glances for an hour, and had gotten nowhere with him. When the carriage jolted, his coat shifted, and she saw it: a metal plate on a cord around his neck, the size of a palm. Silver. Worn smooth at the edges, the stamped crest almost gone.

She put down the last of the cheese.

"Hey." Her voice came out louder than a princess would have liked. "You. You're one of the Nine, aren't you?"

Up front, Hann laughed. It was the first sound he had made in three hours, and it was not a kind one.

"The Nine," the driver said, to the horses. "That circus still touring, is it?"

The man had not moved. He looked at the girl for a moment — she felt the look arrive, somehow, before she saw his eyes, and for the space of a breath she wanted to apologize to somebody without knowing why. Then he smiled, warmly, and the feeling seemed silly.

"Technically," he said.

"I knew it." She sat forward. "The plate. That's a company plate. My — I have read that the Nine were the greatest company of the age. Founded in honor of the heroes themselves. Sworn to take any worthy quest, however small, from any petitioner, however humble."

"That's the recruiting speech," the man said. "The dues are two marks a year and you buy your own tabard."

"But you are a member."

"I said yes once and no one has let me take it back." He tucked the plate away. "The word you want is 'was.' I peddle potions now. Joint salve. Wart-water. There's a tonic for baldness that works nine times of ten, and the tenth man buys a hat and no harm done."

The girl was not listening. The answer had already been decided. She had the satchel open. She produced, in order: a ledger bound in green cloth, a stick of graphite in a brass holder, and a small purse that made a thin sound.

"I wish to engage a hero," she said. "I can pay three silver marks and forty coppers. That is a fair month's wage for a silver-plate adventurer, at guild rates, less the customary discount for meals, which I would be providing." She looked up. "I have costed it."

"Meals like that bread roll?"

"There will be other rolls."

"Mm." The man tipped his head back against the boards. "What's the quest?"

For the first time, she hesitated. The rain filled it in.

"There is a problem," she said carefully, "in the south. Along the old shrine road. A — family matter."

"The shrine road's closed," Hann said from the front, to nobody. "Two years now. They light candles at the bottom of the pass and go home again. Waste of good candles."

The girl's chin came up again. Her chin, the man thought, was going to have a hard life. "It is not closed to me."

"A family matter," the man repeated. "On a dead road. For three marks and forty."

"And meals."

"And meals." He closed his eyes. "No."

"You haven't heard the—"

"I've heard it. Somebody's granddame is sick, or a well's gone sour, or there's a beast in the barley that's twice the size it was when they saw it. It's always one of those, and a village lad with a boar-spear handles it nine times of ten."

"And the tenth time?"

"The tenth time," the man said, eyes still shut, "they should send for a hero. I sell tonics."

The carriage rolled. The rain came hard at the roof. The girl sat with her spine very straight and her ledger very square on her knees, and anyone else her age would have cried, or shouted, and she did the sum again instead and found where she had been careless.

"You misunderstand," she said. "I was offering payment out of courtesy. The debt already exists."

The man opened both eyes.

"In the year of the victory," she said, in the voice of a lesson recited so many nights it had worn grooves, "a hero passed through the south, before the roads had names again. My family did him a kindness. He left a letter. One favor, whenever asked, to whoever carries it. It has never been asked." She drew it from beneath her coat then, where she had carried it against her skin: a fold of paper gone soft with carrying, wrapped in oiled silk, sealed with a blob of plain wax that bore no crest at all — only the small, deliberate print of somebody's thumb. "If you are one of the Nine — even a — even a *potion* one — then the debt is the company's, and I am calling it in."

"That's not how letters work," the man said. "Or companies. Or debts."

"It is how honor works."

"Honor." He said the word quietly, and for a moment he sounded tired. He held out his hand. "Let's see it, then."

"You'll note the seal is unbroken."

"I note it."

"It is three hundred years old."

"Then it's had a better life than most letters."

She handed it over slowly, with terms understood. The man turned it over once in his hands. He was not smiling now. She would think about that later — that he had gone quiet all the way through, the way the ward-stone was quiet — but at the moment she was only watching the seal, and so she missed the small, held stillness of him.

He slid his thumbnail beneath the wax without breaking the print, which cannot be done, though nobody had told him so. He unfolded the letter.

The paper had yellowed. The ink had gone brown. The rain went on. Inside the carriage, no one spoke.

*One favor is owed to the bearer of this letter,* it said, in a neat, patient hand, the letters a little stiff. *It shall be paid in full, without condition, though the asking come late and the world be changed. Kindness is the only debt that gathers interest.*

There was no signature. There was only, at the bottom, drawn in three strokes, a small road that went over a small hill and did not show what was on the other side.

The man read it twice. It did not take long. It was not a long letter.

Outside, the seventh mile fell behind them, and the eighth came on through the rain, and somewhere far to the south, under a shrine with no light in it, something kept doing what it had been doing, patiently, for a while now.

"Well?" said the girl. Her hands were folded so that they would not shake, and it almost worked. "Is it good? The debt."

"It's good." He folded the letter along its old creases, where it wanted to go, and gave it back. "I'll take the job."

She blinked. She had prepared several further arguments, and being spared them seemed to unbalance her more than refusal had. "You — well. Naturally you will. That is." She recovered her chin. "The terms include meals."

"The terms include rolls," he said. "I heard. What do I call you? Not the whole thing," he added, as she drew breath. "Whatever's left when you take the whole thing off."

She let the breath go. "Lise," she said. "Lise will do. And you?"

The man looked out the window. The rain had eased without anyone noticing, and the light was coming up thin and clean off the wet fields, and the road went south ahead of them, over a hill, not showing what was on the other side.

"Kuon," he said.

It meant nothing to her. It had meant nothing to anyone for a long time. He watched her write it in the green ledger under *expenses*, spelling it wrong, and he did not correct her, because the spelling did not matter and never had.

What mattered was the letter, going back beneath her coat, against her heart, where it had ridden all this way to find him.

He had recognized the seal at ten paces. He had known the road-and-hill mark at once. And the neat, stiff, patient hand that had written *kindness is the only debt that gathers interest*, three hundred years ago, in a farm kitchen that no longer stood, by the light of a fire long gone out —

The handwriting was his own.
