She went on the third night, after closing.

Devan handed her a wooden box at the door. It was warm, and heavy, and tied with string.

"Duck, bread, and a honey thing we're testing," he said. "Never visit family empty-handed."

"He means it for your aunt," said Lydia, "so eat only half."

Quinn held the box and looked at the two of them — the cook in his apron, the innkeeper with her folded hands — and found her throat too tight for anything useful. So she bowed, guest to house, the way Lydia had taught her was proper here, and turned to the front door of the restaurant.

Say the place, and walk.

"The winter hall at Marchhold," she said. "By the great hearth."

She opened the door onto darkness and cold, and stepped out of the world's warmest room into the north.

---

The hall was as big as she remembered. Everything else was smaller — she had been nine — but not the hall. Stone pillars going up into dark. Banners overhead, too dim to read. The great hearth stood banked for the night, coals breathing under ash, and the cold came off the flagstones through her boots.

Behind her, the door she had come through was already gone. Bare stone.

Quinn stood alone in the heart of her aunt's fortress, five hundred miles from the snowed-in pass, and let herself have one breath of pure disbelief. Then she set the box down and did what an innkeeper would do.

She built up the fire.

That was where the sentry found her, ten minutes later — a stranger in a worn cloak, kneeling at the great hearth of Marchhold, feeding it splits of birch. He was young. His sword came out fast, and the point of it wavered.

"How—" His voice cracked. He tried again. "The gates are shut. How did you get in?"

"Through hard country," said Quinn, which was true. She rose, and kept her hands where he could see them. "Wake the Lady Hedda. Tell her that her sister's daughter is standing in her hall."

"Her sister's—" The boy stared. "Her sister is dead."

"Yes," said Quinn. "But I am not. Go and tell her."

---

Hedda of the March came down armed.

She came with six men and a naked blade, gray hair loose on her shoulders, a fur thrown over her nightclothes, and she stopped ten feet away and looked at the stranger by her fire for a long time.

Quinn looked back. She had planned words for this. She let them go, and simply stood, and let her aunt look.

"Torchlight lies," Hedda said at last. "Come here."

Quinn came. A hard hand took her chin and turned her face to the fire, this way, then that.

"You have Elin's jaw," said Hedda. "And her eyes. That proves you a bastard of some cousin, or a player, or a ghost. The March has had all three." The hand did not let go. "The gray pony your mother kept. What did she call it?"

"Turnip," said Quinn. "Because it bit like one. She let me name it. I was four."

Silence. Behind Hedda, the sentries stood very still.

"Nobody knows that," said Hedda, quietly. "She wrote me one letter about that pony and I burned it with the rest when she died, because I could not—" She stopped. Her hand came off Quinn's chin, and gripped her shoulder instead, hard enough to hurt, and that grip was the whole embrace, and it was enough. Northern grief keeps its back straight.

"You are supposed to be at your father's court," Hedda said. "Eating off gold. What are you doing in my hall at the black end of night, dressed for a funeral in a ditch?"

"Surviving," said Quinn.

Hedda looked at her a moment longer. Then she turned to her men.

"Build up the fire," she said. "Bring wine and meat. And wake no one else. My niece was never here."

"I brought food," said Quinn, and held out the box, because the box was warm in her hands, and something in her wanted this over with before the telling. "A gift. From — friends on the road."

Hedda opened it there at the hearth: roast duck, dark bread, a small covered dish that smelled of honey. She frowned at it. She tore off a piece of the duck, and ate it, and stood entirely still.

"Your friends," she said, "can cook."

"Yes," said Quinn. "They can."

---

The telling took an hour.

They sat at the hearth end of the long table, and Quinn laid it out in columns, the same way she had at the counter — the illness, the accounts, the appointments, the rotations, the seal. The plums. Merta.

She said Merta's name without her voice catching, this time. She had a use for the grief now, and grief with a use will hold its line.

Hedda listened without moving. She asked four questions, all of them about dates. When it was done she sat with her wine untouched and looked into the fire.

"Vantry," she said. "I met him once, at your mother's wedding. He held my stirrup, and I remember thinking: this one holds stirrups the way other men buy land." She drank. "You should have come to me the day the taster died."

"The pass—"

"Damn the pass. You should have—" Hedda stopped herself. "No. You are here, and alive, and that is the only column that matters. So. The March musters in spring. Five hundred lances of mine, and I can shame or marry another eight hundred out of the valley lords by thaw. Papers can be got to the temples. The old chancellor's men still owe your mother's name a debt or two." She was moving pieces now, fast, a woman built for exactly this. "But nothing marches before the thaw, girl. That is four months. Vantry holds your father, the seal, and the capital for four months, and if your father dies in that time with you gone from court, the story writes itself: the mad princess fled, the grieving chancellor stood fast. We would arrive in spring as rebels."

"I know," said Quinn. "That is why I will be in the capital."

"You will be behind my walls."

"By spring, aunt, there will be nothing left to march for. He does not need to kill me now. He only needs my father's heart to stop on a cold night, with me missing. I have to go back."

"Back," said Hedda. "Through the passes that nearly killed you. In deep winter. Into his city, past his gates, under his guards, into the one palace in the world where every corridor answers to him." She set down the cup. "How did you get into my hall, Quinn?"

And there it was.

Quinn had thought hard about this moment, on the third bend of a dead road and every night since. Lies were tools; her tutors had been thorough. But the March was old country. The old customs still had teeth here — bread and salt, hearth-right, the guest bound to the house and the house to the guest. If there was one soul in the kingdom raised to understand her arrangement, it was the woman across this table.

"I was taken in," she said, "when I was dying on the mountain. An inn that is not on the surveys, kept by a keeper the surveys would not believe. There is a door there that opens onto other places, for guests in good standing." She put her hand flat on the table. "I ate at a stranger's table, aunt. I gave my word as a guest, and the rules of that house hold me. Do not ask me for more than that, because I will not give it."

The hall was quiet. The new fire cracked and settled.

Hedda of the March, who had buried a sister and hanged reavers and held a frozen border for thirty years, looked at her niece with an expression Quinn had never seen on her — and it took a moment to recognize it, because it did not belong on that face.

Care. Old, cold, superstitious care, the kind that lives in border country and never quite dies out of it.

"You ate at a stranger's table," Hedda repeated slowly. "And it took you in, and it feeds you, and it opens doors." She leaned forward. "Then hear me, girl, because my grandmother would rise from her cairn if I said less. Keep its rules. Whatever they are, however small. Pay what it asks, thank it plainly, and never, ever lie under its roof. The old tales are clear on the houses that find the dying. They are kind past all reason, right up until the rule is broken. And then they are not there, and neither is the road home."

"I know the rules," said Quinn. "There are two. Pay your bill. The house is not a weapon."

"Not a weapon." Hedda sat back, and Quinn watched the soldier in her aunt run the sums anyway — watched her see it, the whole campaign of it: doors into any hall, any bedchamber, any gate-tower, a whole war carried through in single file. Watched her want it.

Watched her put it down.

"So we march the old way," said Hedda. "Slow, in the spring, on our own feet. And you walk your road, and I never ask you to carry so much as a knife-man through that door." She said the whole sentence in one breath, and it had the weight of an oath. "Agreed. What do you need from me?"

"Three things. A letter in your hand, under your seal, naming what I've told you — held here, opened if I go silent. A name: whoever you still trust inside the capital. And ten days from now, a man you'd stake the March on, waiting at the Owl Gate at midnight."

"For what?"

"For what I bring out," said Quinn.

---

She left before the hall woke.

Hedda walked her to the hearth — Quinn had asked for the hearth, a place she could stand with her back to warm stone — and the two of them stood a moment in the small hours, the lady in her furs, the princess in her mended boots.

"Your mother," said Hedda, "would have hidden behind my walls, and been right to. You are more like our mother. That is not entirely a compliment."

"I'll take it," said Quinn.

"Eat before you do anything foolish. You're still thin." A pause. "And tell your keeper — the one the surveys would not believe—" Hedda stopped, and did something with her face that on anyone else would have been called uncertainty. "Tell them the March remembers the old courtesies. If a light is ever needed in the north, we will put one in every window we own."

"I'll tell them," said Quinn.

She took out the card, and faced the stone wall beside the great hearth of Marchhold, and said, clearly, "The Sweetwater Inn. The kitchen."

A door stood in the wall where no door had been. Warm light came out from under it. Through the gap, faintly: bread, and woodsmoke, and the small sounds of a house that is kept.

Quinn looked back once. "Spring, aunt."

"Spring," said Hedda of the March.

The door closed, and was stone.

Hedda stood alone in her hall for a long while, with her guards asleep and her wine cold and the smell of that impossible bread still in the air. Then she did a thing she had not done since her sister died. She went to the great hearth, and set on it a piece of bread and a pinch of salt, the old way, the grandmother's way, and spoke to the fire in the low voice the north keeps for things it does not tell the temples.

"Whatever you are," she said, "you fed her. The March does not forget."

The fire burned on, ordinary and gold.

Five hundred miles south, in a kitchen under a mountain, an innkeeper paused a moment over her washing-up, and smiled at nothing, and did not say why.
