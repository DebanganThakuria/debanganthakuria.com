The road had been a good road, once.

It had carried wool south and salt north, and every cart on it had passed the inn at the ford, because the ford was the only crossing for a day's travel in either direction. The inn had nine rooms, a deep cellar, and a kitchen that never fully went cold. Travelers planned their journeys around reaching it by nightfall.

Then the king's engineers cut a pass through the mountains, two valleys east. The new road was shorter. It was safer. It was better in every way that could be measured, and within twenty years the old road belonged to grass.

The town at the ford emptied out slowly, house by house. The inn held on longest. Inns always do.

The woman who kept it was named Lydia. She had been born under its roof, and she kept it to the end — past the last paying guest, past the last neighbor's wagon rolling away east, past the point where keeping it made any sense at all. Some people cannot stop taking care of things. It is not a decision. It is just the shape of them.

She never married. The road that would have brought her someone died first.

She died in winter, quietly, in her thirty-sixth year, in the room behind the kitchen. There was no one left to find her.

That was two hundred years ago.

---

The inn was a ruin now. The roof had fallen in a lifetime ago. Moss had taken the tables she once waxed every spring, and young trees stood in the common room, growing through the floor she used to sweep twice a day.

She still swept it.

Not with a broom — brooms rotted, and her hands passed through the new ones she tried to gather. She swept the way a ghost sweeps, which is to say she walked the old paths between the tables at the old hours and wanted them clean, and wanting was the only work left to her. Morning and evening. Two hundred years.

She was not angry. She had watched other lingering things go bitter and thin, out on the dead road, and she had decided early that she would not become that. She was an innkeeper. If she had to haunt something, she would haunt it properly — with the fires banked, so to speak, and the tables ready.

But no one had ever come.

That was the whole of it, really. Not the dying. The dying had been easy, almost gentle. It was the two hundred years of an open house and no guests. It was being made entirely out of welcome, with no one to spend it on.

Then, on an ordinary night, a door appeared in her kitchen wall.

It was a plain wooden door. It had no business being there — she knew that wall, she knew every stone of it, and there had never been a door. Warm light spilled out from under it, the color of banked coals. And through the gap, faint but unmistakable, came a smell she had not smelled in two centuries.

Someone, somewhere, was cooking.

She stood in front of that door for a long time. A night and a day, though time meant little to her. She was not afraid. She had been a ghost too long to fear strange things; she was the strange thing. What held her there was something older and smaller.

She looked down at herself — at the apron she had died in, faded to gray, at hands the firelight passed through.

An innkeeper knows better than anyone: you do not arrive at someone's table like this.

But the smell of good fat and searing meat drifted under the door, patient, unhurried, and it undid her. On the second evening, she smoothed the apron that could not be smoothed, touched her hair the way she had a thousand years of habit for, and knocked.

"Come in," said a voice. "It's open."

She pushed, and the door swung, and Lydia stepped out of her ruin into lamplight.

---

Warmth. That was the first thing. Not the memory of warmth — warmth, on her skin. Real.

The second thing was the sound of her own footsteps.

She stopped in the doorway and looked down at her feet on the wooden floor. She took one more step, deliberately. It made a sound. Her weight made a sound. She had not made a sound in two hundred years.

"Anywhere you like," the voice said. "Counter's warmest."

The room was small and honest, and it was full of evening light. That was the third strange thing, and she counted it carefully: lamplight with no flame in it. A dozen glass jars glowed the deep gold of a summer sunset, steady, without a single flicker among them.

The fourth strange thing was the front wall. It was one great window, and beyond it there was no street, and no sky. There was a darkness that went down forever. Far out in that darkness hung a root — a root the size of a river, faintly shining, descending past all seeing — and pale motes drifted down around it, slow as snowfall.

So it was true, then, what she had suspected about where this door led. She noted it, and put it away. She was a guest here, and staring is not how a guest behaves.

Eight stools stood at a counter of dark polished wood. To one side, a hearth burned low, two deep chairs drawn up before it — and even from the doorway, she could feel that fire noticing her. She gave it a wide, respectful berth without being told to.

Behind the counter was an open kitchen, and in the kitchen a young man in glasses, sleeves rolled, doing something with a pan that filled the whole room with that smell.

He did not look like death. She had rather expected death, when she was younger, to look grander.

Lydia crossed the floor — listening to every footstep — and sat carefully on the middle stool. The stool held her. The counter was solid under her hands. Her hands were solid. She turned them over slowly in the lamplight and made herself stop, because staring at your own hands is not how a guest behaves.

The young man set a cup of water in front of her. Just water, cold, in plain clay.

She drank it.

She had poured ten thousand cups for other people in her life, and it turned out that after two hundred years, the taste of cold water was almost more than she could bear. She set the cup down with great care, and did not let her face do anything at all. She was an innkeeper. She knew what it looked like when a guest was one kind word away from breaking, and she was not going to be that guest.

"First time?" the young man asked, which struck her as a strange question from a psychopomp, but perhaps they had their manners too.

"Yes," she said. "I— yes. First time."

"Then I'll just bring you what's good, if that's all right."

"That would be very kind."

He turned back to the stove. She watched him work.

Whatever else this place was — and she had her suspicions; she was dead, and a door had opened where no door should be, and there is only one sort of house that receives the dead — whatever else it was, the man knew a kitchen. She had spent a lifetime judging cooks by their backs, and his was a good back: no waste, no hurry, everything reached for on the first try. The pan noise changed and he answered it before it finished changing.

If this was her judgment, she thought, then judgment came with a better cook than she had expected. And a cleaner counter.

The plate, when it came, was simple. Sliced meat off a roast — pork, by the look, though richer and darker than any pork she knew — fanned out with the fat gleaming, a spoonful of something sharp and bright beside it, and pale hearts of some vegetable, stacked in a neat pile. Bread, clearly baked here, still holding its warmth.

"River-boar," the young man said. "House specialty. On account of it being the only specialty."

She did not know what was expected of her. A prayer? A settling of accounts? He seemed to expect nothing at all. He just stood there, easy, the way she had once stood — a host watching a plate land, wanting to know one thing only.

So Lydia picked up the fork, and speared one slice of meat, and ate.

---

There is no good way to describe two hundred years of nothing, so here is the shape of what happened instead.

The fat came first, warm and sweet — genuinely sweet, river-sweet — and it melted before she was ready for it. Then the meat, and the salt. Salt. Of everything she had lost, she had never once thought to miss salt, and it went through her before she was ready, and behind it came the sharp bright sauce, and the whole thing together was so loud and so alive and so *much* —

Lydia put down the fork.

She put both hands flat on the counter and looked at them, because her eyes had begun to blur, and blurring meant tears, and tears meant her body was doing things bodies do, which meant she had a body, which meant —

"It's all right," the cook said quietly. "Take your time."

She had kept an inn all her life and been a ghost for two hundred years, and she wept at a stranger's counter over a slice of pork, and could not stop for a while, and the stranger let her be. He moved to the far end of the counter and busied himself with something that made small unimportant sounds, which is the correct thing to do, and she loved him a little for that. Anyone would have.

Then she picked the fork back up and ate everything.

She ate the meat and the bitter-then-sweet hearts and wiped the sharp sauce with the bread, and when the plate was clean she sat with her hands in her lap while warmth spread through a body she had not owned that morning.

"Seconds?" he asked.

"I couldn't possibly," she said, and then, with total honesty, "Yes."

---

He watched his first guest destroy a second plate of river-boar, and mentally took his hat off to whoever had built her.

Because this — *this* — was content.

Fourteen years he had played this game. He knew its NPCs like he knew his own hands: shopkeepers with three lines, quest-givers with five, the good ones with maybe a dozen and one joke. They were fine. They were furniture that talked.

This one had knocked.

This one had stepped through the door and stopped dead at the sound of her own footsteps, and looked at her feet — a detail no sane developer would bother with — and then smoothly pretended she hadn't. She had drunk plain water and gone somewhere far away for a second. She had cried over the food, silently, with her hands flat on the counter, and then apologized to nobody and cleaned her plate.

He had pulled up her status while she worked on the second helping, out of fourteen years of habit.

**Lydia. Wandering Spirit. Level 101.**

A full status page. A species line. A real level in a real entry, same as any creature in the bestiary. That was commitment. The level itself was the only sloppy part — one-oh-one, in endgame country? The corridor outside his front door spawned things four hundred levels above her. If this innkeeper was meant to be recurring content, somebody should have leveled her for the zone.

It would explain the pathing, too. She had kept a careful distance from the hearth since the moment she walked in. Somebody had clearly flagged her as flammable.

He filed it under *charming design flaw* and refilled her water.

Motion capture, maybe. Some archived actress. Or the endgame system was generating this on the fly, in which case fourteen years of flavor data was not even the most impressive thing hiding in this game's code.

Either way: the completion reward was, apparently, guests worth cooking for. He found this to be an acceptable prize for fourteen years.

"So," he said, taking her second empty plate. "Passing through?"

"In a manner of speaking," she said. "I keep — I kept — an inn. On the other side of your door. It has seen better days."

An innkeeper. Of course. A hospitality NPC, spawned for a restaurant. The system had read his build and matched the content, and that was excellent design.

"Then you'd know," he said, "that I have to ask how the food was."

She folded her hands.

"I ran a kitchen all my life," she said. "I never once served anything that good, and until tonight I would have told you my roast duck was the best on the north road."

"North road, huh."

"It was famous," she said, with the small firmness of someone stating a fact, not a boast. "In its day."

"I believe it." He did. Whoever wrote her would have thought of that.

She looked at the empty plate, and then at him, and he watched her gather herself up to ask the real question. Here we go, he thought. The quest hook. Fourteen years of instinct sat up straight.

"What do I owe you," she said quietly, "and — forgive me — what happens now?"

The second half of that sentence had weight on it. Her eyes were steady in the way of someone braced for an answer. NPC dialogue always had a shape, and the shape of this was: a woman expecting the meal to cost more than money. Terrific writing. He genuinely could not wait to see where this questline went.

But it was two in the morning, and he was a cook, not a quest-giver, so he answered as the restaurant.

"You owe me for one meal — the second plate was on the house, first-guest special. And what happens now is you come back whenever you're hungry." He wiped the counter out of habit. "We're open most nights. Sundays are iffy."

Lydia sat very still.

"I may come back," she repeated.

"Sure. Regulars are the whole point of a place like this."

"Regulars," she said, testing the weight of the word. And then something happened to her face — relief, arriving slowly and then all at once — and for a moment she looked at him with an expression he filed away as *phenomenal animation work*, because no game he had ever played could render gratitude and grief at the same time and get both right.

"Then I will come back," she said.

She paid from a small purse: three copper coins, worn nearly smooth, older than any currency he recognized from any expansion. Prop coins, he assumed. Nice ones. He rang them into the till because it seemed polite to complete the transaction the questline offered.

At the door — her door — she paused, hand on the frame, and turned back.

"May I know the name of this house, and of its keeper?"

"House is called Respawn. It's — a family name, sort of. And I'm—"

He stopped.

Fourteen years, and no one had ever asked him that in a way that mattered. His name floated up — the account name, the true one, chosen at fifteen with the Æ and everything — and he heard himself about to say *Dæmon* out loud, as an adult, to this dignified woman, and could not do it.

"—the Glasses Knight," he said.

There was a pause.

"The Glasses Knight," she repeated, with a gravity the name had absolutely never carried before.

"It's an old nickname. From my guild days. Just — Glasses is fine, honestly."

"Thank you for the meal, Sir Glasses," said Lydia, and bowed the precise, small bow of one professional to a much greater one, and stepped through her door before he could correct any part of that sentence.

The door clicked shut. It did not disappear. It stood there in his back wall, plain and wooden, exactly as impossible as it had been an hour ago.

Devan looked at it for a while.

Then he looked at the three copper coins in his till.

"Best game ever made," he said, to nobody, and went to do the dishes.

---

On the other side, the ruin was as it always was. Moss, and young trees, and the fallen roof open to the stars.

Lydia stood in her broken kitchen for a long moment, feeling the warmth fade out of her hands — the weight leaving, the solidity going back to wherever it was kept. By the time she crossed the common room she was smoke and habit again, the same as she had been for two hundred years.

The same, except.

Behind her, in the kitchen wall, the door stayed. Warm light under it, steady.

*Come back whenever you're hungry,* the keeper of the dead had said. *Regulars are the whole point.*

She had been made entirely out of welcome, once, with no one to spend it on. She had never imagined — in forty years of living or two hundred of lingering — that what came after everything might be a counter, and a lamp, and someone asking if she wanted seconds.

Lydia walked the old paths between the mossy tables, evening rounds, the way she always did.

But now, as she went, she was thinking. The thoughts had a shape she had not felt in a very long time, and it took her most of the night to recognize it.

She was planning tomorrow.

That corner table, for instance — if the young trees were trimmed back, it might almost be respectable. Not that anyone would come. But that had never once stopped her before, and it certainly was not going to stop her now, when she had somewhere to be in the evening.

An inn at her back and a door in her wall. A cook on the other side of it who could use, in her professional opinion, some help around the front of house.

She would eat there a hundred times before she found the courage to offer.

But that is the next part of the story.
