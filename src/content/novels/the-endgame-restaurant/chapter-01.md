The quarterly review meeting took place on the thirty-first floor of a building that did not exist.

The office was virtual, like most offices now. The carpet had a pattern if you looked closely. Outside the windows was a city no one had bothered to finish, because no one ever looked out the windows.

Devan sat in the fourth chair from the door. He always sat there. Nobody else wanted it.

"—which is why," said the section head, "we're calling it a lateral quarter."

Everyone nodded. One avatar nodded four seconds late, then twice more, catching up. Nobody laughed. Lag happened to everyone.

Devan's review this quarter came down to one word: *consistent*. It had been the same word for nine years. There were worse things to be, he supposed.

At six he took the helmet off. That was his whole commute.

His apartment came back around him — the smell of last night's noodles first, then the ceiling, then the window with its narrow slice of real city. He put water on to boil. While he waited, he opened the box that had been sitting by his door for three days. He had wanted to finish the work week first. He was that kind of person.

Inside the box was the new helmet.

The old one rendered sight down to the pore. Sound down to the footstep. Touch well enough that rain was worth standing in. But taste — taste, no machine had ever rendered.

The new generation did. FULL GUSTATORY SUITE, said the box. TASTE EVERYTHING.

The water boiled. Devan made his noodles and ate them standing up, reading the manual. The helmet could reproduce forty thousand distinct flavor compounds, the manual said. The noodles were chicken flavor. They were attempting maybe three.

He washed the cup. Then he sat and looked at the new helmet for a while.

Here is the thing Devan knew, and nobody else on Earth had thought about:

*Worldroot Online* was fourteen years old. And it had simulated flavor from day one. It was right there in the earliest patch notes, if you dug back far enough. Devan had dug. Every beast, every herb, every fish and fruit and mushroom in that whole abandoned world had a complete flavor profile, hand-coded by developers who knew that no player would ever taste any of it.

They built it anyway.

The studio shut down eight years ago. The servers were supposed to go dark six years ago. There was an announcement, a farewell event, a date. The date came and went. The lights stayed on.

Nobody looks closely at a dead game.

And tomorrow was Saturday.

He put the new helmet on.

---

The login screen came up, and then the words:

**WELCOME BACK, DÆMON.**

He had been fifteen when he made this account. The Æ had felt very important at the time.

So a man whose performance review said *consistent* stood alone in his apartment while a dead game welcomed him back with a name out of a prophecy. He had stopped noticing years ago.

Then: dusk.

He logged in at the Amberfall terraces, where he had last logged out. It was the game's most famous view — seven hanging gardens, waterfalls dropping through all of them, golden-hour light that never moved. The endgame patch had frozen it that way, back when the developers turned their dying world into a museum of itself.

A dozen level-one tourists stood at the railing in starter clothes, taking pictures. Free accounts. People came to see where an era had happened, and then they left. Two of them were arguing about whether the falls had always been this color.

They had not. But that was a fourteen-year-old argument, and he did not join it.

He walked past the overlook, past the retired quest-givers who still raised their hands to travelers out of habit. One of them called out to him, the same words it had called out for fourteen years.

"I was an adventurer once."

"I know, Aldous," Devan said, not slowing. "I know."

He reached the far stair. A sign there said UNDERVAULT APPROACH. A system prompt said: *This region is not recommended.*

He started down.

The descent took an hour if you knew the way. Tourists could not survive it. Nothing needed to hunt them — the mountain itself was enough. Cold in the third gallery. Spore-drift on the fungal terraces. The long dark of the river stair, where the handholds were a rumor. A level-one account would not last twenty minutes.

The Undervault had been endgame country once. The last dungeon of the last expansion. It was never patched into a tourist park, so it still made its own weather, and it still spawned its own monsters. The wiki used to claim there were other things too, deeper down. But the wiki was frozen now — a snapshot of what people knew on the day they stopped looking.

He crossed the third gallery with his collar up and his glasses fogging. The glasses were cosmetic. The avatar had worn them for fourteen years. That was a story he was not going to tell anyone.

Then the passage opened up, and there was Lantern Row.

His guild had built this street in the boom years, when a hundred of them practically lived down here. A shopping street at the bottom of the world. Why not? The auction fees topside were robbery, and Wren had wanted somewhere to sell arrows. A dozen storefronts under a vaulted ceiling. Lanterns strung from wall to wall — those gave the street its name.

The fletcher's stall: shuttered. The dye shop: shuttered, its window still stained nine colors. The auction stall still showed its standing offers. WTB whetstone recipes, one said. The man who posted it was a nurse now, in another country, in another life. He had not logged in for three years. The offer had no expiry date. Nobody had ever coded one. It would stand there forever, politely.

Overhead, the guild banner hung the same as always, colors bright. Nothing fades down here. Devan had never decided whether that was the best thing about this place or the worst. The question only came up on the walk in, and the walk in was short.

At the end of the Row, one window was lit.

The Row ended where the world did. Past the last storefront, the street opened onto the Drop — the Undervault's central shaft, a darkness so deep the game had never bothered giving it a bottom. Far out in that darkness hung the Worldroot itself: a root column thicker than a castle tower, glowing faintly, going down and down until it was gone. Spore-snow drifted past it all night long. Some nights it drifted upward.

The shop stood right at that edge. It had been the alchemist's, then storage. Then, four years ago, he had accepted two things: that he was the only one still coming down here, and that he could either be a man alone in a dungeon or a man with a project.

So it became the restaurant.

He had knocked through two walls himself, and glassed the whole front of the shop with still-glass — river water from the ninth level, frozen clear by an ice spell he had simply never ended — so that the counter looked out over the Drop, and the Root, and the falling spore-light. He built the counter out of black riverwood, with a Carpentry skill he had maxed in one summer that people still wrote retrospectives about. Eight stools. An open kitchen behind the counter, because a cook you can watch is a cook you can trust.

In the side wall he had set a hearth, with two deep chairs and a low table, for guests who might someday want to sit a while. The fire in it burned on a single ember taken off the old thermal floors, boss-grade, and it had never once needed feeding. The lamps were his work too: jars of bottled sunset, filled one evening at a time at the Amberfall terraces, so the room kept the light of a golden hour that never ended. A cold-room in back, winter-spelled, always exactly cold enough. And over the door, a hand-carved sign — a joke with exactly one living reader:

RESPAWN.

Nobody had ever eaten here but him. That was not sad. He would have said so, if there had been anyone to ask. There wasn't. So he hung up his coat, lit the stoves, checked the water barrels, and listened to the small sounds his kitchen made in answer.

Best part of his week.

Tonight the menu chose itself. For the first taste — the first taste ever — it had to be the river-boar.

He took the short bow. His class was battle mage. It had been battle mage since the Æ era, with enough support classes stacked behind it to staff a small hospital — the kind of build you end up with when the thing you actually enjoy is other people doing well. The bow belonged to none of it. But when everything is maxed, you use the tool the work deserves.

This work deserved quiet.

---

The river caverns were forty minutes down-gallery. The ceiling there rose into blackness too high for lamplight, and the water ran wide and slow between banks of pale reeds. The reeds did not need light to grow. Fourteen years, and he had never learned what they needed instead. The wiki never said. He liked that there were still things like that.

He found sign within minutes. Reeds stripped in a hand-wide swathe at shin height. Cloven prints in the mud, pressed deep at the toe. A feeding trail.

The river-boar of the Undervault is a tidy animal. It strips a reed bed row by row. It works upstream, so the silt it kicks up drifts away behind it. It digs a fresh wallow every cycle. The game had been made by people who believed a monster should have habits, and Devan had read the old bestiary page so many times he could recite it. *Prefers the roots. Wary of still air.*

Wary of still air. So he wet a finger, found the cave draft, and circled wide to come at the wallow from downwind. He was not in a hurry. Patience is most of hunting. The rest is knowing where to wait.

The boar was feeding at the wallow's edge. Three hundred weight. Silt-gray. Tusks the yellow of old ivory. By the numbers it was a monster, and the tourists upstairs would not have survived its first charge. The numbers had stopped meaning anything to him a decade ago.

He drew, held half a breath, and put the arrow through its heart at thirty paces.

The boar took one step, sighed, and lay down in the reeds.

One arrow. One animal. No fear on either side. He had killed ten thousand things in this world back when killing was the game, and he had not enjoyed it much since the fifth year. What he enjoyed was this part: kneeling by the riverbank in the huge dark, saying the old hunter's thanks he had learned from a village NPC fourteen years ago. Four lines, written by some scriptwriter who was probably in a different industry now.

He said them anyway.

Then the work.

Done right, it took an hour. Bleed it at the river's edge. Open it from vent to sternum, a shallow cut, two fingers under the hide — puncture the gut and you ruin the meat, and hurried men puncture the gut. The organs came away in one warm mass. He kept the heart, and the liver. The old files claimed the liver was a delicacy. No one anywhere, in any world, had ever confirmed it.

He propped the cavity open with a reed stalk to cool. Quartered the carcass at the joints. Wrapped the quarters in waxed cloth he had made himself. Then he washed his arms in river water so cold it was almost a flavor by itself — and caught himself thinking that. *A flavor.*

He smiled at the reeds.

The walk home, loaded, was the same walk as always. It had never once felt long.

---

The loin first. If you have one night and one animal, the loin tells you the most.

He worked with his sleeves rolled and the kitchen quiet. He scored the fat cap in a crosshatch, half a finger deep. He salted it with gray salt from the flats on the ninth level — salt he had harvested himself, ground himself, and cooked with for four years on pure theory, because until tonight there had been no way to taste it.

The pan was black iron. His own casting. Smithing 100.

The knife had been a sword once. A famous one — the kind of drop that whole raids were built around, back when a thousand people wanted it and one got it. He had melted it down years ago and recast it as twenty centimeters of kitchen steel. It could cut anything he asked it to, up to and including the world. Mostly he asked it to slice bread.

He laid the loin in fat-side down and brought the heat up slow, because rendering is not searing. The kitchen filled with a long, patient hiss.

He had cooked this dish two hundred times. He knew its numbers cold — the buff, the duration, the stamina curve. He had plated it for nobody, fed it to the fire, and cooked it again, chasing the one thing the game gave him no way to check.

Whether it was good.

The fat went amber, then gold. He turned the loin, seared the flesh side, basted it twice. He rested it on the board exactly as long as it had cooked. Then he cut one slice from the center, thick as his thumb. Steam rose off it.

Outside the window, the dead street kept its dozen shuttered shops. Upstairs, a hundred tourists photographed a sunset that never ended. None of them knew what was about to happen at the bottom of the world.

Devan ate it.

He stood very still, eyes closed. No one — no player, no developer, no one in the history of anything — had ever tasted this before him.

Then: it needs acid.

The fat was sweeter than the files let on. River-sweet. It wanted something bright against it, and he had a pantry holding fourteen years of unanswered questions. So the night went on. The cave-apple vinegar he had barreled three years ago, opened at last — sharper than advertised. Magnificent. The reed hearts, blanched — bitter at first, then not. The liver, seared and spread on his own bread — the old files had been right, and he stood at the counter at one in the morning, alone, slowly shaking his head.

He cooked, tasted, adjusted, tasted. Somewhere in the small hours he stopped being a man checking answers and became just a cook in his kitchen, working. The lanterns of the dead street burned outside the window the whole time.

He was plating the loin properly — sauce drawn in one stroke, reed hearts stacked in a neat pile, because plating is respect, and respect had been part of the skill all along — when the chime sounded.

He knew every chime this game had. He had heard some of them ten thousand times.

He had never heard this one.

**[Cooking has reached its maximum.]**

He straightened up and looked at the message. Fourteen years. Cooking was his last unmaxed skill — he knew that, but he opened the list and checked anyway. A full column of small gold numbers. Every art this world had to teach, learned.

He had not planned to feel anything about it. What he mostly felt was that the loin was getting cold.

Then the screen changed.

**[Congratulations. You have completed the game.]**

The letters looked exactly the same as every other system message. Later, that was the part he could never explain — nothing about them was different, and still he had sat down slowly on his own counter stool, a guest in his own shop.

**[You may now accept guests from another world in your restaurant.]**

**[Do you accept?]**

Devan stared at the words for a long time. The stoves ticked as they cooled.

*Guests from another world.* So there was one last feature, sleeping in the code for fourteen years, waiting for a completion nobody ever reached. Cross-server visitors, maybe, from some sister game he had never heard of. Or — more likely, knowing these developers — NPCs. Patron NPCs, coded and never spawned. A reward for the one player crazy enough to finish everything. Little scripted regulars, for his restaurant.

He found that he wanted that so badly it surprised him.

"Sure," he said.

**[Registration accepted.]**

**[Please wait for your first guest.]**

The prompt folded away. The kitchen stood quiet. Nothing arrived. Nothing changed. The world did not so much as flicker.

Devan waited a full minute anyway, feeling silly, feeling thirteen again. Then he laughed at himself and got up, because whatever a "first guest" was, the counter wasn't going to wipe itself.

He wiped down the riverwood. He racked the knives. He set tomorrow's stock going on low heat, because bones do their best work while a man is elsewhere. The plated loin went into the cold-room. Whole. Untouched.

If you had asked why he didn't eat it, he would have said he was full. That was mostly true. Underneath, in a place he did not check, there was another reason.

A first guest deserved something to eat.

At the door, coat on, hand on the lamp, he paused.

He left it burning. One lamp, over the door of the last open shop on Lantern Row — one small jar of bottled evening. It would glow for as long as it was needed. He could not have said what it was for. It just felt, tonight, like the right thing to do.

---

Very far away — farther than the tourists, farther than the servers, farther than any distance the wiki had a page for — there was the ruin of an inn, on a road that died two hundred years ago.

The tables had gone to moss long ago. Something patient still swept between them anyway, out of habit.

In the kitchen wall stood a door that had never been there before.

Warm light spilled out from under it.
