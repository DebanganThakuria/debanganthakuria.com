The door opened on warmth and gold light, and a woman in a clean apron.

Quinn had prepared words. Something about being a traveler caught by the snow, something with a false name in it, something dignified. What came out, through lips that had stopped working properly, was:

"I saw the lamp."

"That is what it's for," said the woman. "Come in, child."

Afterward Quinn could not put the next hour in order. There was a chair by a fire, and her back was to the fire, and the heat of it went into her the way water goes into dry ground. There were her boots coming off, ruined, and a sharp intake of breath that was not her own. There were basins of water, first cool, then warm — cool first, on frozen feet; some far-off part of her knew that was right, knew someone here understood cold better than the palace physicians did. There was a blanket that smelled of cedar. There was a cup of broth held to her mouth in a steady hand, a little at a time, no faster.

"Slowly," the woman said. "You've been empty for days. We do not fill you all at once."

There were questions Quinn had been braced for since the laundry court. Who are you. Where from. Why alone, on a dead road, in the first snow.

None of them came.

At some point she was going up a staircase, and she must have been walking badly, because the innkeeper simply gathered her up and carried her — up a full flight, without hurry, without effort, the same way she had carried the basins. Quinn thought, from a great distance: *she is strong.* And then: *the sheets are clean.* And then nothing, for fourteen hours.

---

She woke to sunlight on whitewash, and lay still, and could not remember a morning like it.

The room was small and perfect. A bed, a chair, a washstand, a window. The window glass was the clearest she had ever seen — palace glass rippled and greened; this might as well have been open air. Beyond it, the mountains stood white to their shoulders.

Snow. The pass.

She sat up too fast, and her body reported from a dozen places, and she took the report and got up anyway, because panic does not wait for aches. How long had she slept? A day lost — two? The riders. The pass. She had a schedule; the schedule was the plan; the plan was all she had —

On the chair, folded, lay her clothes: washed, dried, mended. The hem she had torn to bind her boots had been hemmed anew. Her boots stood beneath, cleaned, resoled, the split seams stitched with waxed thread. Beside them: thick wool socks that were not hers.

Quinn sat back down on the bed and looked at the socks for a while.

In the palace she was dressed by three women whose names she knew and whose wages Vantry signed. She had been waited on all her life, and it had never once felt like this, and she was young enough to be ashamed of how much her eyes stung, and old enough to put it away and get dressed.

Downstairs, the common room was empty and immaculate. The long table was scrubbed white. The fire was lit. Fourteen windows let in the mountain morning, and outside the snow lay deep and unmarked on the old road, and there were no other guests, and there was no sound but the fire.

"The pass shut in the night," said the innkeeper, coming through with bread. "It will hold shut a week, maybe two. Sit. Breakfast."

"I—" Quinn stood at the end of the table with her spine straight, because what was coming had to be said standing. "Mistress, I must be honest with you. I cannot pay for this. The bed, the mending — I have four coppers and some small things not worth your taking. If you'll trust me for the rest, I will send payment when I reach my people in the north. I know how that sounds. But you will be paid. I keep accounts."

The innkeeper set down the bread, and looked at her — a long look, unhurried, head to foot. Not unkind. It was the look of a woman counting, and Quinn, who had spent nine days being looked at by Chancellor Vantry, knew the difference at once. Vantry counted what you were worth. This woman counted what you needed.

"Bed and board at the Sweetwater is two coppers a night," she said. "That is the price. It does not change for weather, and it does not change for trouble." A pause. "For guests who are wintering, there is another arrangement. The inn wants a second pair of hands — the linens, the wood, the pots. Work mornings, and your bed and board are met, and we will say no more about accounts."

Quinn had negotiated with the treasury tutors since she was twelve. She knew a constructed kindness when she heard one — a price built downward, on the spot, to fit what a girl with four coppers could pay while keeping her back straight.

She took it anyway. That was the other thing the treasury tutors taught: know when the terms will not get better.

"I accept," she said. "Though I should tell you — I have never scrubbed a pot."

"No," agreed the innkeeper, in a voice that had noticed her hands days ago. "You'll learn. Sit. Breakfast first. In this house, work is not done empty."

The bread was still warm. There was butter, and honey, and a soft-boiled egg in a wooden cup, and broth. Plain food, guest-house food — and Quinn, who had eaten at state tables all her life, off gold, under chandeliers, ate a boiled egg in an empty inn with snow at the windows and had to stop, twice, and breathe, because nothing in nineteen years had ever tasted like this, and she did not have the words yet for why.

The why was simple, though it would take her weeks to find it.

It was the first meal of her life that no one could have poisoned.

---

"Your name, for the book," said the innkeeper, when the plates were cleared. "The inn keeps a ledger. It needn't be the whole of your name. It needn't," a small pause, level and deliberate, "even be yours. But the Sweetwater writes its guests down."

The pause told Quinn everything and asked her nothing. She stood at the counter with her heart going hard, and reached for the name she had rehearsed on the road — a merchant's daughter, a plain name from a market town.

What came out was:

"Merta."

She heard herself say it and could not take it back and did not want to. The innkeeper wrote it without comment, in a hand so fine the treasury clerks would have wept: *Merta. Wintering. Works mornings.*

Above it, at the head of the new page — the first entry in the book, alone — stood a line of writing Quinn could not read. Not northern script, not the old liturgical hand, not the merchants' cipher her tutors had made her learn. Sharp, clean, foreign characters, set down with total confidence, and beneath them, smaller, in the same unknown letters, a second line.

"Your other winterer?" Quinn asked, before she could stop herself.

"A traveler," said the innkeeper, and closed the book, and that was the end of that.

---

Lydia, for her part, had known five things about the girl before the door finished opening.

The boots were city-made and a month old. The hands had never carried anything heavier than a book. The cloak was wool bought for its plainness by someone who did not know how to buy plain wool — the weave was too fine; it had cost triple what it pretended to. The hair had been cut in one stroke, recently, by someone in a hurry, and sold, because a girl running does not keep what she can sell. And she had knocked — at the end of her strength, half-dead on her feet, on an unlocked door — and waited to be let in.

Gently born. Hunted. Honest about her debts, ashamed of her hands, and polite to the bone even three days past her last meal.

Two hundred years, Lydia thought, and the road had sent her a good one.

Who the girl was, she did not ask, and did not need to. The Sweetwater had kept travelers' names out of its mouth for two hundred years before the roof fell, and would keep them for two hundred more. An inn that gossips is an inn that closes. But she marked, without appearing to, the way the girl sat facing doors. The way she flinched, once, at hoofbeats that turned out to be snow sliding off the roof. The way she had said *my people in the north* with the smallest catch, the catch of a plan spoken aloud for the first time.

Someone was looking for this child. Someone with riders.

That evening, after the girl slept, Lydia stood in her doorway a long moment, looking down the white road toward the third bend and the boundary stone, her boundary stone, the edge of everything that was hers.

Let them come up her road, if they liked. Let them come with their gray coats and their warrants, up the old road, past the stone, onto the innkeep's land, after her guest.

The fire in the great hearth stood up a little, all by itself, and settled.

She went in, and banked it for the night, and swept the day's snow from the doorstep, the way her mother had, the way her grandmother had, so that the step would be clean for whoever the lamp found next.

---

The days fell into a pattern.

Mornings, the girl worked. Badly at first — she scorched the first linens and flooded the scullery once, and apologized in the stiff, over-complete way of someone unaccustomed to being allowed mistakes — then less badly, then, within the week, with the particular stubborn thoroughness of a mind that has decided to be good at a thing out of spite against its own softness. She scrubbed pots to win. Lydia, who had trained more chambermaids than she could count, kept her face still and privately awarded her the highest grade in the ledger of her mind: *does not do things halfway.*

The ducks adopted her. She fed them mornings, and they escorted her from task to task afterward, and she talked to them when she believed herself unobserved — in the low, practical voice of a girl briefing her council.

Afternoons, the girl slept, or sat at the window watching the road, or read the map she thought Lydia had not seen, measuring with her finger the passes to the north.

Evenings, they ate together at the end of the long table, because a table of two does not stand on ceremony in a snowed-in house. The girl was fed up properly, meal by meal — color coming back, the wrists losing their sharpness — and she talked, a little more each night, in careful merchant's-daughter sentences with holes where a life should be. Lydia poured the tea and did not step in any of the holes.

But she noticed the girl noticing.

The glass, first — every window flawless, on a mountain, at a ruin's prices. The lamp by the door that burned gold every night and took no oil. The bread that was better than any bread had a right to be. The innkeeper who was awake when the girl slept and awake when she woke, who carried full water barrels one-handed, who was never cold, who kept a nine-room inn spotless alone and unwearied.

And the kitchen door. The plain wooden door in the kitchen's back wall, which did not match the stone around it, and which the innkeeper kept always closed, and past which — the girl's face said, one evening, having heard something — there was, just for a moment, the sound of a street.

"Storage," said Lydia.

"Of course," said the girl called Merta, princess of the realm, guest of the house, scrubber of pots, and looked back down at her tea, and did not believe her at all.
