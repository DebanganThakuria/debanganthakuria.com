Chancellor Vantry rose at five, as he had every morning for thirty years.

He worked two hours before the palace woke. He answered letters in his own hand, because a man who lets clerks answer his letters lets clerks know his business. He ate one egg and one slice of bread, standing. Then he put on gray — he had worn gray since he was a junior secretary, and had made it mean something in Averen, the way a uniform means something — and went down to hold the kingdom together.

That was how he thought of it. Men who called him a schemer had never sat where he sat. For three years, while the king faded, someone had signed the grain contracts, paid the garrisons, answered Veyland's envoys, kept the river tolls flowing and the valley lords from each other's throats. The someone was him. History would not thank him. History thanked kings.

He had made his peace with that. Mostly.

The winter council met at ten.

---

They came in by rank, and the hall filled with the kingdom.

The two dukes first. Ambrose of Coldmere, gouty and ancient, who wanted nothing but his fire and his wine and had to be carried up the steps. Hallern of the West Reach, older still, pious, half-deaf, who wanted nothing but a good death. Between them they held a third of Averen and the two strongest claims to a regency, and neither would take it, and Vantry had built three years of careful work on that fact.

Then the counts. Esler of the river lands, the richest man below the throne, who watched everything and committed to nothing. Damar of Reedmark, loud, indebted, easily steered. The counts of the middle shires in a cluster, furred and jeweled, each with a son of marrying age and each with the same question behind his teeth.

Then the barons — a dozen of them with winter grievances, Tolley of the toll pass at their front, still smarting that his garrison had been made to answer questions about a runaway girl in autumn and had nothing to show for it.

Then the crown's own men. Marshal Bors, who filled a doorway and distrusted rooms like this one. General Corren of the capital garrison, young, correct, careful — Vantry's work, that career, and worth every favor. Lord Withy of the treasury, with his folios held to his chest. Lord Cartwell of the works, with his surveys. Archmagus Tull, sixth circle, in robes older than some of the barons, leaning on a staff he did not need indoors.

And by the far pillar, in plain wool, Mistress Crane, who held no rank the heralds could announce and heard everything said in the kingdom, usually twice.

Vantry took his place at the foot of the empty throne. Not on it. Never on it. The empty chair did more work for him than he could ever do sitting in it.

"My lords," he said. "Winter business."

---

Winter business ran two hours. Grain stores, adequate. River ice, early. Veyland's envoy wintering in the capital, asking polite questions about succession law that Vantry answered politely and reported to no one. A border baron's quarrel over a bridge. The council droned, and Vantry conducted, and the kingdom was held together in front of him, item by item.

Then Count Esler folded his hands, and Vantry knew what was coming, because Esler never spoke before the small business was done.

"The princess," said Esler. "Four months now, Chancellor. My question is not where she is — I accept that grief wants privacy. My question is legal. If the king's health should turn, Averen has an heir no one can produce. The succession cannot rest on a convent with no name."

The murmur ran the length of the table. There it was: the question every count had carried in, wearing its most respectable clothes.

"Her Highness writes that her health improves," said Vantry, with exactly the right weight of tired patience. "I will not drag a grieving girl back to court to satisfy the calendar. When she is fit to return, she will return."

"And the offers of marriage?" That was Damar, too loud. "My son—"

"—will be considered alongside every other son in this room," said Vantry, and got the laugh, and used it to move on.

He moved on to the marshal, which was a mistake, but the agenda was the agenda.

"Garrisons," said Bors, in his parade voice, and the walls got some of it too. "The northern forts are at half strength for a third winter. I am told it is economy. I have buried men in the passes to teach Averen what a half-strength fort costs. I want them manned by thaw."

"The treasury—" Withy began, faintly.

"The treasury can find it," said Bors, not looking at him. "The treasury found enough to gild the new customs house."

"Noted, Marshal," said Vantry, and made a note, and the note said *watch him.* Bors had been growling for a year. Growling was nothing. But Crane had brought him a small thing a week past — the March was buying oats, more oats than the March had horses — and small things in the north had a way of belonging to each other. He drew a thin line under the note.

"Roads," he said. "Lord Cartwell."

Cartwell shuffled his surveys and cleared his throat and reported the state of the winter roads at a length that emptied faces up and down the table. At the end of it, almost apologetically, he added:

"One oddity, Chancellor. The old Sweetwater road — the dead one, over the shoulder pass. A carter in the foothills has been telling anyone who will stand still that he saw lights up there. An inn, lit and open, where the survey has a ruin. He is not the first, apparently. There are — stories." He blinked at his own papers. "I mention it only because the survey office is asked to keep its maps honest."

Somewhere down the table a baron laughed. "Ghost inns now. My grandmother knew that road. The Sweetwater is forty years of moss."

"Two hundred," said Cartwell, mildly.

"Thank the survey office for its diligence," said Vantry, already turning the page. A dead road with a folk tale on it, in a winter with real problems in it. "Next—"

"I would hear more of that," said Archmagus Tull.

The old mage had not spoken all morning. The table went quiet, the particular quiet of men who have all privately wondered whether the Archmagus still counted, being reminded that he did.

"More of what?" said Vantry.

"Of anything," said Tull, "that is where it cannot be." He turned to the council, and Vantry watched him decide something, and disliked it. "Since we are on the subject of things that cannot be. My lords, in autumn I was asked to scry for Her Highness — a formality, an old man reassuring a worried court. I have now performed that scrying eleven times. Blood-kin bond, her own possessions, correct conditions. Textbook conditions." He set his staff across his knees. "Eleven times, the Art has returned nothing. Not death — death reads clearly; I have read it all my life. Not distance; distance dims, it does not blind. Something between my art and that girl turns the scrying aside, and it does so gently, without effort, the way a roof turns rain."

"Meaning what, Archmagus?" said Esler, very still.

"Meaning," said Tull, "that in the whole of Averen there is no circle that could do it. I could not do it. Meaning Her Highness sits behind wards our academies do not possess — or in a place our maps do not hold. She is alive; the bond tells me that much, and it is the only thing it tells me." He looked down the table, old and unhurried. "Someone is sheltering the princess of Averen, my lords. Someone with power I cannot name. I thought the council should know what its mage does not."

The hall broke into voices.

Vantry sat at the foot of the empty throne and let the noise spend itself, and behind his stillness the columns would not sum, and would not sum, and would not sum.

---

He kept Crane back when the hall emptied.

They understood each other, he and Crane. He signed her budgets; she told him what the kingdom said in its sleep. If she also kept a file on him — and she kept a file on everyone; that was what she was for — then it was a file of clean accounts. He had made sure of that for thirty years.

"The convent story is done," Crane said, without preamble. "It held four months. It will not hold to thaw. The counts' men are asking at every convent in the west; two houses have already denied her in writing. And the ghost stories are breeding. The palace has one now — the servants feed it bread, if you can credit that. A kind ghost, that walks at night and mends the king." Her voice put no weight on any of it. Crane's voice never put weight anywhere. "And the carter's inn has a dozen tellings in the foothills already. Lamplight on a dead road. A keeper who does not age. In some versions there is a door that opens where it likes."

"Peasants have told that story for a thousand years."

"They have," Crane agreed. "It is being told more."

Vantry stood at the window. Below, the capital smoked in its river fog, roof by roof by roof — his city, in every way that mattered, held together by his hand for three years without a word of thanks, which was the arrangement, which he had accepted.

The king was better. That was the fact under all the other facts. Steadier hands. The spring hunt. A laugh in the small court, once, that had gone through the household staff like a change of season. The physician swore the regimen was unchanged. The apothecary swore the flasks left full and came back empty. Every column in that ledger was correct, and the total was wrong, and a man does not survive thirty years of treasuries without learning what that means.

It means someone else is writing in your book.

"Chancellor?" said Crane, from the door.

"Send someone up the old road," said Vantry. "Someone sensible. If there is an inn, I want its keeper's name. If there is nothing, I want the carter's tongue quieter." He kept his eyes on the fog. "And Crane. The princess's chambers. Have the seals on that door checked."

"They were checked at the new year."

"Have them checked again."

She went. Vantry stood at the window a while longer, patient, gray, alone with a city that did not thank him, and found that for the first time in three years he was thinking about the shape of the winter rather than the items in it. Somewhere in his kingdom a door had come open. He could not see it, count it, or accuse it.

But he could feel the draft.
