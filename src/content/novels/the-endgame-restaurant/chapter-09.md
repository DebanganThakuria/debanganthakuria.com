On the seventh morning, the storage cupboard said good morning to her.

Quinn was at the scullery sink, sleeves rolled, working on the porridge pot. The kitchen was warm and bright and smelled of the second baking. Behind her, a latch clicked. The door in the back wall opened — the door that did not match its stone, the door that was storage, of course, storage — and with it came the sound of another place.

A young man stepped out, carrying an empty basket, whistling.

He wore glasses. His clothes were plain, but wrong — cut in no fashion of the kingdom, or of any kingdom she had maps of. He looked entirely at home.

He saw her mid-whistle and stopped.

"Oh," he said. "Guest."

Quinn stood with a scrub brush in one hand. Nineteen years of etiquette training produced nothing at all.

"Master," said Lydia, arriving from the common room with no surprise at all, "this is Merta, wintering with us, working mornings. Merta — the traveler I spoke of. He keeps a cook-shop, some way off. He comes through on Saturdays."

"Through," said Quinn faintly, looking at the door. Through the wall. Through the mountain, then, and out the other side into — she had heard it again just now, before the door shut. A street. Far-off voices. Weather that was not this weather.

"It's a shortcut," the young man said, and seemed to think that settled it.

"A shortcut," Quinn agreed, with great care.

---

Devan, for his part, was delighted.

A second NPC at the inn. The settlement questline was generating population — first the innkeeper, now a winterer, and he hadn't even had to build anything. He pulled her status out of pure habit while she stood there holding the scrub brush.

**Quinn. Human. Level 7.**

And there it was. The tooltip said Quinn. She had been introduced as Merta.

He kept his face straight. Fourteen years of games had trained him for exactly this. A disguised NPC — real name in the data, false name in the dialogue. The oldest flag in the book. Somewhere down this questline there would be a reveal: a lost heir, a runaway noble, a witch's daughter. He had played this story a dozen times. It was a good story every time. The correct move — the only respectful move — was to play along and let it unfold at its own pace.

"Merta," he said gravely, and gave her a small bow. "Pleasure."

The girl blinked at the bow.

It was too deep. A cook greeting a scullery girl does not bow like that. It was about the right depth for a princess — though he would not learn that for a long time.

Quinn went to bed that night quietly certain the strange traveler knew everything, and was being courteous about it.

They had breakfast, the three of them, at the end of the long table. It was the strangest meal of Quinn's life to that point, a record it would hold for several hours. The traveler asked her what she thought of the mountain. Whether the snow was heavy this year. Whether porridge was better with honey or without. He listened to every answer with his whole attention. No one had listened to her that way in her life. Courtiers listened for openings.

This man just listened.

And Lydia deferred to him. Lydia, who deferred to nothing. Poured his tea first. Called him master. Watched him butter bread with settled satisfaction.

The innkeeper strong as three men. The impossible windows. The unreadable name in the ledger. The traveler who came through the mountain on Saturdays.

Quinn scrubbed her pot, kept her face pleasant, and stacked the facts one on another. She did not like the shape of the stack. She could not stop looking at it.

After breakfast, Lydia folded her hands.

"Master. A liberty. The girl has been a week on bread and broth and my plain table, and she has weeks of winter left in her." A pause. "I would bring her to the restaurant. She should eat something good."

"Lydia," said the traveler, wounded. "Your table is not plain."

"It is honest," said Lydia. "Yours is *good*. May I bring her?"

"Of course. Tonight. I'll do something proper." He was already standing, already reaching for the basket, already planning a menu in his head. At the storage door he paused and looked back at Quinn, and behind the glasses his eyes were bright.

"First time's the best time," he said. "Come hungry."

The door closed on the sound of a street.

Quinn looked at Lydia. Lydia looked back, entirely serene, a woman who had said all she intended to.

"Mistress," said Quinn carefully, "where exactly is the restaurant?"

"Some way off," said Lydia, and went to the linens.

---

They went through after dark.

Lydia opened the storage door without ceremony. Lamplight came out of it, and the smell came out to meet her — searing meat, broth, bread, woodsmoke. No fear could argue with it.

Quinn stood on the threshold with her heart loud in her ears. Her nurse's tales stood there with her. Doors in walls. Tables of the patient dead. Eat nothing, her nurse had said. They that eat there, stay.

Then again: her nurse had also said the mountain lights took travelers. The mountain light had taken her in, dried her boots, and taught her to scrub pots.

"Guests first," said Lydia.

Quinn breathed in once, gave her back its full court posture, and stepped through a wall.

Warmth first. Then gold light, everywhere — the same steady, flameless gold as the traveler's lamp jar. A long, low room. A counter of dark wood, shining. Eight stools. A hearth to one side, with deep chairs. An open kitchen, copper and black iron and rising steam, and in it the traveler, sleeves rolled, entirely in his element.

And the far wall was glass, and beyond the glass was the end of the world.

Darkness — not night; night has a sky. Darkness going down and down past reckoning. Far out in it hung a shining column, pale gold, wider than any tower ever raised, descending until sight gave out. Snow that was not snow drifted past the windows. Some of it drifted upward.

Every tale her nurse had ever told settled into one certainty: whatever this place was, no map of the living had it.

"Sit at the counter," said Lydia, hanging up her cloak, tying her apron, entirely at home in the underworld. "The counter is warmest."

Quinn sat. The stool was solid. The counter was solid. Ahead of her, close enough to touch, the cook worked — and she found, watching him, that her fear had nowhere to stand.

Because she could see everything.

That was the thing. That was the impossible thing, more impossible than the window. The kitchen was open. The pot was open. He ladled broth in front of her. He seasoned it in front of her. He tasted it in front of her with a small spoon, frowned, and adjusted. Every motion in plain sight, six feet from her eyes.

Nineteen years she had eaten food that arrived from behind doors, carried by hands she had to trust, tasted first by a woman who had died of the arrangement.

Nobody could do anything to this food. She would have seen.

"House specialty," said the cook, setting it before her. "We call it ramen."

Noodles, gold broth, dark slices of some rich roast, a soft egg, green things she had no name for. Steam on her face. Lydia, beside her, picked up chopsticks and looked at her sidelong.

"There is an etiquette," said the innkeeper, with total gravity. "The noise is a compliment to the cook."

The princess of the realm ate ramen at a counter at the bottom of the world. She slurped. Partway through, she gave up on comparisons. The palace kitchens had never made anything like this, because nothing in her world tasted like this. That was simply a fact. She was too busy to weep about it.

It was a near thing.

She drank the broth to the bottom, with both hands. She'd been told that was polite.

"So," said the cook, refilling her water. "Lydia says you're for the north, when the pass opens. That's a hard road in winter." He said it lightly, no hook in it anywhere. "Family up there?"

Quinn was warm, and fed, and safer than she had ever been at any table in her life.

She heard herself begin to talk.

She kept the names out. She was not a fool. A merchant house, she said, in the south. A father, ill. A man of business who had made himself indispensable, whose accounts no one else could untangle, who was patient. A cup — she said cup, not plums — meant for her, that a serving woman drank. An aunt in the north, with means, who did not yet know any of it. A pass, shut, and weeks bleeding away while the patient man sat in her house with her father's seal in his keeping.

It came out level and orderly, a report. She had practiced it in her head on the road. Only once did it catch — at the serving woman — and she took a breath and finished the sentence, because the serving woman deserved a finished sentence.

The room was quiet. The fire ticked. Lydia's hand rested on the counter, near hers, not touching.

"Okay," said the cook.

He said it in the voice of a man who had seen the whole problem. Then he wiped his hands, reached under the counter, and set something down in front of her.

A card. Dark, heavy, the size of a playing card, with a grain to it that was not quite wood and not quite stone. On its face, in inlaid gold, the unreadable letters again — the ledger letters, the sign letters — and under them a small image: a door, ajar, with light coming through the gap.

"House membership," he said. "Two rules and a trick. Trick first." He nodded at the front door of the shop. "A cardholder can open a door to this restaurant from anywhere. Any wall, any door, anywhere in your world, any hour. Knock or don't; it opens for you. That's the way in." He turned the card over once, showing the back, plain dark. "The way out: from here, a cardholder can step through to any place she's been before. Any place your own two feet have stood. Name it at the door and walk."

Quinn looked at the card and did not touch it.

Any place she had been.

She had stood in Marchhold's winter hall at nine years old, holding her mother's hand, watching her aunt laugh by a fire the size of a wagon. She had stood in her own bedchamber in the palace every night of her life until twelve days ago.

The pass did not matter. The snow did not matter. The riders, the toll gates, the descriptions — none of it mattered anymore. The whole map of her flight collapsed. She sat very still while a plan of weeks rebuilt itself into a plan of days, and her heart went so hard she was sure they could hear it.

"The rules," said the cook, holding up one finger, and his voice stayed light but something under it did not. "One: pay your bill. Two: the house is not a weapon. You come here as a guest, you leave as a guest, and what you carry through these doors is yourself and nothing else. No soldiers. No knives meant for other people's backs. The card serves guests. The moment it's serving a war instead—" he turned his hand over, "—it goes blank. No warning, no appeal. It's not my rule, exactly. It's just how it works. The door decides."

"The door decides," Quinn repeated slowly.

"House rules," said Lydia, in the voice of scripture.

"How much," said Quinn. She was her mother's daughter. Her hands wanted the card so badly she had folded them.

"For the card? Standard price. One gold, or a winter's honest work, whichever you have." He glanced at Lydia. "She's been working mornings, you said?"

"She scrubs pots to win," said Lydia. "The inn will stand for her."

"There you go. The inn stands for you. Paid."

Quinn looked between them. The cook had just handed her the keys to her whole war, for the price of scrubbed pots. The innkeeper had stood surety without blinking. She understood, clearly, that she had no idea what was happening — that she had had no idea since the third bend of the old road. And she understood that these two, whatever they were, had just changed the fate of her kingdom at a wooden counter, over noodles.

Court had taught her many speeches for many debts. She used none of them.

"Thank you," she said.

"Come hungry, pay honest, mind the rules," said the cook, and went to do his dishes.

---

She did not sleep that night.

She sat at the window of her small perfect room with the card in both hands, turning it over, watching the snow come down on a pass that no longer had any power over her. She planned in the treasury tutors' columns: assets, movements, days.

Marchhold first. Her aunt. Five hundred lances, and a winter hall she had stood in at nine years old.

Then the palace. A patient man in her father's house, sleeping soundly behind guards he had chosen. A man who believed the princess was dying somewhere on a mountain — in a ditch, under snow.

And her own bedchamber was in that palace. She had stood inside it every night of her life.

"The door decides," she said softly — to the card, to the snow, to the patient man far in the south who did not know what was coming toward him through the walls of the world.

She was smiling. It was her mother's smile, the one from the portraits, and it had lances in it.
