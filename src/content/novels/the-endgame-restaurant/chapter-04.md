The stock came ready on the third night.

He knew it before he tasted it. Good stock announces itself — the surface goes glossy and calm, the steam turns rich, and the whole kitchen starts to smell the color gold. Eighteen hours of boar bones, then twelve more because the bones had more to say. He strained it through cloth, twice, and what came through was pale and cloudy and heavy in the ladle.

Then he made noodles.

Flour from the old farm terraces, up in the tourist country, milled himself. Water from the ninth-level salt flats — mineral water, sharp with something the wiki had never named. He had used that water in bread for years because bread liked it. Tonight he learned what it was actually for. Kneaded into noodle dough, it turned the noodles yellow and springy and strong, and somewhere far away, in another world entirely, a hundred ramen masters would have nodded, because he had reinvented kansui in a cave at the bottom of a dead game.

He rolled the dough thin, cut it fine, boiled a first portion for exactly as long as instinct said, and built a bowl. Broth. Noodles. Slices of the loin, seared fresh. A spoonful of rendered fat, gleaming on the surface. Reed hearts for green.

He ate standing at the counter, and for one moment the first bowl of ramen in the history of two worlds made him forget both of them.

Then he pulled up the second portion ten seconds earlier, because the noodles drank the broth faster than they had any right to, and the bowl wanted an egg he did not yet have, and perfection was a direction, not a place.

His gray life ate noodles from a cup. The cup was attempting three flavor compounds. This bowl had all forty thousand, and he had made it in his sleep.

He wrote *eggs?* on the slate by the cold-room, and started the next batch.

---

Lydia knocked at her usual hour.

She had come every night that week. She took the middle stool, folded her hands, and regarded the bowl he set before her with the caution of a professional being shown a competitor's work.

"Noodles," he said. "New on the menu. You eat them with these."

She held the chopsticks correctly on the second try, which he filed away as excellent adaptive scripting.

"One more thing," he said. "Where this dish comes from, slurping is polite. The noise is a compliment to the cook."

Lydia looked at him for a long moment, weighing whether he was making fun of her. He was not. Nobody in the restaurant business jokes about compliments to the cook.

So she bent her head, and slurped, with the grave thoroughness she brought to everything.

She was quiet for a while after the first mouthful. Then she said, "In forty years behind a counter, I never once served anything I did not understand." She looked into the bowl. "I do not understand this."

"That's the broth. It takes thirty hours."

"Thirty hours," she repeated softly, in the voice of a woman revising her opinion of the afterlife upward.

She finished everything, drank the last of the broth from the bowl with both hands — he told her that was polite too, and this time it was even true — and paid five coppers, and would not hear argument.

---

By the sixth night, she was working. Neither of them had discussed it.

It began with small things. She stacked her own dishes, then his. She straightened the stools on her way out. She refolded the cloths by the basin because his folding, she said, wasted a corner. When he was deep in the kitchen, she watched the room for him — and told him, precisely, that the hearth needed nothing, that the corner table's chair had a loose peg, that a draft came under the front windows when the spore-snow fell heavy.

She was, in short, running his front of house from the middle stool, and she was better at it than he was.

The dawn always interrupted them. His dawn — the hour when the alarm in another world crept close, and he would wipe his hands and say the thing he always said: *I'll retire to the back room. Take your time finishing.* And by the next evening he would be back, and she would knock, and neither asked the other where they went.

On the sixth night, when he said it, Lydia did not nod.

She set down her cloth — she had somehow acquired a cloth; he had no idea from where — and stood very straight, and said:

"Master cook. Before you retire. I wish to speak about the house."

He turned down the stove and gave her his attention, because her voice had the weight of something rehearsed in an empty room.

"You keep this house alone," she said. "You cook, you serve, you wash, you keep the books — badly; I have seen the till — and when you retire, the house sits empty and dark until you return. An empty house at the bottom of a place with monsters in it." Her chin came up a little. "I kept an inn all my life. I know the work. I am asking you to let me keep yours, in the hours you cannot."

The kitchen was quiet. The stoves ticked.

Inside Devan, fourteen years of games stood up and applauded.

*A settlement mechanic.* Of course. Complete the game, unlock the restaurant, and the content builds itself: first a guest, then a regular, then the regular asks for a job. The innkeeper NPC was a companion hire. There was probably a whole staff roster sleeping in the code — he could have wept at the design of it.

"Yes," he said. "Absolutely. Hired."

Her hands, folded in front of her apron, tightened slightly. That was all. A lifetime behind a counter teaches a face to keep its own books.

"Then I—"

"One problem," said Devan, because his professional brain had finally caught up, and the professional brain had seen her status page.

Level 101.

He looked around the room he had built. The hearth that could unmake her. The knife, asleep in its drawer, that did not know the difference between a carrot and a soul. The cold-room's deep shelf, where things from the lower floors waited in a cold that was not entirely temperature. The corridor outside the front door, where the Undervault went about its old business at level five hundred and up.

A house made of endgame. She would be working inside a machine that could kill a raid party, and she was level one-oh-one, and the game had clearly shipped this questline expecting him to fix that, because games do not give you a companion you cannot use.

"Before you start," he said, "there's a meal."

"A meal."

"House policy." He was already moving toward the back stairs, down toward storage. "Staff eat before the shift."

It was a good policy. He had invented it entirely just now.

---

The storage room held fourteen years.

Racks and chests, floor to ceiling, the loot of a finished world — armor no one would wear again, weapons outgrown by their own wielder, materials for recipes he had learned and mastered and shelved. He walked the aisles with a lamp, going by memory, to a small iron chest in the far corner that he had not opened in five years.

Inside, alone on a cloth, sat a jar. And in the jar was a light.

Pale, cold, patient light, the light of deep water with a moon somewhere above it. It did not flicker. It had never flickered.

**[Soul of the Hollow King]**
**[Material — Unique]**
**[No known use.]**

The Hollow King had been the drowned lord of the flooded galleries, the raid at the bottom of the game's last expansion. The raid was never cleared while the game lived — the guilds broke on it, the players drifted away, the servers were scheduled to die, and the King sat undefeated in the dark, which is its own kind of victory.

In the ninth year, alone, out of respect, Devan had gone down and finished the fight. It took him a winter. At the end there was no cutscene — the game had none left to give — only the King coming apart into cold light, and this jar, and a tooltip with no answer in it.

*No known use.* Five years it had said that.

He picked up the jar, and the light turned in it slowly, and he thought: everything in this game had a use. The devs carved the backs of statues. They coded flavor no one would taste. They did not make a unique material with no purpose — they made a key, and left it lying in the dark until the game finally produced its lock.

A soul, edible by exactly one kind of guest.

"Found it," he said to the tooltip. "Took me a while."

---

He built her bowl the same as his own. Broth, noodles, loin, fat, the green of reed hearts. Then he opened the jar.

The light came out slowly, the whole shape of it, and lay on the surface of the broth. For a moment it held there, a coin of drowned moonlight on the gold. Then it went through — not sinking, exactly; accepted — and the broth turned the deep clear color of open water at evening, and the steam came up silver.

He set the bowl in front of Lydia.

"Staff meal," he said. "Eat it while it's hot."

---

She knew, before the first spoonful, that this was not the same dish.

The steam did not rise off it the same. The bowl sat heavier in front of her than a bowl should sit — church-door heavy, winter-night heavy, a weight the hands feel without touching. And her employer stood there, easy, sleeves rolled, watching with nothing on his face but a cook's ordinary interest in a customer's first bite.

She had suspicions about what this house was. She had held them since the first night, and kept them folded away, because a guest does not pry. Now she was staff, and staff see more, and what she saw was: the keeper of the house of the dead had taken something out of deep storage, and it had a light inside it, and he had given it to her in a bowl.

An innkeeper knows what it costs to serve the good bottle. The one from the back of the cellar. The one you were saving.

She did not ask what it was. She bent her head, and ate.

The first mouthful was broth, and it was wonderful, and that was all. The second was noodles, and the warmth of them went into her hands and did not leave. She noticed that. The warmth of this place always faded from her the moment things cooled — heat passed through her the same as lamplight, borrowed, never kept.

This warmth stayed.

The third mouthful, the room grew louder. Not noise — depth. The fire's crackle gained its low notes back. The stove's tick became a dozen small ticks, each one distinct. She had not known her hearing had thinned. There had been nothing to compare it to, for a very long time.

She put down the spoon. She picked it up again. She kept eating, faster now, not from hunger — from the feeling, rising in her, of a tide coming in over land that had been dry so long it had forgotten it was a harbor.

Her edges filled.

There was no better way she could have put it. For two hundred years there had been a thinness at the rim of her, everywhere, always — she had stopped feeling it the year the last roof-beam fell, the thinness of a letter read so many times the folds go transparent. And now, spoonful by spoonful, quietly, the paper thickened. The folds closed. The writing came back dark.

Names returned to her.

Her regulars. All of them, in order of their usual arrival — the carter who came at dusk, the wool-buyer and his silent brother, the ford-keeper's daughter who married the mason, whose wedding had filled the yard, whose name was — *Ivett.* Ivett. Two hundred years that name had been a smooth place worn in cloth, and now it sat in her mind, whole, with a face attached, and Lydia bent over the finest meal ever cooked in any world and wept into it for the second time in ten days, and kept eating, because she was not going to let it go cold, not this bowl, not if the world ended.

When it was done she drank the broth with both hands, to the last, the polite way.

She set the bowl down. The room held still around her, deep and warm and loud with small sounds, and none of it was fading.

"Thank you for the food," she said, when she could.

---

Devan, who had watched his level-101 hire eat a level-940 raid boss with tears running down her face, checked her status out of courtesy.

**Lydia. House Spirit. Level 941.**

The species line had updated itself. He looked at that for a second — *House Spirit* — and decided it was the companion system assigning her class. The level he had expected; consumables that grant levels were rare, but they existed, and the system had clearly designed the whole loop. Feed the unique material to the unique hire. Elegant. The crying had been a lot, animation-wise, but the endgame did nothing halfway.

"Right," he said. "Orientation."

She stood, and smoothed her apron, and there was something new in the way she stood — nothing showy. A door standing in its frame the first day the hinges hang true.

"Three rules for the house," Devan said, counting them off. "One: the knife in the flat drawer — that one stays mine for now. It cuts what it's told, and it takes instructions very literally. Two: the deep shelf in the cold-room, behind the cloth — nothing there is for guests. Or for touching. Three—" he nodded at the side wall, "—the hearth. It needs banking at close and stirring at open, and up to tonight it would have been the death of you, so I handled it. That's changed. Go ahead."

Lydia looked at the fire.

She crossed the room to it — the fire she had walked around, all this time, along the polite outer curve of its attention — and knelt, and took the iron, and banked the coals of a boss-grade ember with her bare hand steadying the grate.

The fire held still for her. Heat lay against her skin and stayed there, hers now, kept.

She remained kneeling a moment longer than the work needed. If the master of the house understood what he had done — fed her a king so the fire could not take her — he gave no sign, and she understood that too. The old stories agreed on very little about the keepers of the dead, but they agreed on this: the great ones never explain, and you do not thank them past the once.

She stood, and returned the iron to its hook, and turned to the matter of terms.

"Wages," she said.

"Name them."

"Meals, the room above the shop if it can be cleared, and two silver a month."

"Five."

"Two." Her voice did not rise. "A house that overpays its staff is a house that plans to close. I have buried one house, master cook. I will not bury this one."

"...Two, then."

"And I will see the till," she said, "because you ring it wrong, and it hurts me."

"It's yours."

Lydia nodded once, satisfied, terms concluded. And then she did the thing she had rehearsed in an empty ruin, in the dark, across a night and a day — the words of the old oath, the one her grandmother's grandmother would have given a great house, because some positions deserve the full form and she held one of them now.

She pressed her palm flat over her heart, and bowed.

"I am sworn to carry your burdens."

There was a silence.

"...The classic oath," her employer said at last, in a strange, delighted, strangled voice. "They gave you the classic oath."

"It is the proper form," said Lydia.

"It's perfect," he said. "You're perfect. Welcome aboard."

---

Dawn came, the far-off alarm in its other world, and for the first time he almost resented it.

"I'll retire to the back room," Devan said, apron off, at the door of the little office he never let her see into. "Take your—"

"I know the hours of the house," said the Manager. "Go and rest, master. It will all be here."

It will all be here. He carried that sentence with him out of the dive and into the gray morning, through the helmet swap and the standup and the dashboard weather. A small smooth stone, kept in a pocket, touched all day.

And in the restaurant under the world, the new Manager of Respawn stood alone in the bottled evening light, in the quiet after the door closed, and listened to the house around her. The stove, ticking down. The fire, banked, breathing slow. The spore-snow, ticking soft against the still-glass. Small sounds, deep and whole, every one of them hers to keep.

There was a broom by the cold-room door. A real one. He had made it himself, at some point in four years, and used it badly.

Lydia took it in both hands.

Two hundred years she had swept a floor she could not touch, with nothing but the habit of wanting it clean. Morning and evening. Bristles of pure intention, and the dust never moved.

She swept the floor of her house, and the dust moved, and it was morning.
