The princess's bedchamber had been sealed for a month.

Chancellor Vantry had ordered it locked the week she vanished — out of respect, he said. The official story was known to every servant in the palace by now: the princess, broken by grief and frail of health, had withdrawn to a convent in the west, under the chancellor's protection, and would return when God and her nerves allowed. The convent was never named. Letters could not be forwarded. It was a tidy story, and the palace repeated it in the exact flat voice servants use for stories they do not believe.

On the first night of the thaw moon, inside the sealed room, a door opened in the wall.

Quinn stepped out between the dust sheets of her own furniture and stood still, listening. Boots in the corridor: one guard, walking his round, unhurried. The room smelled shut in. On her writing desk, under the sheet, her books sat where she had left them the night she went out through the laundry court.

A month ago she had fled this palace with bread and cheese and a map.

She had come back with a key to every wall in it.

---

The palace had raised her; that was its mistake.

Lords knew palaces by their halls. Quinn knew hers by its seams — the servants' stairs, the linen passages, the old warming-tunnels that ran behind the state rooms, the panel in the map room that had never latched right in a hundred years. She had been a bored royal child in an enormous building. There was no better training on earth.

She went barefoot, boots slung at her back, through passages Vantry's guards did not watch because Vantry's guards did not know them. Twice she stood behind paneling while footsteps passed. Once she waited a quarter hour in the dark of the linen stair while two maids folded sheets and talked.

Their talk was worth the wait.

The king was worse, they said. He faded and rallied, faded and rallied, but each rally was lower. The new physician — the chancellor's man — allowed no one near but the night nurse. And the cordial went up at every dusk, the amber one, the king's cordial, prepared in the chancellor's own apothecary now, because — and here the older maid's voice went flat as palace stories — *nothing was too much trouble for His Majesty's comfort.*

Quinn stood in the dark and put it together, and the last piece went in without a sound.

Her father was not ill.

Her father had never been ill. Slow, patient, unprovable — an illness that arrived the year the chancellor's man took over the apothecary. She thought of the physicians at Merta's deathbed: *wolfsbane and something else. Something slower.*

Something slower. Something amber, in a warmed cup, every dusk, for three years.

She did not remember the rest of the linen stair. Her body walked it while she stood somewhere behind her own eyes, doing the arithmetic of it — three years of watching her father dim and calling it grief, three years of the cup going up at dusk in front of the whole court. Rage came at last. It was cold, not hot. She filed it under *spring*.

---

The king's chamber, at the third hour of night, held one sleeping nurse and one dying man.

Quinn came out of the old warming-tunnel behind the great bed, where a princess of six had once hidden to jump out at her father, in a palace that had been alive then. She stood at the bedside rail and looked at what three years had made.

He was thin. His hands on the coverlet were an old man's hands, and he was not old. The cup stood on the night table, drained, a film of amber at the bottom.

"Papa," she said, very low.

He woke slowly, the surfacing of a man under heavy water. His eyes found her in the rushlight and wandered, and settled, and for a moment she watched him fail to solve her — and then something cleared, some window blown briefly open, and his hand came up off the coverlet and took hold of her sleeve.

"Quinn," said the king.

Not her mother's name. Hers.

"They told me—" His voice was a ruin. "A convent. I signed something. Quinn. I sign so many things."

"I know, Papa."

"I am so tired," he said, in the sudden simple way of the very sick, "and I cannot think why. I sleep and sleep, and I am so tired."

"I know why," said Quinn. She took the cup off the night table, and folded it into her cloak. "And it is finished now. Do you hear me? It is finished. Drink what comes at dusk, be as you have been, tell no one you saw me. Not the nurse. Not the physician. No one, Papa, whatever they ask, however kind. Can you do that?"

The window was closing; she could see it close. But her father had been a king before he was a patient, and something of the king came up through the tiredness and looked at her, and understood at least the shape of it: secrecy, danger, his daughter alive with dust-sheet lint on her shoulder where a convent should be.

"You have your mother's jaw," he said, "and my nose for rot. Go carefully, girl."

His eyes closed. His hand held her sleeve a moment after the rest of him slept, and then let go.

Quinn stood by the bed until her own hands were steady. Then she went to the night table, and did what she had come to do.

---

She had asked them two nights before, at the counter, and she had made herself ask it plainly, because Hedda's warning stood in her: never lie under this roof.

"The cordial is poisoned. My father drinks it at every dusk. I mean to take it and leave something harmless in its place, so the man who poisons him believes nothing has changed." She had put her hands flat on the counter. "I am telling you because I will use your door to do it, and I will not use this house crookedly. Is it against the rules?"

The cook and the innkeeper had looked at each other.

"You want to swap out a bad drink," said the cook, "for a safe one."

"Yes."

"That's not a weapon," he said. "That's dinner service."

And he had turned to his stove, and begun, then and there, to build her father a cordial. Amber, he said, holding a glass of it to the lamp an hour later — same color, same weight on the tongue, warm spice over honey, nothing in it but things that mend. Bone broth reduced to silk. A tonic his own world would have bottled and sold buildings for.

"He'll sleep better in a week," the cook said, corking the first flask. "Steadier hands in three. If anyone's watching him for decline, they'll be waiting a while."

"They are watching him for decline," Quinn had said quietly.

"Then we'll give them a long winter," said Lydia, and wrapped six flasks in cloth so they would not clink.

Now, in the king's chamber, Quinn poured the palace cordial away into her flask for keeping — evidence, every dusk of it — and set the cook's amber in its place, and wiped the cup, and was back inside the wall before the nurse stirred.

One night. Six flasks in her satchel and a rider coming to the Owl Gate in nine days.

The palace thought its princess was in a convent.

The palace was about to learn it had a ghost.

---

She worked the next ten nights. Mornings, an inn on a dead road: pots, linens, ducks, the quiet of the mountain. Nights, her father's house: the cordial swapped at the third hour, the dosing ledger in the physician's tower copied out page by patient page, the talk of the palace gathered from behind its own walls.

She was careful. She was not, quite, invisible.

A under-cook saw a shape on the linen stair and dropped a tray. The night nurse woke once to a chamber door ajar that she had surely latched. Food went missing from the king's untouched supper plates — the king, the kitchen noticed, had begun cleaning his plates. By the sixth night the young maids were leaving the linen stair unswept after dark, and by the eighth a heel of bread stood on the sill of the map room window, put there by no one who would admit to it.

The palace decided, below stairs, in the old quiet way palaces decide things, that something walked in it at night. And that it was kind. The proof was simple: the king was better.

The king was better. Color at breakfast. Steadier hands. On the ninth day he asked, in front of the whole small court, whether the spring hunt might be planned — and laughed at something, once, short and real, and the sound of it went through the palace staff like weather.

Chancellor Vantry attended the king that afternoon. He was gracious. He expressed joy. He stayed three-quarters of an hour, and watched the king's hands, and said nothing with great skill.

That evening he visited his apothecary, alone, and stood a while looking at the amber row of flasks prepared for the coming week, and found them exactly as they should be, and was not comforted.

Something had changed. He could not name it, count it, or accuse it. For the first time in three years, a column in his ledger would not sum.

He was a patient man. He told himself so twice.

---

On the tenth midnight, at the Owl Gate, a rider waited in the shadow of the arch with two horses and his hood back, as instructed.

A young woman came out of the dark on foot. He had been told not to ask from which direction, and could not have said.

"Cold night," said the rider.

"Turnip," said the princess.

He took the satchel she gave him: nine flasks of amber evidence, a copied ledger in a clean hand, and a letter to the Lady of the March, sealed with a plain button of wax and worth five hundred lances. He was riding before the second bell.

Quinn stood in the gate shadow of her own capital and watched him out of sight. Around her the city slept: her city, her father's city, roofs and smoke and river fog, a patient man asleep somewhere in its finest bed with a ledger that would not sum.

"Spring," she told it softly.

Then she walked into an alley she had known since childhood, and opened a door where no door was, and went home to the inn — and was at the pots by morning, because they would not scrub themselves, and she had a standard to keep.

She scrubbed them to win.
